Agentic coding and persistent returns to expertise (5 minute read)

Anthropic analyzed roughly 400,000 Claude Code sessions from October 2025 to April 2026 to study how people direct agentic coding. Users typically make the planning decisions while Claude handles execution, and the more domain expertise someone brings, the more work Claude does per instruction and the more often the session succeeds. Over the seven months, time spent debugging fell by nearly half, usage shifted toward end-to-end agentic work, and the estimated value of a typical task rose about 25%.

The 100k Whys of AI (1 minute read)

The author argues that AI-generated text is distinguishable from human writing not because LLMs have alien mannerisms, but because they fall back on the same complex set of mannerisms in response to almost any prompt. The evidence is a collage of roughly 150 near-identical Amazon book covers surfaced by searching "100000 whys," many featuring the same roaring dinosaur, rocket, or golden retriever. Because the tools are quasi-deterministic, a hundred authors giving similar prompts get functionally identical output around 80% of the time.

LLMs Are Complicated Now (1 minute read)

The author argues that LLM architectures have grown as messy as the recommendation systems they once contrasted with, citing the jump from Llama 3 to Nemotron 3 Ultra. Attention now comes in grouped, compressed, sparse, linear, and sliding-window variants, Mixture-of-Experts routing has spread well beyond feed-forward layers, and inference spans multiple GPUs. The takeaway: performance has shifted from optimization to necessity, so research iteration needs fused, fast baselines before a new variant can even be evaluated.

Local Qwen isn't a worse Opus, it's a different tool (1 minute read)

The author argues that local Qwen models aren't a cheaper substitute for Opus but a distinct tool with a different cost-benefit profile, drawing on real numbers from running a bootstrapped software business. An RTX 6000 Pro card paid for itself within two or three months on his specific Go and infrastructure workloads, but Qwen still can't be trusted unsupervised. Its worst failure modes, infinite loops and hallucination, surface most once quantized down to fit a consumer GPU.

The data black hole at the center of AI (1 minute read)

The author argues that AI progress has come not from improving sample efficiency but from dramatically widening the data distribution. RL is the main driver, functioning as synthetic data generation that burns compute against a verifier to find correct rollouts, but it still depends on bespoke human expert trajectories for every skill. That dependency is why the data-labeling industry now earns billions a year and is heading toward deca-billions.

Why the Human Genome's Tangled Physicality May Confound AI (5 minute read)

The author argues that the human genome resists the kind of understanding biologists once expected, since barely 2% of its 3 billion bases code for genes and the real difficulty lies in how those genes are regulated. Genomic foundation models like Evo 2, Genos, and DeepMind's AlphaGenome sidestep that complexity by learning correlations from known cases rather than mechanisms. The piece contends this will likely prove useful for prediction while leaving the deeper question of how the genome works inside a computational black box.

The Mythical Agent-Month (5 minute read)

Wes McKinney revisits Fred Brooks's The Mythical Man-Month to ask whether its lessons hold when a single developer orchestrates a swarm of AI coding agents. He argues the human expert still feels essential, bringing rigor and continuous supervision through tools like roborev rather than "prompt and chill" vibe coding. The open question is whether parallel agents escape the old bottlenecks of scope creep, architectural drift, and coordination overhead, or simply reintroduce them at machine speed.

AutoJack Attack Lets One Web Page Hijack AI Agent for Host Code Execution (1 minute read)

Microsoft researchers detailed AutoJack, an exploit chain that turns an AI browsing agent into a vehicle for remote code execution by getting it to load a malicious page whose JavaScript reaches a privileged local service and spawns a host process. The flaw chains three weaknesses in AutoGen Studio's MCP WebSocket route, which trusted localhost, skipped authentication, and ran commands straight from request parameters, and it shipped in two pre-release PyPI builds that remain unyanked.

MosaicLeaks: Can your research agent keep a secret? (5 minute read)

MosaicLeaks is a benchmark that tests whether deep-research agents leak private enterprise data through their external web queries, where an adversary watching only the cumulative query log can reassemble fragments into sensitive facts. The authors found that agents frequently leaked private information, and their privacy-aware RL method, PA-DR, cut answer leakage from 34.0% to 9.9% while raising strict chain success from 48.7% to 58.7%.

Intel and AMD's new ACE CPU extensions bring an efficient AI-oriented instruction set to x86 — a new design makes matrix multiplication more power- and density-efficient (1 minute read)

Intel and AMD released the full specification for ACE, an x86 instruction set extension that adds dedicated matrix-multiplication silicon on top of existing AVX10 registers. AMD ATOM + ATOMesh: Prefill/decode Disaggregation on ROCm (3 minute read)

AMD released ATOM and ATOMesh, a ROCm-native LLM serving stack that disaggregates the prefill and decode phases of inference onto separate pools of GPUs. Prefill is compute-bound and decode is memory-bandwidth-bound, so splitting them lets each pool tune for its own bottleneck rather than letting one long prompt stall everyone's token stream, at the cost of shipping the KV cache across the interconnect.

Anthropic, Microsoft, and Okta are among early adopters.

New framework for auditing machine unlearning (5 minute read)

Google Research introduced Regularized f-Divergence Kernel Tests, a framework presented at AISTATS 2026 for auditing machine unlearning by querying a model and analyzing its output samples. The tests provably control false positives at any sample size and drive false negatives toward zero as samples grow, addressing the loss of statistical power that makes standard two-sample testing computationally expensive on large models.

AI coding agents may be getting bad instructions from ‘smelly’ config files (2 minute read)

Researchers at Brazil's Federal University of Minas Gerais published the first catalog of "configuration smells" for coding agent files like Agents.md and Claude.md, identifying six recurring flaws including lint leakage, context bloat, skill leakage, and conflicting instructions.
