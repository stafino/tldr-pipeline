Understanding the (In)Security of Vibe-Coded Applications (1 minute read)

This paper presents a systematic security study of vibe-coded applications, where users build software primarily through natural-language interactions with AI agents. Analyzing a large corpus of real deployed apps, the authors find recurring vulnerability patterns distinct from conventional development, including placeholder logic, unfiltered input, and exposed secrets. These flaws trace to systematic agent limitations like memory loss and weak security knowledge, and better models or prompting reduce but do not eliminate the underlying risk.

Causal Discovery in the Era of Agents (1 minute read)

This paper argues that LLM agents should assist causal discovery without supplying the causal claims. The authors contend that asking models to infer edge directions, propose graph structures, or inject priors risks passing off textual associations, prompt artifacts, and hallucinated mechanisms as evidence. They propose causal-learn+, a platform where agents handle data inspection, method recommendation, and interpretation while orientations and conclusions stay grounded in formal algorithms and expert decisions, demonstrated on Big Five personality data.

On the Limits of Prompt-Conditioned Language Models as General-Purpose Learners (1 minute read)

This paper argues that LLMs are not universal problem solvers through prompting alone. Modeling user-system interaction as a cheap-talk game, the authors derive PAC-Bayes bounds and identify two irreducible error floors: an expressivity floor, where language's limited channel capacity makes complex tasks indistinguishable, and an objective-misalignment floor, where alignment constraints push the ideal output outside the feasible set. They suggest multimodal inputs and external memory could lift these limits.

AI Exposure Scores: what they measure, what they miss, and what comes next (1 minute read)

This paper revisits the influential "GPTs are GPTs" exposure scores from Eloundou et al. (2023), which define AI exposure as the share of occupational tasks an LLM can assist with. The authors argue these static scores have two widening gaps: a structural one between what they measure and what policy questions require, and a coordination gap where policymakers keep citing the original scores while ignoring methodological updates. They survey five families of newer measures and call for participatory methods, better data infrastructure, and a shift from prediction to preparedness.

Hallucinations in Organization-backed AI advisors: Evidence about Skepticism, Verification, and Reliance in Goal-Directed Use (1 minute read)

This paper reviews empirical evidence on how users handle hallucinations from organization-backed AI advisors, arguing that studies routinely conflate four distinct things: whether users are skeptical, whether they verify, whether verification succeeds, and whether it changes reliance. Across product search, medical decisions, and chatbot tasks, nearly all work measures reliance but rarely measures skepticism or verification directly. The most deployable intervention for organizations, generic hallucination warnings, shows the weakest and most mixed effects.

SPIRAL: Learning to Search and Aggregate (1 minute read)

SPIRAL is a reinforcement learning framework that trains language models to use sequential, parallel, and aggregative reasoning as a unified inference pipeline, sampling independent chain-of-thought traces in parallel and conditioning a final aggregation trace on them. Optimized end-to-end against the aggregated response reward, it outperforms GRPO by up to 11x in scaling efficiency and 15% in performance when all three compute primitives are scaled.

Evaluation Awareness Is Not One Capability: Evidence from Open Language Models (1 minute read)

This paper argues that evaluation awareness in language models is multivariate rather than a single capability, testing 37 open-weight models across seven families in eight experiments. The authors find that detectability, behavioral shifts, and controllability vary independently, with hard refusals dropping 5.8 points under hypothetical framing and probes retaining 0.98 AUROC even after behavior collapses below chance.

CoorDex: Coordinating Body and Hand Priors for Continuous Dexterous Humanoid Loco-Manipulation (1 minute read)

CoorDex is a learning pipeline that enables humanoids to perform dexterous manipulation while walking, replacing the usual stop-and-go approach of pausing to operate a low-DoF gripper. It distills whole-body and hand motion-tracking teachers into frozen latent priors, then runs residual reinforcement learning over them, letting a Unitree G1 with a 20-DoF hand grasp bottles, open fridge doors, and pick-and-turn cubes on the move.

Kamera: Unified Position-Invariant Multimodal KV Cache for Training-Free Reuse (1 minute read)

Kamera is a training-free method that lets multimodal agents reuse KV cache for video frames, screenshots, and other artifacts at any position, rather than re-encoding them from scratch every time the context window slides. It pairs exact RoPE re-rotation with a small low-rank conditioning patch that restores the cross-chunk binding naive reuse drops, recovering full accuracy on multi-hop benchmarks across MLA, GQA, and MHA at a fraction of the KV footprint.

Tmax: A simple recipe for terminal agents (1 minute read)

Tmax is an open reinforcement-learning recipe for terminal-using agents that scores 27% on Terminal-Bench 2.0 with just 9B parameters, outperforming much larger models from prior work. The authors generate training environments using a taxonomy that combines difficulty control, personas, and verifier diversification, and they open-source the resulting dataset, which is over 2.5x larger than previously released terminal-agent datasets, alongside their models and code.

Habituation at the Gate: Rising Approval and Declining Scrutiny in Human Review of AI Agent Code (1 minute read)

This paper studies whether human reviewers lower their scrutiny of AI-generated pull requests over time, analyzing 11,429 reviews from 400 repeat reviewers across a seven-month period using the AIDev dataset. Approval rates rose from 30.1% to 36.8% as reviewers gained experience, while inline comment volume fell 22% and review latency grew 3.5x, a pattern the authors read as reflexive habituation under workload rather than calibrated trust.

Exposing the Illusion of Erasure in Knowledge Editing for LLMs (1 minute read)

This paper examines knowledge editing for LLMs from an adversarial elicitation angle and finds that "erased" facts are often not removed but redistributed within the model's representation space. Through mechanistic analysis, the authors show low-rank edits act as targeted suppression rather than deletion, leaving knowledge in narrow, perturbation-sensitive regions that indirect prompting and adversarial attacks can recover, making KE methods inherently bypassable.
