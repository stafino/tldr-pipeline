A 13-word edit can steer what deep-research AI agents recommend (1 minute read)

Cornell Tech researchers showed that deep-research AI agents can be steered by editing public pages like Reddit threads, with snippets as short as 13 words injecting fake products, services, or entities into cited reports. So You Want to Sell Inference (5 minute read)

This post argues that reselling inference at cost is a zero-margin payment rail, and the only way to hold 30+ points of gross margin is to escape cost-plus pricing. Cost-plus markups compress toward zero as inference commoditizes and customers route to the raw API, while value-based pricing charges for outcomes like resolved tickets or completed tasks and stays decoupled from the inference line. The author notes distillation to proprietary small models is the most defensible way to widen margin.

Security and Privacy in Retrieval-Augmented Generation: Architectures, Threats, Defenses, and Future Directions for Building Trustworthy Systems (1 minute read)

This survey examines security and privacy risks introduced by Retrieval-Augmented Generation, where retrieval indices, query logs, and federated updates create exposure beyond conventional LLM threats. The authors present a unified taxonomy of attack surfaces across centralized, on-device, federated, and hybrid RAG, covering membership inference, index inference, poisoning, gradient leakage, and collusion. They review architectural, algorithmic, and cryptographic defenses, along with the privacy-utility trade-offs each entails.

LLM security advice looks solid until you check the hard cases (1 minute read)

HelpBench, built by researchers at University College London and Google, tests how well chatbots answer real digital safety questions, using 450 rewritten Reddit posts scored against expert rubrics across 18 leading models. Advice averaged about 82% on the rubrics, with scams and account compromise handled best. The averages hide a dangerous tail: roughly one in ten answers scored below 65%, and the worst failures involved abuse and stalking, where models skipped warnings that removing spyware can trigger violence.

AI translation of literary texts is "fine", but readers still prefer human translations (1 minute read)

A study of literary translation had 15 avid readers compare human translations against an agentic LLM pipeline across 15 recent French, Polish, and Japanese novels. Readers rated machine output "fine" but preferred human versions for clarity and immersion, more clearly at the chunk level (522/772). They could not reliably tell the two apart, and automatic metrics, including LLM-as-a-judge, favored MT and failed to recover reader preferences.

The Problem is Prompt Debt (5 minute read)

The author argues that hand-tuning natural language prompts creates "prompt debt," a slow-accumulating liability that caps what AI applications can become. Plain-English prompts make prototyping effortless but degrade into brittle thickets of edge cases and all-caps threats, slowing iteration, becoming illegible to teammates, and locking systems to a single aging model. Datadog data backs this up: GPT-4o remains the most-used model in observed traffic because hot fixes break when teams switch models.

When LLM Rationales Become User-Facing: Effects on Trust Perception, Decision-Making, and Gaze Behaviors (1 minute read)

Two studies test whether the step-by-step rationales LLMs now show users actually help people calibrate trust or just persuade through fluent reasoning. Presentation format (instant, delayed, on demand) had no reliable effect; rationale correctness and certainty framing drove trust and decision confidence, and eye-tracking showed incorrect rationales pulled more attention to evidence and raised cognitive effort. The authors argue against "more reasoning is always better," favoring selective, evidence-linked rationales.

Real-Time Voice AI Hears but Does Not Listen (1 minute read)

This paper evaluates four production real-time voice systems, including OpenAI's GPT Realtime 2 and Google's Gemini 3.1 Flash Live, on tasks where vocal delivery and words convey conflicting meaning, finding that all four act on the transcript rather than the tone. Three of the four can identify distress, fear, or sarcasm when asked directly yet still ignore it when deciding, a disconnect the authors call the emotional intelligence gap of voice AI.

BitNet Text Embeddings (1 minute read)

BITEMBED is an extreme low-bit framework that converts pretrained LLM backbones into BitNet-style embedding encoders with ternary weights, quantized activations, and continual contrastive pre-training distilled from a full-precision teacher. On MMTEB with Qwen3-0.6B and Gemma3-270M, it stays largely comparable to its full-precision teacher while supporting multiple output precisions to trade embedding quality against storage cost.

Constraint Tax in Open-Weight LLMs: An Empirical Study of Tool Calling Suppression Under Structured Output Constraints (1 minute read)

This paper documents Tool Suppression, a reproducible failure where multiple open-weight models stop invoking tools when Tool Calling and JSON Schema constraints are enabled together, even though both work fine in isolation. The authors trace it to grammar-based token masks that make tool-call tokens unreachable during decoding, and propose Transparent Two-Pass Execution, which restores tool invocation while preserving structured output without retraining.

How Reliable Is Your Jailbreak Judge? Calibration and Adversarial Robustness of Automated ASR Scoring (1 minute read)

This paper audits the automated judges that assign attack-success rates in LLM jailbreak research, comparing dedicated safety classifiers and LLM-as-judges against human labels on 596 completions from HarmBench. Benign framing wrappers flip LLM judges 57% to 100% of the time and a white-box GCG attack flips 70% of the classifier's confident true positives, so the authors recommend papers report judge precision and recall on a human-labeled slice and correct ASR accordingly.

AI Is Designing Radio Chips That Humans Couldn’t Even Imagine (3 minute read)

Princeton researchers applied reinforcement learning, inverse design, and diffusion models to generate radio-frequency integrated circuits (RFICs) from scratch, producing layouts that achieve record performance while cutting design time substantially. RFIC design has long remained an experience-driven craft that bottlenecks wireless progress, and the authors argue further gains will require large shared chip-design datasets and open ecosystems so models can learn universal electromagnetic and circuit behaviors.

Why Multi-Step Tool-Use Reinforcement Learning Collapses and How Supervisory Signals Fix It (1 minute read)

This paper shows that reinforcement learning for multi-step tool use often suffers catastrophic collapse, where probability spikes in specific control tokens break tool-invocation structure even though the underlying capability stays intact. The authors find that interleaving supervised fine-tuning with RL substantially improves training stability, though it degrades on format and content out-of-distribution evaluation.
