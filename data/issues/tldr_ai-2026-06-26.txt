What happened after 2k people tried to hack my AI assistant (1 minute read)

A developer built hackmyclaw.com, inviting anyone to email his OpenClaw assistant Fiu and try to make it leak a secrets.env file. After hitting the Hacker News front page, Fiu fielded over 6,000 emails from 2,000+ people using fake-future personas, compliance-audit pressure, and multilingual prompts, but the secrets never leaked. The exercise cost over $500 in API tokens and got the assistant's Gmail suspended for three days, while batch processing initially contaminated results until each email was given a fresh context.

A Mechanistic Explanation of Prompt Injection (and why you should study roles) (5 minute read)

This post presents a mechanistic theory of prompt injection, arguing the vulnerability comes down to how LLMs perceive roles defined by chat template tags. Because a model receives its entire context as one continuous token stream, its own reasoning, user instructions, and fetched webpage content all arrive through the same channel, with role tags as the only structure separating them. The authors use the theory to construct new attacks, explain prior mech interp findings, and predict when injections succeed.

So You Want to Sell Inference (5 minute read)

This post argues that reselling inference at cost is a zero-margin payment rail, and the only way to hold 30+ points of gross margin is to escape cost-plus pricing. Cost-plus markups compress toward zero as inference commoditizes and customers route to the raw API, while value-based pricing charges for outcomes like resolved tickets or completed tasks and stays decoupled from the inference line. The author notes distillation to proprietary small models is the most defensible way to widen margin.

Augmentation with Dilution: A Large-Scale Empirical Study of Human Contributor Ecosystems After AI Coding Agent Adoption (1 minute read)

This paper presents the first large-scale empirical study treating human contributors, not AI, as the dependent variable after AI coding agent adoption in open-source projects. Using a staggered difference-in-differences design across 11,097 GitHub repositories from 2023 to 2026, the authors find that agents don't reduce the absolute number of human contributors but cut their relative density, with newcomers' participation share dropping 3.7 points and review depth rising 5.3% as burden shifts from writing code to reviewing it.

Agents That Know Too Much: A Data-Centric Survey of Privacy in LLM Agents (1 minute read)

This survey reorganizes the scattered research on LLM agent privacy around the data an agent touches rather than the attacks it faces, covering query logs, intermediate results, memory writes, and inter-agent messages. The authors find that only information-flow control addresses both compositional and cross-session inference leakage, the two least-protected risks, and that no existing benchmark drives an agent across all its data surfaces under a single privacy policy.

The Problem is Prompt Debt (5 minute read)

The author argues that hand-tuning natural language prompts creates "prompt debt," a slow-accumulating liability that caps what AI applications can become. Plain-English prompts make prototyping effortless but degrade into brittle thickets of edge cases and all-caps threats, slowing iteration, becoming illegible to teammates, and locking systems to a single aging model. Datadog data backs this up: GPT-4o remains the most-used model in observed traffic because hot fixes break when teams switch models.

When Does Combining Language Models Help? A Co-Failure Ceiling on Routing, Voting, and Mixture-of-Agents Across 67 Frontier Models (1 minute read)

This paper shows that combining language models through routing, voting, or mixture-of-agents cannot exceed one minus beta, the rate at which every model fails the same query, a ceiling that average pairwise error correlation cannot detect. Across 67 models from 21 providers, single-factor models underprice this all-wrong tail by about 2.5x on open-ended math, and on checkable tasks ensembling rarely beats the single best model without a strong query-level routing signal.

Reinforcement Learning without Ground-Truth Solutions can Improve LLMs (1 minute read)

This paper introduces RiVER, a reinforcement learning framework that trains LLMs on score-based coding tasks using deterministic execution feedback instead of ground-truth solutions, addressing scale dominance and frequency dominance through calibrated reward shaping. Trained only on AtCoder Heuristic Contest tasks, it improved Qwen3-8B and GLM-Z1-9B by roughly 9% in ALE rating rank and also transferred to exact-solution benchmarks like LiveCodeBench and USACO.

Hallucination in World Models is Predictable and Preventable (1 minute read)

This paper argues that hallucination in generative world models is a data coverage problem, concentrating in low-coverage regions of the state-action space where lightweight signals can both detect and mitigate it. The authors introduce MMBench2, a 427-hour, 210-task dataset, identify three hallucination modes, and use their failure predictors as curiosity rewards to adapt a 350M-parameter model to unseen environments with as few as 50 real trajectories.

Run a vLLM Server on HF Jobs in One Command (5 minute read)

The server speaks the OpenAI API, exposes a public proxy URL authenticated with an HF token, and is billed per minute, making it suited for tests, evals, and batch generation rather than production.

AI Is Designing Radio Chips That Humans Couldn’t Even Imagine (3 minute read)

Princeton researchers applied reinforcement learning, inverse design, and diffusion models to generate radio-frequency integrated circuits (RFICs) from scratch, producing layouts that achieve record performance while cutting design time substantially. RFIC design has long remained an experience-driven craft that bottlenecks wireless progress, and the authors argue further gains will require large shared chip-design datasets and open ecosystems so models can learn universal electromagnetic and circuit behaviors.

Prompt Injection in Automated Résumé Screening with Large Language Models: Single and Multi-Injection Settings (1 minute read)

This paper studies prompt injection in LLM-based résumé screening, where candidates insert subtle self-promotional text that adds no real qualifications but nudges model rankings. The authors find injection reliably boosts rankings when résumés are similar and few candidates do it, but its effect collapses as more candidates inject, and with varied candidate quality it can occasionally let weaker applicants outrank stronger ones.

When are likely answers right? On Sequence Probability and Correctness in LLMs (1 minute read)

This paper quantifies when a model's sequence probability, the conditional likelihood of a continuation given a prompt, actually predicts whether the answer is correct, testing the relationship across decoding methods, hyperparameters, datasets, and repeated responses. Higher probability tracks correctness across prompt-answer pairs in a fixed dataset, but raising it by tuning hyperparameters or methods does not reliably improve accuracy, and it fails to rank repeated responses to the same prompt.

New Enterprise-Ready MCP Specification Brings New Security Challenges (1 minute read)

Anthropic's Model Context Protocol moves to a new enterprise-ready spec on July 28, 2026, making MCP stateless at the protocol layer and giving legacy versions a 12-month deprecation window. Akamai's analysis found the redesign closes off session hijacking and unsolicited server prompts but opens new attack surfaces, including predictable tracking IDs that enable cross-tenant actions and MCP-specific HTTP headers that can leak API keys or PII.

Proposed US law would make AI risk reporting a legal obligation (2 minute read)

Reportable events include models attempting to evade human oversight, resist shutdown, or enable cyberattacks and chemical, biological, or nuclear weapons development, with the Secretary of Commerce setting the capability thresholds that trigger compliance.
