The gap between open weights LLMs and closed source LLMs (1 minute read)

A single benchmark, the Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index, shows the gap between open-weights and closed-source LLMs shrinking to zero around December 3rd, 2026. The author re-ran the analysis across all 18 of Artificial Analysis's benchmarks and found the average gap holds nearly flat at just under five months for the entire period. Most of the headline convergence comes from coding, which closed from 15 months behind to one or two, while other benchmarks barely moved.

Agents That Know Too Much: A Data-Centric Survey of Privacy in LLM Agents (1 minute read)

This survey reorganizes the scattered literature on LLM agent privacy around the data an agent touches rather than by attack type, covering databases, retrieved documents, persistent memory, API calls, and inter-agent messages. The authors argue sensitive data leaks not just through final answers but through queries, intermediate results, and memory writes. Two gaps recur: only information-flow control addresses compositional and cross-session inference leakage, and no benchmark drives an agent across all its data surfaces under a single privacy policy.

DSpark: Speculative decoding accelerates LLM inference [pdf] (1 minute read)

DSpark is a speculative decoding system from DeepSeek that accelerates LLM inference by drafting candidate tokens and verifying them in parallel against the target model. The paper details how the approach reduces latency without altering output quality, building on DeepSeek's DeepSpec project to push faster generation on existing model weights.

Accelerating Gemini Nano models on Pixel with frozen Multi-Token Prediction (1 minute read)

Google introduced a method that retrofits Multi-Token Prediction onto frozen, production Gemini Nano v3 models to speed up on-device inference on Pixel phones without running separate drafter models. Building on the EAGLE and CALM frameworks, the approach has rolled out to the Pixel 9 and 10 series as an out-of-the-box speedup for features like AI Notification Summaries and Proofread, cutting latency and energy use.

When Does Combining Language Models Help? A Co-Failure Ceiling on Routing, Voting, and Mixture-of-Agents Across 67 Frontier Models (1 minute read)

This paper shows that combining language models through routing, voting, or mixture-of-agents cannot exceed accuracy of one minus beta, the rate at which every model fails the same query, a ceiling the field rarely measures. Across 67 models from 21 providers, single-factor models underprice this all-wrong tail by roughly 2.5x, and on checkable tasks ensembles rarely beat the single best model without a strong query-level routing signal.

Prompt Injection in Automated Résumé Screening with Large Language Models: Single and Multi-Injection Settings (1 minute read)

This paper studies prompt injection in LLM-based résumé screening, defined as self-promotional text that adds no real qualifications but is designed to sway model rankings. Controlled experiments show injection reliably boosts rankings when candidate quality is uniform and few applicants inject, but its effect collapses as the tactic spreads and can occasionally let lower-quality candidates outrank stronger ones.

Hierarchical Muon: Tiled Newton-Schulz Updates for Efficient Muon Optimization (1 minute read)

This paper introduces Hierarchical Muon (HiMuon), a tiled Newton-Schulz scheme that partitions each momentum-gradient matrix into T×T tiles, applies the same finite map independently to each, and reassembles the results. The approach cuts the leading optimizer work from O(r²sK) to O(HWTK) and decomposes into independent small dense operations, enabling tile-size GPU kernels and cross-layer batching while keeping transformer training behavior close to full-matrix Muon.

How Much Static Structure Do Code Agents Need? A Study of Deterministic Anchoring (1 minute read)

This paper studies whether injecting lightweight static analysis, such as call graphs and inheritance topology, as plain-text anchors makes LLM code agents navigate repositories more deterministically. Building on OpenAI's Codex, the authors find that anchoring improves function-level localization by 2.2pp, shortens trajectories by 1.6 rounds, and roughly halves run-to-run variance at the cost of about 10% more input tokens.

Learning to Fold: prizewinning solution at LeHome Challenge 2026 (1st place online, 2nd offline) (1 minute read)

A solution to the LeHome Challenge 2026, an ICRA bimanual garment-folding competition, placed 1st of 62 teams in the simulation round and 2nd in the real-world final by adding a reinforcement-learning loop to a vision-language-action policy. The same network predicts actions, success, progress, and task-relevant future quantities, and those predictions drive advantage estimation, live failure detection, and candidate selection.

Reinforcement Learning without Ground-Truth Solutions can Improve LLMs (1 minute read)

This paper introduces RiVER, a reinforcement learning framework that trains LLMs on score-based coding tasks using deterministic execution feedback instead of ground-truth solutions, with calibrated reward shaping to counter scale and frequency dominance. Trained only on AtCoder Heuristic Contest tasks, it improved Qwen3-8B and GLM-Z1-9B by 8.9% and 9.4% in ALE rating rank and transferred to exact-solution benchmarks like LiveCodeBench and USACO.

Jailbreaking for the Average Jane: Choosing Optimal Jailbreaks via Bandit Algorithms for Automatically Enhanced Queries (1 minute read)

This paper introduces a multi-armed bandit attack that efficiently learns the optimal jailbreak for a target LLM from a large choice set using a small number of noisy exploration queries. Tested with FrankensteinBench, a benchmark of 11,279 malicious queries, the method reached success rates as high as 97% on average across 15 state-of-the-art open-weight models, and adding query complexity raised success rates by up to 26%.

Hallucination in World Models is Predictable and Preventable (1 minute read)

This paper argues that hallucination in generative world models is fundamentally a data coverage problem, concentrated in low-coverage regions of the state-action space where lightweight signals can both detect and mitigate it. The authors release MMBench2, a 427-hour, 210-task dataset, identify three hallucination modes, and use their predictors as curiosity rewards to adapt a 350M-parameter model to unseen environments with as few as 50 real trajectories.

Paved with True Intents: Intent-Aware Training Improves LLM Safety Classification Across Training Regimes (1 minute read)

This paper argues that safety classifiers should model user intent as an explicit signal between a prompt and its harm label, and introduces AIMS, a human-annotated dataset of 1,724 difficult prompts each paired with an intent description. Across supervised fine-tuning, preference learning, distillation, and reinforcement learning, rewarding intent faithfulness with GRPO yields the strongest average performance on five external safety benchmarks while forming the latency-F1 Pareto frontier.

Einstein World Models (1 minute read)

This paper proposes Einstein World Models, a blueprint that places visual-temporal rollouts inside an LLM's reasoning trace so it can run visual thought experiments rather than reasoning through text alone. The LLM calls a separate world-module to generate short scene rollouts, treating each one not as the answer but as an inspectable hypothesis that supports later reasoning, extending tool-calling into visual simulation.

A Deterministic Control Plane for LLM Coding Agents (1 minute read)

A study of 10,008 public GitHub repositories found that LLM agent configuration files behave as an unmanaged supply chain, with 10.1% of tracked paths being exact duplicates across independent repos and fewer than 1% declaring permission boundaries. The paper proposes Rel(AI)Build, a deterministic control plane that treats agent definitions as content-addressed managed components, enforces tiered permissions before LLM invocation, and detects prompt drift via Jaccard similarity.

MIRROR: Novelty-Constrained Memory-Guided MCTS Red-Teaming for Agentic RAG (1 minute read)

MIRROR is a red-teaming framework for multimodal agentic RAG systems that runs memory-guided Monte Carlo tree search, conditioning attack generation on retrieved context while a deterministic Novelty Gate rejects any candidate that duplicates the retrieval set. Across four attack surfaces it reaches 76% attack success on image poisoning versus 52% for baselines and 97% on orchestrator attacks at half the query cost, and the authors release ART-SafeBench with over 41,000 records.

CARVE: Content-Aware Recurrent with Value Efficiency for Chunk-Parallel Linear Attention (1 minute read)

CARVE is a linear-attention architecture that fixes three coupled flaws in the leading delta-rule model (GDN-2) by erasing memory only on the key axis, which the authors prove is necessary and sufficient to keep the WY-form chunk solver valid. At 1.3B parameters trained on 100B tokens, it cuts WikiText perplexity by 0.18 and leads on common-sense and RULER retrieval benchmarks at 0.4% throughput overhead, 13% lower peak memory, and 19% fewer parameters.

Safe Autoregressive Image Generation with Iterative Self-Improving Codebooks (1 minute read)

This paper proposes iterative self-improving codebooks, a method that hardens autoregressive image generators against unsafe outputs without human annotation. The model uses its own understanding to flag harmful generations, builds harmful and safe image-text pairs to update the codebook's quantized mappings, then fine-tunes on safe pairs to preserve quality, repeating until safety no longer improves.

When are likely answers right? On Sequence Probability and Correctness in LLMs (1 minute read)

This paper measures when sequence probability, the conditional likelihood of a continuation given a prompt, actually tracks correctness in LLMs, testing across decoding methods, hyperparameters, datasets, and repeated responses. Higher probability predicts correctness across prompt-answer pairs within a fixed dataset, but tuning hyperparameters or methods to raise probability does not reliably improve accuracy, clarifying the limits of decoding, self-consistency, and verifier-free self-improvement.
