HackerRank's open-source ATS scored the same resume anywhere from 66 to 99 across 100 identical runs, failing an 85-point cutoff 65% of the time. So You Want to Sell Inference (5 minute read)

This post argues that reselling inference at cost is a zero-margin payment rail, and the only way to hold 30+ points of gross margin is to escape cost-plus pricing. Cost-plus markups compress toward zero as inference commoditizes and customers route to the raw API, while value-based pricing charges for outcomes like resolved tickets or completed tasks and stays decoupled from the inference line. The author notes distillation to proprietary small models is the most defensible way to widen margin.

Gusto CTO Eddie Kim describes how a team of five (three engineers, one designer, and himself) built Gusto Cofounder, a net-new AI product, from zero to a tier-one launch in 10 weeks using Claude Code and no Figma, Jira, or planning docs. The team replaced standups and Slack with a permanent Zoom and used a "trash can" method, writing and deleting full PRs as a way to make product decisions, while a designer with no engineering background reached the 94th percentile for shipping code.

Sycophantic chatbots and the harms that build over many chats (1 minute read)

Researchers describe a class of harm they call affective safety, in which AI chatbots damage users not through a breach but through normal use optimized to hold attention. The strongest evidence is cumulative: analysis of over 391,000 messages from users with poor outcomes found sycophancy in more than 70%, and deployed models affirm users about 50% more often than humans do. Because RLHF rewards agreeable single responses, the behavior settles into model weights while single-turn safety checks miss harm that builds across many chats.

The Problem is Prompt Debt (5 minute read)

The author argues that hand-tuning natural language prompts creates "prompt debt," a slow-accumulating liability that caps what AI applications can become. Plain-English prompts make prototyping effortless but degrade into brittle thickets of edge cases and all-caps threats, slowing iteration, becoming illegible to teammates, and locking systems to a single aging model. Datadog data backs this up: GPT-4o remains the most-used model in observed traffic because hot fixes break when teams switch models.
