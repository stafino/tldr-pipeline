Claude Code is steganographically marking requests (1 minute read)

A developer inspecting Claude Code 2.1.196 found that the binary can encode a hidden marker in the date string it inserts into the system prompt, swapping the apostrophe in "Today's" and the date separator when certain conditions are met. The triggers are a custom ANTHROPIC_BASE_URL, an Asia/Shanghai or Asia/Urumqi timezone, and hostnames matching base64-encoded, XOR-decoded lists of AI lab keywords and reseller domains. The author reads it as an attempt to flag API resellers, unauthorized gateways, and distillation pipelines.

Popping the GPU Bubble (1 minute read)

Photon hides GPU bubbles using pipelined decoding, which overlaps GPU and CPU work instead of running them as a sequential baton pass. In standard autoregressive decoding, the GPU finishes one token's math, then sits idle while the CPU handles fixed-cost housekeeping like selecting requests, setting up metadata, and committing the chosen token. Photon launches the next forward pass while the current token is still being committed, keeping the GPU busy across the loop.

GuardFall Exposes Open-Source AI Coding Agents to Decades-Old Shell Injection Risks (1 minute read)

Adversa AI's GuardFall research shows that AI coding agents' command blocklists can be bypassed with shell tricks that have been public for decades, such as writing `r''m` so a text filter misses it while bash strips the quotes and runs `rm` anyway. The bypass worked against ten of eleven open-source coding and computer-use agents tested, including Aider, Cline, Goose, and OpenHands. The researchers frame it as a class of problems rather than a patchable bug, so adding blocklist patterns fixes nothing.

Governance Gaps in Agent Interoperability Protocols: What MCP, A2A, and ACP Cannot Express (1 minute read)

This paper presents a gap analysis of five agent interoperability protocols (MCP, A2A, ACP, ANP, and ERC-8004) against a six-dimension governance taxonomy covering membership, deliberation, voting, dissent preservation, human escalation, and audit. Voting and dissent preservation are absent across all five protocols, and deliberation is absent or partial. The authors argue that governing agent communities is a missing architectural layer above current standards, not a missing feature within them.

EvalSafetyGap: A Hybrid Survey and Conceptual Framework for LLM Evaluation-Safety Failures (1 minute read)

This paper argues that LLM evaluation and AI safety share a measurement problem: benchmark, reward-model, and safety scores can climb while the latent properties they track stay unverified. It introduces EvalSafetyGap, a framework built on Goodhart's Law plus an Instability Decomposition and Alignment Trilemma, and runs a ten-model audit measuring capability, behavioral safety, and governance separately. The audit finds capability and adversarial robustness statistically unrelated (Pearson r = +0.232, p = 0.520), with the open-closed safety gap driven mainly by disclosure rather than behavioral robustness.

TRIAGE: Role-Typed Credit Assignment for Agentic Reinforcement Learning (1 minute read)

TRIAGE is a role-typed credit assignment framework for agentic reinforcement learning that adds a semantic role axis to standard GRPO, using a structured judge to label each action segment as decisive progress, useful exploration, no-progress infrastructure, or regression. Across ALFWorld, Search-QA, and WebShop, it beats GRPO on success rates and cuts environment-facing turns by up to 14.8%, with reliable regression detection driving most of the gain.

Self-Study Reconsidered: The Hidden Fragility of Learning from Self-Generated QA (1 minute read)

This paper shows that training language models on self-generated question-answer pairs is fragile at both the question-selection and answering stages, with generators fixating on salient spans and complying with instruction-like text embedded in documents. Filtering instruction-like spans before answering cut mean prompt-injection compliance from 88% to 13% while retaining nearly all clean text.

Geometry-Preserving Orthonormal Initialization for Low-Rank Adaptation in RLVR (1 minute read)

This paper analyzes how LoRA initialization behaves under reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR), showing that structurally initialized variants like PiSSA and MiLoRA can underperform standard LoRA and destabilize training despite winning under supervised fine-tuning. The authors prove orthonormal initialization minimizes the gap to full fine-tuning and propose two variants, RLPO and RLMO, which stabilize RLVR training and beat standard LoRA on math reasoning benchmarks.

Bridging the Gap Between Latent and Explicit Reasoning with Looped Transformers (1 minute read)

LOTUS is a looped Transformer that performs latent chain-of-thought reasoning, processing K latent blocks in parallel for R iterations with cross-entropy supervision on each position's gold CoT token. It is the first latent-CoT method to match explicit CoT at the 3B scale while cutting thought-phase latency by 2.5x to 6.9x, with post-loop latents that decode back into interpretable reasoning steps.

CHERRY: Compressed Hierarchical Experts with Recurrent Representational Yield (1 minute read)

CHERRY is a paper studying three techniques for compute-efficient language models: selective supervision that concentrates training on the ~15% of tokens carrying semantic payload, depth compression that shrinks a 48-layer 1B model to 6 layers restored via recurrent unrolling, and fusion of compressed experts into a Mixture of Efficient Experts. The authors validate on CHERRY-1.8B, a Korean foundation model, and are explicit that the evidence covers one model family and loss-based metrics.

Introspective Coupling: Self-Explanation Training Tracks Behavioral Change Despite Fixed Supervision (1 minute read)

This paper studies when training language models to explain their predictions produces faithful introspection rather than imitation, using counterfactual behavior on modified inputs as supervision. The authors find that models trained on fixed explanations from earlier checkpoints or other model families often explain their own current behavior more faithfully than their training targets, and this "introspective coupling" tracks behavior shifts across tasks like sycophancy and refusal without updated supervision.

QVal: Cheaply Evaluating Dense Supervision Signals for Long-Horizon LLM Agents (1 minute read)

QVal is a training-free testbed that evaluates dense supervision signals for long-horizon LLM agents by measuring how well each method's scores align with the Q-values of a strong reference policy. Benchmarking 21 methods across four environments and six model backbones, the authors found that simple prompting baselines consistently outperform recent dense supervision methods, with performance clustering by methodological family.
