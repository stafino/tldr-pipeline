AI Writes Faster Than Humans Can Review: A Longitudinal Study of an Enterprise 2x Mandate (1 minute read)

A longitudinal study tracked a mid-sized company's "2x" mandate to double merged pull requests per engineer, analyzing 802 developers and 196,212 PRs from January 2024 to April 2026. Per-capita throughput hit 2.09x the pre-mandate baseline, with a difference-in-differences design linking most of the gain to AI adoption and accumulated use rather than the mandate itself. Review restructured around automation: per-reviewer load roughly doubled and automated review overtook human review, while merge and revert rates held steady.

Adoption and Impact of Command-Line AI Coding Agents: A Study of Microsoft's Early 2026 Rollout of Claude Code and GitHub Copilot CLI (1 minute read)

A study of Microsoft's early-2026 rollout of Claude Code and GitHub Copilot CLI across tens of thousands of engineers found that adopters merged roughly 24% more pull requests than they otherwise would have, with the lift persisting across a four-month window. First use spread primarily through social networks, and retention tracked engineers' coding activity more than demographics. The authors argue organizations should treat visible peer use as central to CLI agent rollout strategy.

Decoupling Code Complexity from Newcomer Participation: A Causal Study of AI Coding Agent Adoption in OSS (1 minute read)

A causal study of 1,888 open-source projects finds that adopting AI coding agents like Cursor and Claude Code does not crowd out newcomers. Using difference-in-differences against matched controls, the authors show newcomer inflow, onboarding, and retention are unchanged after adoption. Agents do raise per-function complexity, roughly +11% on a cognitive metric for Python, but newcomer participation holds steady even in the subsets where complexity rose.

Vercel's Andrew Qu on why agents are a new kind of software (1 minute read)

Vercel Chief of Software Andrew Qu argues that agents are a distinct kind of software, not just an application layer built on top of models. In this Latent Space interview, he explains how Vercel built its agent framework eve out of primitives it accumulated while shipping v0, solving problems like switching providers, adding fallbacks, and making runs resumable. Qu contends skills, sandboxes, and agent-readable websites now matter as much as the models themselves.

Chain-of-Thought Spoofing Targets Reasoning AI Models (1 minute read)

Researchers Charles Ye, Jasmine Cui, and Dylan Hadfield-Menell demonstrated CoT Forgery, a prompt-injection attack that exploits how reasoning models prioritize writing style over role metadata tags. Because LLMs treat text matching their distinctive internal `<think>` style as trusted reasoning, an attacker can inject convoluted fake reasoning and have the model act on it as an already-reached conclusion. The authors argue role perception isn't binary, so prompt injection will stay an evolving arms race rather than a solved problem.

The short leash AI coding method for beating Fable (1 minute read)

The author argues that expert developers should keep AI agents on a "short leash" rather than delegating to swarms of parallel agents managed by an orchestrator. The post contends that "vibe" workflows make it impossible to build real understanding of a codebase, since agents drift off the rails and produce working but inefficient, ugly code that only surfaces later. It distills a year of using AI on security-critical software as a maintainer.

The fall of the theorem economy (1 minute read)

The author argues that mathematics' real product is clarity and understanding, not theorems, so AI that automates proof-writing threatens only the least valuable part of the work. Drawing on his own unpublished results, he contends the hard part was always conjecturing and building the conceptual framework, while writing up the proof was mechanical drudgery. His conclusion is that AI could collapse the "theorem economy" of formal publication while leaving the actual intellectual core of math largely intact.

The website of the future may assemble itself for every visitor (1 minute read)

Adobe is experimenting with "agentic sites" that assemble a personalized page around each visitor's inferred intent in real time. Principal Scientist Carlos Sanchez demonstrated a system that reads browsing and search signals, sorts them into an intent category like exploring or preparing to purchase, and uses an LLM to compose the page. Rather than inventing content, it retrieves from the site's existing material as a grounding corpus, moving personalization past predefined segments toward what Adobe calls an "audience of one.

Human Capital, Not Model Benchmarks, Predicts Hybrid Intelligence in Forecasting (1 minute read)

This paper uses Polymarket as an externally resolved benchmark to show that the value of human-AI collaboration in forecasting depends on measurable human capital, not model benchmarks. Hybrid performance was trimodal: most people either deferred to the model or used it to rubber-stamp a prior guess, while a minority engaged in genuine complementary reasoning and matched or beat the market. Collaborative traits like perspective-taking, intellectual humility, and curiosity, rather than raw cognitive ability, distinguished who reached that mode. The findings are preliminary but statistically robust.

Most AI Work Can Wait (5 minute read)

This post argues that teams building AI agents pick the model first and the architecture second, when the model choice should come last. What matters is the router, a small piece of code that assigns each request to a model tier based on the classifier's label, complexity, and context size. Get it right and 70-80% of traffic runs on near-free local or async batch models, cutting AI spend by 90% or more.

Is One Layer Enough? A Single Transformer Layer Matches Full-Parameter RL Train (1 minute read)

This paper challenges the assumption that RL post-training benefits every transformer layer equally, showing that training a single layer can recover most of the gains from full-parameter RL and sometimes exceed them. Across seven Qwen3 and Qwen2.5 models, three RL algorithms, and tasks spanning math, code, and agentic decision-making, the high-contribution layers consistently cluster in the middle of the stack, with rankings that stay stable across datasets and methods.

CursorBench 3.1 (1 minute read)

Cursor released CursorBench 3.1, an evaluation suite that scores coding agents on ambiguous, multi-file tasks drawn from real Cursor sessions. The update adds problems focused on codebase understanding, bugfinding, planning, and code review, and reports average cost per task by applying each model's published token pricing to the tokens it used.

Program-as-Weights: A Programming Paradigm for Fuzzy Functions (1 minute read)

This paper introduces fuzzy-function programming, which compiles a natural-language spec into a compact, locally-executable neural artifact instead of routing each call to an LLM API. Their Program-as-Weights system uses a 4B compiler trained on the released 10M-example FuzzyBench dataset to emit adapters for a frozen 0.6B Qwen3 interpreter, matching direct Qwen3-32B prompting while using about one-fiftieth the inference memory and running at 30 tokens/s on a MacBook M3.

Controllable Sim Agents with Behavior Latents (1 minute read)

CNeVA (Controllable Neural Variational Agents) is a traffic-simulation framework that infers a per-agent Gaussian behavior latent from per-channel discounted returns, conditioning a rectified-flow trajectory generator so engineers can steer agents along interpretable axes like speed, acceleration, and safety. On the Waymo Open Motion Dataset it reaches competitive realism while exposing per-channel controllability that higher-ranked imitation models lack, using soft eligibility gates to preserve gradient signal near reward thresholds.

WattGPU: Predicting Inference Power and Latency on Unseen GPUs and LLMs (1 minute read)

WattGPU is a pair of predictive models that estimate mean GPU power draw and inter-token latency for LLM inference using only public model metadata and GPU specifications, with no profiling or hardware access required. Across 42 open-source LLMs and 8 GPUs, it achieves median absolute percentage errors as low as 3.4% on unseen GPUs and cuts error roughly 4x versus TDP and roofline baselines.

Coding Agents Are Guessing: Measuring Action-Boundary Violations in Underspecified DevOps Instructions (1 minute read)

UnderSpecBench is a benchmark that measures action-boundary violations in LLM coding agents like Claude Code, Codex, and OpenCode on 2,208 DevOps prompt variants derived from documented incidents and CVEs. Across five agent-model configurations, 55.8% to 67.8% of runs violated at least one safety boundary, showing that underspecified instructions push agents to guess rather than fail, and that completion-centric benchmarks overstate safe autonomy.

Fast Multi-dimensional Refusal Subspaces via RFM-AGOP (1 minute read)

This paper adapts the Recursive Feature Machine (RFM) algorithm with a probe-informed initialization to extract the multi-dimensional refusal subspace in LLMs in seconds, on both reasoning (Qwen 3) and non-reasoning (Qwen 2.5) models. Prior subspace-extraction methods are too expensive to run on reasoning models with long traces, and RFM also outperformed alternatives on the ablation task.

AgenticSTS: A Bounded-Memory Testbed for Long-Horizon LLM Agents (1 minute read)

AgenticSTS is a testbed for long-horizon LLM agents that replaces appended raw transcripts with a bounded contract, where each decision runs from a fresh prompt assembled by typed retrieval so any single memory layer can be ablated in isolation. Instantiated in the deck-builder Slay the Spire 2, it ships 298 tagged trajectories, frozen memory and skill snapshots, and analysis scripts for studying how explicit memory layers shape agent decisions.

Bayesian Sparse Low-Rank Adaptation for Large Language Model Uncertainty Estimation (1 minute read)

This paper introduces DALorRA, a variational Bayesian framework that addresses overconfidence in fine-tuned LLMs by shifting uncertainty quantification from dense parameters to the rank level of low-rank adaptation. It applies stochastic masking to LoRA's rank dimensions, enabling Bayesian regularization during training and ensemble-like calibration at inference, and improves calibration without reducing reasoning accuracy.

HaloGuard 1.0: An Open Weights Constitutional Classifier for Multilingual AI Safety (1 minute read)

HaloGuard 1.0 is an open-weights safety classifier that applies the constitutional-classifier paradigm to input filtering, driven by a natural-language constitution of 46 policies and 2,940 subcategories across 46 languages. The 0.8B model reaches the best average F1 (90.9) of any open guard tested, beating baselines up to 27B parameters while holding false positives to 4.3% and false negatives to 9.5%.
