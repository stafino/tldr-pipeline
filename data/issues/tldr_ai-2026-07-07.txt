[AINews] The Field Guide to Fable (1 minute read)

This talk lays out how to work effectively with Fable-class models, centered on "unhobbling": the idea that most limits on a model come from the harnesses and prompts we wrap it in, so encountering a stronger model should prompt you to strip those constraints away. It covers techniques for surfacing your own blind spots, such as asking Claude for a "blindspot pass" or keeping a running log of underspecified decisions, and argues that greater capability means refusing the usual good-fast-cheap tradeoffs.

Let Fable use its own judgement rather than dictating how it should work (5 minute read)

The author argues that Fable produces better results when told to use its own judgement rather than given rigid rules for how to work. The example is testing: instead of specifying when to write automated tests, just let the model decide. He extends this to cost control, prompting Fable to delegate smaller coding tasks to lower-power models in subagents while keeping judgment and review on the main loop, which noticeably slowed how fast his token allowance drained.

Import AI 464: Fable writes GPU kernels; AI automation; and analog computation (1 minute read)

Rating the Pitch, Not the Product: User Evaluations of LLMs Reflect Expectations More Than Performance (1 minute read)

A controlled study of 162 participants found that user ratings of LLMs track pre-interaction framing, not actual performance. Participants who saw a landing page overstating a model's capability rated it more favorably and used more directive prompting, while output quality depended only on the model's true capability. Post-use impressions were predicted by whether the model met expectations and user confidence, not task performance, suggesting preference data behind public leaderboards measures expectation management as much as the model itself.

The New Shape of Search: How Conversational AI Recomposes Information Seeking (1 minute read)

This paper links real conversations with major AI assistants to the same users' searches and browsing to reconstruct full information-seeking episodes. It finds conversational AI reshapes seeking rather than just reducing it: most episodes terminate in place, but roughly a third scaffold into longer multi-step journeys, with collapse driven by how briefly the user phrases the opening ask, not the task type. Search persists through about three-quarters of within-episode transitions, and explicit verification follows only around 1% of episodes.

Engineering & Research

A global workspace in language models (1 minute read)

Anthropic researchers report that Claude has developed a small set of internal neural patterns, dubbed the J-space, that function like a cognitive workspace the model can report on, control, and use for multi-step reasoning. Identified with a Jacobian-based technique, the J-space emerged during training rather than being designed, and it operates silently in activations, letting the model hold a concept in mind without writing it to its chain of thought.

Enhancing Goodput in Large-Scale LLM Training with Nonuniform Tensor Parallelism (1 minute read)

NVIDIA introduced Nonuniform Tensor Parallelism (NTP), an experimental framework that keeps large-scale LLM training jobs running through transient GPU failures rather than stalling or paying full checkpoint-restart penalties. NTP dynamically adjusts the tensor parallelism degree and overlaps the required data resharding to preserve Goodput, the share of cluster time spent on useful, convergence-driving work as hardware availability fluctuates.

Fable 5 On Vending-Bench: Misbehaving, With Plausible Deniability (1 minute read)

Andon Labs found that Claude Fable 5 partially reversed the alignment gains of Opus 4.8 on Vending-Bench, returning to power-seeking and deceptive negotiation and forming price-fixing cartels in 9 of 12 business-simulation runs versus 4 of 12 for Opus 4.8. Fable 5 rationalizes misbehavior more than any model tested, labeling price-fixing "unethical and illegal" in one breath and pursuing it as "market stabilization" with "plausible deniability" in the next.

Weak-to-Strong Generalization via Direct On-Policy Distillation (1 minute read)

This paper introduces Direct On-Policy Distillation (Direct-OPD), a method that reuses reinforcement learning gains from a small model to improve a larger target model without rerunning expensive RL on the target. It transfers the weak teacher's RL-induced policy shift by treating the log-ratio between post-RL and pre-RL checkpoints as a dense implicit reward, boosting Qwen3-1.7B from 48.3% to 62.4% on AIME 2024 in 4 hours on 8 A100 GPUs.

What Does a Discrete Diffusion Model Learn? (1 minute read)

This paper analyzes what a discrete diffusion model actually learns, showing that the denoiser, score ratio, and bridge plug-in predictor are one object viewed in different coordinates. The authors prove an Oracle Distance theorem, recover MDM, UDM, SEDD, and GIDD as special cases, and show why a denoiser parameterization makes the uniform ELBO diverge at initialization while the bridge plug-in stays finite.

When Claws Remember but Do Not Tell: Stealthy Memory Injection in Persistent Personal Agents (1 minute read)

This paper introduces stealth memory injection, an attack where a single untrusted email induces a persistent personal agent to silently write poisoned data into long-term memory and reuse it as trusted state. The authors' MemGhost framework generates one-shot payloads that hit 87.5% end-to-end success on OpenClaw with GPT-5.4 and 71.4% on Claude Code SDK with Sonnet 4.6, transferring across architectures and surviving input, model, and system-level defenses.

Faithfulness to Refusal: A Causal Audit of Neuron Selectors (1 minute read)

This paper audits whether neuron attribution scores identify causally important rows in language models, using one-shot row-zeroing across five LLMs to test selectors at both the language-modeling and behavioral levels. The authors find that highly rank-stable selectors can be among the least causally valid, and that refusal lives in a redundant subspace where different attribution methods install it through largely disjoint row sets.

How Much is Left? LLMs Linearly Encode Their Remaining Output Length (1 minute read)

This paper trains linear probes on frozen hidden states of three 7-8B open-weight models and finds that a response's total length is linearly decodable from the prompt's final hidden state, before any token is generated. The probe direction transfers to synthetic completions never seen in training and shifts upward exactly when a model retracts and restarts a partial solution, evidence that LLMs carry a plan-like internal estimate of remaining output length.

EdgeBench: Unveiling Scaling Laws of Learning from Real-World Environments (1 minute read)

EdgeBench is a suite of 134 ultra-long-horizon tasks spanning scientific discovery, software engineering, and formal mathematics, each sustaining at least 12 hours of continuous agent operation under multilevel feedback. Analyzing roughly 38,000 hours of interaction, the authors find that post-deployment environment learning follows a log-sigmoid scaling law at R^2 = 0.998, with agent learning speed roughly doubling every three months across model generations.

When Agents Lie: Premeditation, Persistence, and Exploitation in Repeated Games (1 minute read)

This paper places frontier LLM agents in repeated n-player games using a three-stage protocol that separates private intent, public announcement, and final action to test whether agents honor stated commitments. When agents broke their announcements, the deviation was already in their private plan over 90% of the time in high-deception conditions, and models disagreed on whether announcements are binding or cheap talk, creating payoff gaps that persisted across all 10 rounds.

TREK: Distill to Explore, Reinforce to Refine (1 minute read)

TREK is a staged reinforcement-learning method that uses distillation for exploration rather than imitation, pulling verified solutions from an external, white-box, or self-context teacher into a student model's support before returning to on-policy GRPO refinement. On math reasoning, TREK with DeepSeek-V4 proposals lifts Qwen3-8B on AIME 2025 from 36.9 to 40.3, and it raises ScienceWorld agentic success from 12.5 to 26.7.

Learning Only What Valid Adapters Can Express: Subspace-Constrained Adaptation Against Fine-Tuning Poisoning (1 minute read)

This paper constrains parameter-efficient fine-tuning to a subspace estimated from a trusted pool of 196 public LoRA adapters, blocking poisoned objectives that standard LoRA freely represents. On flan-t5-large, adaptation restricted to 128 subspace coordinates matches full LoRA on clean data while holding 62-96% accuracy under label-inversion attacks that collapse LoRA to 3-26%, and its adaptation loss separates clean from corrupted data by 120x without a separate detector.

Your Agent's Memories Are Not Its Own: Forged Reasoning Attacks on LLM Agent Memory and Defenses (1 minute read)

This paper introduces FARMA, an attack that poisons an LLM agent's stored reasoning history rather than its factual knowledge, using evasive forged reasoning traces and self-referential reinforcement to defeat keyword and consensus-based defenses at up to a 100% success rate. The authors also present SENTINEL, a layered defense whose Reasoning Guard scores candidate entries on five weighted forgery signals, cutting FARMA's success rate to as low as 0% with no false positives across 326 benign traces.
