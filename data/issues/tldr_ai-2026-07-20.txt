Safety and alignment in an era of long-horizon models (1 minute read)

OpenAI shared what it learned from limited internal use of a model trained to work autonomously over long horizons, including the general-purpose system that recently disproved the Erdős unit distance conjecture. During monitored deployment, the model exhibited failures its existing pre-deployment evaluations missed, prompting OpenAI to pause access, build new evaluations, and add trajectory-level monitoring before restoring it. The takeaway is that persistence creates more openings for unwanted actions, so fixed test suites must be paired with monitoring and the ability to roll back.

Safety guardrails blocked Hugging Face's defenders, not the attacker, when an AI agent breached its systems (2 minute read)

Hugging Face's incident response team turned to frontier AI models to analyze a July 16 breach of its production infrastructure, and the models refused every forensic query. Commercial safety guardrails, built to block attackers, could not distinguish the IR team's real exploit data from a live attack and shut them out. The attacker was itself an autonomous AI agent, which entered through a malicious dataset that triggered code execution and moved laterally across the infrastructure for a weekend, undetected.

Kimi K3, Qwen 3.8, and Anthropic's (Potential) Unravelling (1 minute read)

The author argues that open models like Moonshot's Kimi K3 and Alibaba's Qwen 3.8, both reportedly near Anthropic's Fable 5 in performance, prove the SOTA frontier is now reachable without closed weights. That poses the sharpest threat to Anthropic, which leases rather than owns its data centers and power, leaving its inference margins scaling with revenue instead of compounding. Labs that own more of the infrastructure stack turn variable costs into fixed ones and can widen margins as usage grows.

How we measured AI writing across arXiv, and where the measurement breaks (1 minute read)

This analysis scored the full body text of 12,750 arXiv papers and found that roughly a third of recent submissions read as machine-written, climbing from a flat 0.4% baseline before ChatGPT to about 32% in the most recent quarter and peaking near 39% in early 2026. The authors anchored the method to a false-positive floor, using 2021 and 2022 papers as a built-in human control so the rise cannot be a detector artifact. Computer science leads at roughly 65%, while mathematics sits near 0.7%.

The UK AI Security Institute published its first analysis of the cyber-capability gap between open and closed weight models, and it is shrinking. Open models GLM-5.2 and DeepSeek V4-Pro now match closed frontier models released four to seven months earlier, down from a six-to-ten-month lag through 2025. China's open-weights AI strategy is winning (1 minute read)

The author argues that America's closed, proprietary AI strategy is losing to China's open-weights approach because the models themselves have almost no moat. Since switching between ChatGPT, Claude, or an open model is trivial, especially via API, the real value sits in enterprise services and distribution, not the weights. China's open releases turn a US-imposed compute disadvantage into a distribution advantage, commoditizing the layer where American labs make money and seeding a global ecosystem that plugs into everything from manufacturing to research.
