The Value of Adaptivity in LSM Bloom-Filter Tuning: A Log-Law and a Two-Clock Frontier (1 minute read)

This paper asks when adaptive Bloom-filter tuning in LSM trees actually earns its complexity, building on Monkey's static optimum across synthetic sweeps, Twitter cache traces, and a real RocksDB engine. The authors find optimal bits-per-key is affine in the log of access frequency, so coarse hotness estimates lose little, and reallocating filters only at compaction captures 96-99% of continuous tracking's benefit. The takeaway is a three-regime policy that tells teams when fine-grained tracking is worth the machinery and when it isn't.

Group Commit Self-Clocks: Why Tuning Is Unnecessary Above a Device-Set Load Threshold (1 minute read)

This paper argues that group commit's release-timer tuning is mostly unnecessary in real OLTP workloads. Because clients issue their next transaction only after the last commits, arrivals are closed-loop, so a parameter-free greedy policy that flushes the moment the log device is free self-clocks to a fixed point within ~0.1% of the best oracle-tuned timer. The square-root timer rule only matters below a device-set load threshold; on PostgreSQL, commit_delay=0 stays competitive with any tuned value.

SNAS: A Multi-Layer Defense-in-Depth Architecture for Secure Egress in Sandboxed Workloads (1 minute read)

Snowflake detailed SNAS, the production architecture that gives Snowpark's sandboxed UDFs external network access without breaking multi-tenant isolation. The design layers eBPF packet filtering, GENEVE overlay networks, and distributed egress proxies for policy-driven control, plus an EDT-based eBPF bandwidth limiter and safeguards against connection limits and port exhaustion. It runs across all Snowflake regions, supporting petabyte-scale transfers and latency-sensitive integrations to cloud APIs, external databases, and feature stores.

Collaborative Large and Small Language Models for Accurate and Scalable Data Repair (1 minute read)

LasRepair pairs an LLM with a small language model for data repair, using the LLM as an instructor that selects a global repair context to guide a cheaper SLM corrector rather than repairing rows directly in their noisy raw context. Two extensions push accuracy further: an EM-style loop that alternates between updating the corrector and refining the context, and column-calibrated confidence weighting that down-weights uncertain rows. On real-world datasets, the full version beats the strongest baseline by 18.1% F1.

Rethinking Dataset Distillation for Classification: Do Distilled Sets Outperform Coresets? (1 minute read)

Dataset distillation has been pitched as beating coreset selection by synthesizing compact training sets instead of pruning to real samples, but this paper finds the opposite. Benchmarking seven SOTA distillation methods against three coreset strategies on ImageNet-1K, ImageNet100, and ImageNette under standardized protocols, the authors show distilled sets match or underperform coresets at large scale while costing far more to build. Coresets also gave better coverage of the original data distribution, making them the more efficient default for data-centric training.

The Stanford EDGAR Filings Dataset: Reconstructing U.S. Corporate and Financial Disclosures into Layout-Faithful and Token-Efficient Pretraining Data (1 minute read)

Stanford researchers released the Stanford EDGAR Filings Dataset (SEFD), an open reconstruction of SEC filings into layout-faithful MultiMarkdown for long-context LLM pretraining. The corpus is token-efficient and has under 0.1% overlap with Common Crawl, addressing the growing scarcity of clean long-document training data. SEFD-v1 ships a 152B-token snapshot from a larger 550B-token archive, plus two benchmarks for filing-grounded numerical forecasting and complex financial-table transcription.
