News & Trends

JKR evolves KFC's entire identity around the bucket – welcome to the 'Bucketverse' (1 minute read)

Jones Knowles Ritchie rebuilt KFC's identity around the bucket, introducing a 3D logo, custom typefaces, and a refreshed Colonel across what the agency calls the "Bucketverse." The system begins rolling out in more than 150 countries, marking KFC's largest brand evolution and a bet that a single physical asset can anchor consistency across every global touchpoint.

The Xbox vs PS5 logo drama just got even stranger (1 minute read)

Microsoft's latest game marketing now features the PlayStation logo more prominently than its own Xbox branding, the newest turn in an ongoing identity muddle as the company pivots to publishing across rival platforms. The choice underscores how far Xbox has drifted from a hardware brand toward a multiplatform label, leaving its visual identity competing for space on artwork it no longer fully controls.

KFC's The Colonel gets a subtle makeover in new rebrand (1 minute read)

KFC refreshed its Colonel Sanders mascot and bucket packaging in a subtle rebrand that updates the brand's core visual assets without abandoning their recognizable form. The Colonel's illustration is cleaner and more modern, while the redesigned bucket carries the same lighter touch, signaling an evolution rather than a reinvention of one of fast food's most established identities.

Pentagram's Hugh Miller rebrands Hiut, the Welsh denim label bringing jeans-making back to Aberteifi (1 minute read)

Pentagram's Hugh Miller has rebranded Hiut Denim, the Welsh label that restarted jeans-making in Aberteifi, with an identity built on contrasts between industry and nature and utility and craft. The system centers on a custom typeface drawn from the makers' own signatures, tying the brand's mark directly to the people cutting and sewing the denim.

Jesús Rafael Soto's bright yellow kinetic pavilion installed in London (1 minute read)

The Serpentine has installed Pénétrable BBL Jaune, a bright yellow kinetic sculpture by the late Venezuelan artist Jesús Rafael Soto, alongside this year's Lanza Atelier-designed Serpentine Pavilion in central London. The interactive work, made of suspended hanging strands visitors can walk through, marks the first outdoor showing of Soto's art in the UK since his death in 2005.

WilkinsonEyre completes Toronto skyscrapers linked by elevated park (1 minute read)

WilkinsonEyre completed the second and final phase of Toronto's CIBC Square, adding a second mixed-use office tower and an elevated park that bridges the rail corridor running through the site. The development now anchors CIBC's headquarters in the central business district, with the raised public greenspace serving as the connective spine between its two skyscrapers.

Heatherwick Studio and SPPARC shelter rooftop terrace with pleated canopy at London's Olympia (1 minute read)

Heatherwick Studio and SPPARC have completed the first phase of their Olympia renovation in London, opening rooftop bars, restaurants, and landscaped terraces sheltered by a faceted glass canopy with a pleated, folded geometry. The structure tops off the events centre's transformation and signals the venue's pivot from a single-use exhibition hall toward a mixed-use destination of public outdoor space.

MVRDV tops "polite yet radical" Eindhoven housing with angular roofs (1 minute read)

MVRDV completed Nieuw Bergen, a mixed-tenure complex of 237 homes in Eindhoven, topping seven buildings with jagged grass-covered roofs that step up in scale across the site. The development pairs five new blocks with two renovated 20th-century municipal buildings and ranges from social housing to luxury penthouses, an attempt to make a dense urban infill read as varied rather than monolithic.

Patrik Schumacher removes Zaha Hadid's name as studio rebrands to ZHA (1 minute read)

Zaha Hadid Architects has officially renamed itself ZHA, with principal Patrik Schumacher removing the late founder's full name from the studio's brand following the conclusion of a legal battle over its direction. Schumacher framed the change as a "natural brand evolution" in an Instagram post, formalizing the initialism the firm has used informally for years since Hadid's death in 2016.

What’s !important #13: @function, alpha(), CSS Wordle, and More (1 minute read)

The latest issue of CSS-Tricks' What's !important roundup covers the new `@function` rule for authoring custom CSS functions, the `alpha()` function for adjusting opacity on existing colors, and Grid Lanes for more flexible track placement. It also surfaces lesser-known `dialog` element behaviors and a CSS-only Wordle build worth studying for layout tricks.

The hidden UX of payments (3 minute read)

After thirteen years designing financial products at PayPal and Highnote, the author argues that user trust is decided not by branding or onboarding flows but by micro-moments most teams treat as engineering details: a loading state, a confirmation screen, the half-second after pressing "send." The piece makes the case for designing these payment status moments deliberately, since they drive the retention and support-ticket numbers portfolio reviews never capture.

The autonomy dial: a pattern toolkit for designing human control over AI (1 minute read)

This essay introduces the "autonomy dial," a framework for calibrating how much an AI system acts independently versus deferring to a person, paired with six reusable patterns for human oversight. It is a useful vocabulary for design teams shipping AI features who keep relitigating the same control trade-offs without a shared way to name the levels in between full automation and full manual.

Beyond Usability: A UX Case Study on Using "Withdrawal Design" to Challenge Engagement Metrics in Social Robotics (1 minute read)

A randomized 8-week home trial of a social robot for autistic children found that keeping the robot lowered anxiety but reduced parent-reported social motivation and emotion-recognition gains versus children who had it withdrawn. The authors argue UX researchers should stop treating engagement and usability as proxies for success, since high SUS scores can mask ecological downsides for vulnerable users.

The board is not the game (1 minute read)

The author argues that AI product teams keep designing individual pieces, cards, and screens while leaving the underlying system of rules and interactions undesigned. The essay reframes AI design as game design, where the real work is defining how the parts cohere into a coherent experience rather than polishing each component in isolation.

UXBench: Measuring the Actionability of LLM-Generated UX Critiques (1 minute read)

UXBench introduces a benchmark for evaluating LLMs as UX judges, pairing ten families of runnable web fixtures with coverage-gated browser exploration that forces models to gather interaction evidence before critiquing. Report quality is scored by whether a downstream repair agent can actually fix the interface, and across eight frontier models the results show UX judging is neither saturated nor one-dimensional.

How artists created Star Wars: Maul – Shadow Lord's gritty, painterly aesthetic (1 minute read)

Art director Andre Kirk breaks down how the team built the gritty, painterly look of Star Wars: Maul – Shadow Lord, walking through the design of Maul's body and costume from concept to final texture. The piece is a concrete look at translating a hand-painted aesthetic into production animation, where brushstroke detail has to survive rigging, lighting, and motion without flattening into standard CG.

Of course Nike's AI-assisted World Cup kit design looks stupid (1 minute read)

The author argues that Nike's AI-assisted World Cup kit design fails because computational tools optimize for novelty and pattern-mixing rather than the proportion, fit, and restraint that tailoring demands. The piece reads as a broader case against treating generative output as finished craft, positioning human judgment about balance and wearability as the step AI workflows still skip.

This artist created a fantasy world inspired by traditional Thai culture (1 minute read)

Thai artist Choomcherd Virapat built Phaya Akat, a fantasy world that reworks traditional Thai motifs, architecture, and mythology into original digital art. The project shows how cultural specificity can anchor worldbuilding, giving designers a reference for drawing on heritage without flattening it into generic fantasy tropes.

The Missing Layer: Why EdTech Needs Design-Time Generative UI, Not Just Runtime Personalization (1 minute read)

This paper argues that adaptive EdTech is solving accessibility in the wrong place, relying on AI to reshape content at runtime after it has already been locked into a fixed form. The authors propose moving generative UI into the authoring layer, encoding lessons as modality-agnostic semantic units that produce audio-first, simplified-text, or low-bandwidth representations up front rather than patching delivery learner by learner.

Bridging the Usability Gap: Lessons from Interpreting Studies for Machine Interpreting Design (1 minute read)

This paper argues that machine interpreting systems hit near-human parity on textual fidelity benchmarks yet still fail users in practice, an "accuracy illusion" where paper-accurate output doesn't support smooth, goal-oriented conversation. Drawing on interpreting studies, the authors propose three design priorities for evaluating real interaction over isolated metrics: agency (context-sensitive initiative and repair), grounding (multimodal, discourse-level awareness), and a third dimension rooted in communicative effectiveness.

GraphStory: Collaborative Story Writing through Event-Based Narrative Editing (1 minute read)

GraphStory is an interactive writing tool that represents a narrative as a graph of plot points, letting writers connect ideas, branch into alternative storylines, and validate each path through an integrated generation workflow. A study with professional and semi-professional writers tested whether the structured, node-based view better supports early-stage brainstorming than AI assistants built around long-form text production.

Mapping the Design Space for Youth Social Media: A Framework Centered on Friendship Building (1 minute read)

A dissertation proposes a design framework for youth social media built around friendship building, organizing recurring themes from co-design work with teens into three pillars: social understanding, placeness, and identity alignment. The framework is validated through WhoamI Today, a platform deployed with 99 youth across the US and Korea, and translates developmental theory into concrete platform affordances designers can build against.
