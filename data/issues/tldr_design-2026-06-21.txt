Anthropic ships major Claude Design overhaul with design system imports, code round-trips, and a fix for its token-burning problem (2 minute read)

Anthropic overhauled Claude Design, adding design-system imports from GitHub repos or design files, code round-trips, and a rebuilt editor that cuts the heavy token consumption that let early users exhaust a weekly Claude Pro allowance in under half an hour. The update repositions the tool from a prototyping demo into a brand-compliance layer that checks generated UI against imported components and auto-corrects before export.

Heatherwick Studio unveils "space observatory" in AlUla (1 minute read)

Heatherwick Studio released designs for AlUla Manara, a textured-stone stargazing and research center planned for the Saudi desert near the AlUla UNESCO World Heritage Site. The building is shaped around the region's exceptional night-sky visibility, pairing a public visitor experience with astronomical research facilities in one of the few remaining low-light-pollution sites accessible to a mass audience.

Midjourney, the AI image generator, is developing a full-body ultrasonic scanner (1 minute read)

Midjourney, best known for its text-to-image AI, announced Midjourney Medical and its first hardware product: a scanner that submerges you in water and uses a ring of half a million ultrasonic emitters to build a sub-millimeter 3D body map in under 60 seconds. Built with Butterfly Network under an exclusive ultrasound-on-chip license, the machine targets MRI-quality imaging at roughly 100x the speed, with company-run scanning spas planned over the next 12 months.

Two modernisms (1 minute read)

The author traces a thread connecting Mies van der Rohe hiding structural steel, Harry Beck removing geography from the London Tube map, and Radix stripping default styles from UI primitives, arguing that each reduction solved a practical problem rather than expressing a modernist creed. The piece reframes minimalism in design systems as engineering pragmatism, a useful corrective to treating stripped-back components as an aesthetic ideology.

Century-old Melbourne corner shop transformed into family home (1 minute read)

Kister Architects converted a 1910 Melbourne milk bar, which served its neighborhood for over a century before closing in 2016, into a family home organized around a planted internal courtyard that doubles as the entrance. The project is a clean case study in adaptive reuse, showing how a commercial corner footprint can absorb greenery and domestic privacy without erasing the building's street-facing history.

Compact Warsaw apartment evokes "ingenuity and efficiency" of maritime architecture (1 minute read)

Dawid Konieczny carved a 34-square-metre Warsaw apartment into a livable space with curved walls and bespoke joinery, drawing on the interwar docklands and maritime architecture of the Port Praski district. The result reads as a case study in spatial efficiency, where built-in furniture and rounded partitions replace the parallel walls that would otherwise eat the floor plan.

Plan Plan uses "affordable materials" for Brooklyn apartment renovation (1 minute read)

Plan Plan renovated a compact Brooklyn duplex, the CC Residence, on a tight budget using stained birch plywood for built-in storage throughout. The project is a useful case study in treating affordable materials and resourceful planning as the design constraint, rather than a fallback, to fit a family into a small footprint.

The golden rule of Customizable Select (5 minute read)

Customizable Select arrives in Safari 27, letting developers fully style native `<select>` elements (custom arrows, icons, color swatches, option layouts) without JavaScript libraries or nested `<div>` hacks, while keeping built-in keyboard navigation and accessibility. WebKit's one rule: always give option elements real text content or accessible labels, since icon-only options break screen readers, scannability, and unsupported browsers all at once.

Why the best part of the flow isn't the end (1 minute read)

The author argues that the most satisfying part of a flow is rarely the final transaction, pointing to South Korea's "dopamine sites" that let people place food orders that never arrive yet still feel rewarding. The takeaway for designers is that anticipation, browsing, and selection carry much of a product's emotional payload, so stripping a flow down to its endpoint can quietly remove the part users actually enjoy.

How The Dinosaurs' VFX helped audiences travel across vast spans of time (1 minute read)

Netflix's The Dinosaurs uses VFX studio Lux Aeterna's sweeping planetary transitions to make geological time legible, showing continents drifting, coastlines shifting, and climates changing between each prehistoric era. The piece is a useful study in treating environment as narrative rather than decoration, with the showrunner framing comprehension, not spectacle, as the core problem the visual language had to solve.

Bear app developers announce Lettera, a beautiful Markdown editor for Mac (1 minute read)

The developers behind Bear released Lettera, a standalone native macOS Markdown editor in beta on TestFlight, built on the same WYSIWYG editor that powers Bear 2 with live CommonMark rendering, hidden syntax while writing, tables, code blocks, and Math via MathJax. It opens individual files or entire folders as workspaces, defaults to an iCloud folder, and exports to plain text, RTF, HTML, PDF, ePub, and more.

Blender 5.2: Coming Soon With Improved Simulations (1 minute read)

Blender 5.2, entering beta with a July release, rebuilds its physics simulations on top of geometry nodes, making cloth, fluid, and hair effects that previously pushed pros toward Houdini far more workable. The node-based approach makes tricky cases like tearing cloth or pressure-driven inflation straightforward, and optimized algorithms run the standard cloth-on-monkey-head demo roughly twice as fast.
