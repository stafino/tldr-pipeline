Dramatic atriums define Bangkok shopping centre by Linehouse (1 minute read)

Linehouse designed the interiors of Bangkok's 40,000-square-metre Central Park shopping centre around three atriums clad in silver, copper, and bronze, metals read as auspicious in Thai culture. Each void carries a distinct character, from the calm Silver Void at street level to the community-hub Copper Void, whose cladding shifts from dusty pink to reddish copper under overhead light against stepped, illuminated escalators.

Space4Architecture transforms Brooklyn warehouse into residence (1 minute read)

Does the 2026 World Cup font have a problem? (1 minute read)

Typographers including Oliver Schöndorfer argue the same density breaks down on broadcast scoreboards and small stats labels, where the tight forms turn illegible and call for a dedicated low-resolution variant.

When LLM Rationales Become User-Facing: Effects on Trust Perception, Decision-Making, and Gaze Behaviors (1 minute read)

Two linked studies test whether LLM reasoning rationales help users calibrate trust or just persuade through fluent prose, finding that rationale correctness and certainty framing matter while presentation format does not, and that incorrect rationales drew more attention and cognitive effort while lowering trust in the system. The authors argue against the assumption that more reasoning is always better, favoring rationales that are selective, tied to evidence, and calibrated in how they express certainty.

Your design system runs on one person’s judgment-AI is about to prove it (3 minute read)

The author argues that every mature design system develops a single human gatekeeper who reviews each contribution for tokens, naming, API sanity, and accessibility, making that person an unacknowledged single point of failure. As AI-generated contributions flood review queues, the post contends manual gatekeeping becomes untenable, and the only fix is dispersing ownership without lowering the quality bar.
