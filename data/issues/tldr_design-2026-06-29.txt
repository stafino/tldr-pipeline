Layer designs "playful and personal" modular system for charging devices at home (1 minute read)

L-shaped roofs create "generous shadows" over Los Cabos residence (1 minute read)

Estudio Ignacio Urquiza Ana Paula de Alba topped Casa en Palmilla, a hilltop residence in Los Cabos, Mexico, with two lightweight L-shaped roofs spanning four volumes arranged around a central trapezoidal courtyard. The paired roofs cast deep shade across the open-air circulation between the volumes, a passive response to the site's intense sun that doubles as the project's defining formal gesture.

Japan Doesn’t Design With Less. It Designs With Ma. (1 minute read)

The author argues that calling Japanese design "minimalist" is a misread, proposing the concept of Ma, or negative space as an active design element, as the framework that explains both the spare Zen garden and the saturated chaos of Shibuya Crossing. The piece is a useful corrective for designers who reach for "restraint" and "less is more" reflexively, reframing emptiness as intentional spacing between elements rather than the mere absence of them.

How LULACREATES designed baby skincare brand Smoosh to look at home on a beauty shelf (1 minute read)

LULACREATES built the identity for Smoosh, a premium baby skincare brand, around custom rounded lowercase type with a floating 'o' and an ochre-yellow palette, positioning it against a category split between clinical heritage brands and character-led kids' products. The studio worked within tight production limits, varying packaging color per product so parents can grab the right bottle mid-bath, while borrowing beauty-shelf cues to signal both trust and desirability.

The curious case of the disappearing Polish S (2015) (1 minute read)

Marcin Wichary traces a single Medium bug, where Polish users couldn't type the letter Ś, back through four converging causes: the structure of the Polish alphabet, Communist-era hardware constraints, typewriter key layouts, and a browser keyboard-shortcut collision. The piece is a model of debugging as historical detective work, showing how an interface failure can have roots spanning decades of typography and computing.

What are hypertokens? The layer between tokens and components, rebuilt for agents (3 minute read)

Figma's Jake Albaugh coined "hypertokens" at Config 2026 to name the layer between single-decision tokens and full components, the bundles of font, weight, line height, and spacing that every tool currently copies and lets drift. The argument is that designer drift used to be invisibly smoothed over by eye, but agents build exactly what they read and guess the rest, so human-authored intent now needs a shared definition machines can consume.

The bullshit asymmetry principle was survivable. AI made production of slop almost free. (3 minute read)

The author argues that Brandolini's law, where refuting nonsense costs ten times more than producing it, was survivable only because making a claim still cost human hours, and generative AI has collapsed that cost to zero while refutation stays expensive. The takeaway for designers is that the asymmetry is a design problem to be moderated inside your own product, not a fact-checking race you can win.

The state of the creative industry 2026: what our survey tells us about pay, burnout and AI (1 minute read)

Creative Boom's 2026 survey of 882 creative professionals found 69% experienced burnout in the past year, with mid-career designers hit hardest at 77%. AI adoption sits at 86% while approval lags far behind, with only 10% calling its industry impact positive and 58% calling it mixed, a gap that reflects a workforce adopting the tools under pressure rather than enthusiasm.
