Vector Architects rejects "white cube" design for Aranya Art Center Guangzhou (1 minute read)

Vector Architects broke the Aranya Art Center Guangzhou into free-standing galleries arranged around a preserved 50-meter avenue of 25 banyan trees, rejecting the enclosed white-cube museum plan. The main hall pairs textured glass brick outside with translucent polycarbonate inside to pull in daylight while holding a climate boundary against south China's heat and humidity.

How Thirst redesigned M&S's beer aisle, from stamp-inspired lagers to bold, flavour-led craft (1 minute read)

Marks & Spencer rolled out two own-label beer ranges designed by drinks-branding agency Thirst, one Continental line built on a vintage postage-stamp system and one craft range that credits its brewery partners on-pack. Printing splits across four countries with no foils or embossing available, so the premium read comes entirely from composition, colour and etched-style animal illustrations tied to each beer's country of origin.

How to apply Nir Eyal’s Hook Model to AI products, safely (3 minute read)

The author maps Nir Eyal's four-phase Hook Model onto AI assistants, arguing that trigger, action, variable reward, and investment describe chatbot usage almost exactly: the unpredictable quality of each response is the variable reward, and saved preferences or uploaded style guides are the investment that loads the next session. The piece is a practical guide to using the loop where the underlying utility is real, without engineering compulsion for tier upgrades or per-token revenue.

Cities aren't malls. So why are they starting to look like them? (1 minute read)

The authors argue that civic wayfinding has fallen into "mall-ification," where signage systems in Ohio, Texas, and Los Angeles read as interchangeable because they optimize for function alone. They point to Nicolas Vrignaud's work at Docks en Seine and Bruce Mau's Toronto Carpet Factory identity as counterexamples, where material, scale, and form are drawn from the site's own history rather than applied on top of it.

Inside Wolves of Tokyo: the design lessons behind London's new Japan-noir restaurant (1 minute read)

Run for the Hills, the studio behind London's new Wolves of Tokyo restaurant, derived the brand palette from the interior rather than the logo, pulling the lacquer red and midnight blue of the tables and waitstations back into the graphic identity. Co-founder Chris Trotman argues that running branding and spatial teams concurrently rather than sequentially keeps color, material, and type from competing once they share a room.
