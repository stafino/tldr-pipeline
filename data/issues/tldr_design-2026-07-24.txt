Autodesk’s SIGGRAPH announcements look unrelated, but they all solve the same huge problem (1 minute read)

Autodesk used SIGGRAPH 2026 to ship updates across Maya, 3ds Max, Flow Production Tracking, and Arnold, including a MotionMaker feature that lets studios train custom generative motion models on their own rigs and mocap. The scattered-looking announcements share one goal: compressing the gap between idea and rendered result by cutting the waiting built into 3D pipelines, from render queues to remote review to rebuilding real-world assets Gaussian Splats could capture.

A glimpse behind the scenes of Coca-Cola's new branding (1 minute read)

Jones Knowles Ritchie released a behind-the-scenes video on Coca-Cola's identity refresh, built on a brief to "make Coca-Cola more Coca-Cola" by amplifying its core brand assets and unifying the system across territories and touchpoints. The film leans on retro imagery to show how JKR sharpened the existing equities rather than reinventing them, a restraint-first approach worth studying for anyone rationalizing a sprawling global brand.

Derek&Eric embraces the chaos with a joyfully bold brand for The Glorious Mess (1 minute read)

Derek&Eric, an independent London studio, created The Glorious Mess, a new dessert brand for meringue maker Flower & White built around the happy-accident origin of the Eton Mess. The identity leans into that chaos with expressive typography, juicy fruit illustrations, and energetic photography, giving a category thin on distinctive branding a flexible, unmistakably British system that scales across new flavors and formats.

Koto helps restaurant-discovery app Franki make time dance (1 minute read)

Koto gave Franki, the merchant-funded restaurant-discovery app, a warm yellow-soaked identity built on the line "make time dance," positioning it against Google, Tripadvisor, Yelp, and a wave of video-first discovery rivals. The concept treats time as the currency of dining out, translating into an energetic, motion-led system meant to sell an app-only experience and keep users posting recommendations after download.

Manica unveils Kansas City Chiefs stadium topped with transparent dome (1 minute read)

Manica released its design for a 70,000-seat, fully enclosed Kansas City Chiefs stadium topped with a transparent dome, replacing Arrowhead Stadium at a cost of $3 billion. The venue is set to open in 2031, and the clear roof lets the team run a climate-controlled interior while preserving the daylit, open-air feel of the original.

Glasgow School of Art "cannot rebuild" Mackintosh Building (1 minute read)

The Glasgow School of Art said it cannot afford to rebuild Charles Rennie Mackintosh's 1910 building without external funding, eight years after the 2018 fire gutted the A-listed structure for the second time. The admission leaves the future of one of Britain's most significant Art Nouveau landmarks dependent on a fundraising effort the school has not yet scoped, with no reinstatement timeline attached.

Algae "biocells" powered by photosynthesis could replace disposable batteries (1 minute read)

Bio-designer Lucia Giron, working with the University of Cambridge's Department of Biochemistry, built prototypes of algae-based "biocells" that generate electricity through photosynthesis as a replacement for disposable batteries. The design leans on Cambridge's biophotovoltaics research, where living algae act like biological solar panels converting light into current, pointing toward low-toxicity power sources for the small devices that currently drain single-use cells.

Four questions every tool should answer (even Figma) about AI training model usage (3 minute read)

The author argues that Figma's November 2025 class-action suit is less about copyright than about defaults, framing the opt-in-versus-opt-out choice for AI training on customer files as a design decision teams make on their users' behalf. The piece offers a four-question, twenty-minute trust test for auditing your own product's training defaults, using Zoom's and Slack's quiet opt-out enrollments as cautionary precedent.

The screens are getting demoted (3 minute read)

The author argues that much of UI exists only to bridge the gap between what a user wants and what the software forces them to do by hand, and that AI is collapsing that gap by acting on intent directly. As natural-language commands replace multi-step flows like compose-attach-browse-select-send, the piece contends that many screens are becoming fallbacks rather than the primary interface designers build around.

Is the Tinder rebrand really a success? Not everyone is so sure... (1 minute read)

Jade Horton, Strategy Partner at Elmwood Brand Consultants, argues Tinder's decade-first rebrand nails the visuals but not the substance: the bold flame, more confident type, and shift from soft pink to grown-up red set an ambition the product doesn't yet deliver. She points to the gap between the identity's warm talk of honesty and a feature that lets AI scan your camera roll to rate chemistry, a mismatch already trending as "creepy" on TikTok and Reddit.

CRAFT: Exploring Wearable Creative AI on Smart Glasses for Fiction Writing in Real-World Contexts (1 minute read)

CRAFT is a research prototype for AI smart glasses that turns a writer's real-world surroundings into fiction prompts, translating in-situ experiences like serendipitous street encounters into narrative ideas. Across interviews, co-design workshops, and 24 field-trial sessions with writers, the authors surface concrete design goals for wearable creative AI: augment perception without breaking immersion, ground authenticity in lived experience, and preserve creative agency and the boundary between life and art.

Flint: A Semantics-Driven Data Visualization Intermediate Language (1 minute read)

Flint is an intermediate language for data visualization that lets authors write concise, semantics-driven specs, declaring what data fields mean structurally instead of hand-configuring scales, axes, and formatting. A hierarchical semantic model lets the compiler derive and optimize library-agnostic configs, then compile them to Vega-Lite, Apache ECharts, and other target grammars for both human and AI authoring.

A Framework of User Experience Principles for Human-AI Agent Interaction in the Workplace (1 minute read)

This paper proposes eight core UX principles for human-AI agent interaction in enterprise workflows, validated through participatory design workshops, expert review, meta-analysis, and interviews. The principles function as actionable guardrails for the trust and adoption problems designers now face as agents move into daily business tools, offering a structured starting point before empirical norms exist.

Building Ridgeline: Engineering a Real-Time 3D Experience in Webflow (5 minute read)

A developer documents building Ridgeline, a Webflow site that renders three real alpine treks as live Three.js terrain from actual elevation data (SRTM DEMs and cleaned GPX tracks), rather than video loops or baked sprites, while keeping the project human-editable. The build log covers the core architecture decision of getting real-time 3D into Webflow, and details a stack of React Three Fiber, GSAP, and Blender assembled in about a week through the Webflow MCP.

Sculpting a Shapeless Medium (5 minute read)

The author argues that chat-based AI interfaces default to a shapeless text box that hides the model's capabilities and forces users to guess what to type. She makes the case for giving generative tools visible structure and affordances, using spatial layouts, adjustable controls, and previews so designers shape the interaction rather than leaving users staring at a blank prompt.

Steal this UX framework from Dubai’s most over-the-top hotel (1 minute read)

A Google UX researcher argues that hospitality design, not app design, is the richer source of interaction patterns, using a week at Atlantis The Palm to extract reusable UX principles from things like a chocolate fountain that never spills. The framing turns invisible service choreography into design heuristics, a useful prompt to study systems that manage human behavior in physical space rather than reverse-engineering competitor screens.

I learned 5 fundamental truths of game art direction, which aren't always about art (1 minute read)

An art director's talk at Develop: Brighton argues that game art direction starts from first principles rather than aesthetics: define a single creative North Star, then let asset density follow the player's micro, macro, and meta loops. The Skyrim-versus-Arc Raiders comparison is the sharpest takeaway, showing how identical walking-speed density forces very different art budgets depending on map scale.

A GTA 6 concept artist says you’re probably using too many tools – why the 80:20 rule is all you need (1 minute read)

Concept artist Gargi Roy, who worked on GTA 6 at Rockstar, argues that chasing perfection and hoarding tools slows idea generation, and that an 80:20 approach delivers most of the value. Her method: define reference categories before collecting images, never start from a blank canvas, and lean on shortcuts like 2D kitbashing, rough Blender blockouts, and Big-Medium-Small shape hierarchy.

HARP: The Human--AI Research Platform (1 minute read)

This paper introduces HARP, a research platform that places study participants in controlled scenarios with live, configurable AI agents rather than static prototypes, letting researchers fix model parameters and response characteristics across conditions while logging prompt-composition time, deletions, and keystroke pauses. The tooling targets a real gap for UX researchers, since transcripts alone hide how users hesitate over and revise prompts before hitting send.

Flux 3 (1 minute read)

Black Forest Labs released FLUX 3 in early access, a multimodal foundation model that jointly trains on images, video, and audio to generate stills or synced video-plus-audio from text or image and video references. Built on the lab's Self-Flow method for aligning generation and understanding in one architecture, it targets both content creation and physical AI, though early-access limits mean production workflows should wait.
