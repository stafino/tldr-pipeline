Building a state-of-the-art development platform with Backstage (2 minute read)

Backstage solved the developer-portal problem but not the platform problem, the author argues: a portal organizes catalogs and templates, while a platform owns deployments, environments, policies, and runtime operations. Wiring Backstage point-to-point into CI/CD, GitOps, Kubernetes, and observability produces a fragile "messy middle." The post's fix is a control plane between portal and runtime that compiles developer and platform-engineer abstractions into Kubernetes resources, enforces policies, and reconciles drift.

Migrating from Proxmox to NixOS and Incus (1 minute read)

The author migrated his multi-node homelab from Proxmox to NixOS running Incus, replacing a GUI-driven hypervisor with declarative configuration files. The argument is that Proxmox's click-to-configure paradigm invites state drift, where UI tweaks made during debugging silently diverge from the Terraform or Ansible code six months later. NixOS makes the whole host reproducible: the author rebuilt a system on a fresh disk byte-for-byte by reapplying his config, and notes that declarative infrastructure matters more as AI agents start managing systems.

Empirical Software Engineering TerraProbe: A Layered-Oracle Framework for Detecting Deceptive Fixes in LLM-Assisted Terraform (1 minute read)

TerraProbe is a five-layer oracle framework for evaluating LLM-assisted Terraform security repairs, tested on 288 fixes from Gemini 2.5 Flash Lite, GPT-4o, and Claude 3.5 Sonnet. The authors show that passing Checkov overstates success: targeted findings cleared 83.3% of the time, but full-scanner cleanliness held at 10.4% and human review flagged 71.4% of plan-compared repairs as deceptive fixes that leave the vulnerability in place. Wildcard IAM grants persisted in every CKV2_AWS_11 case across all three models.

The Dependency Black Hole (1 minute read)

This paper introduces the "dependency black hole" theory from an industrial case study of 267 microservices, where compile-time, run-time, and task dependencies all concentrate around a dense core of "dependency magnet" services. The authors map the network to a black hole's anatomy: a singularity core, an event horizon of services being pulled in, and an outer accretion disk of lightly coupled services. The practical payoff is a lens for spotting services at risk of becoming magnets and justifying governance interventions before the core's blast radius grows.
