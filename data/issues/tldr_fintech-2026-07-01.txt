Visa, Mastercard and 140 firms launch Open USD, a stablecoin built to undercut Circle (1 minute read)

A consortium of 140+ firms including Visa, Mastercard, Stripe, Coinbase and BlackRock launched Open USD (OUSD), a dollar-pegged stablecoin run by independent company Open Standard that lets businesses mint and redeem at no cost and routes most reserve interest to partners rather than a single issuer. Open Standard Unveils Open USD, a Bank- and Tech-Backed Stablecoin Governed by Its Users (1 minute read)

A consortium of more than 140 companies, including Visa, Mastercard, Stripe, BlackRock, BNY and Coinbase, introduced Open USD (OUSD), a dollar stablecoin that returns nearly all reserve earnings to the businesses that adopt it rather than to a single issuer. Governed collectively through Open Standard and launching natively on Solana in 2026, OUSD inverts the incumbent float-income model, though its reserve custodians, attestation cadence and issuing entity remain undisclosed.

Taiwan passes key crypto law, clearing legal uncertainty for digital asset sector (1 minute read)

Taiwan's Legislative Yuan passed the Virtual Asset Service Act in its third reading, requiring crypto exchanges and stablecoin issuers to obtain a license from the Financial Supervisory Commission before operating, with stablecoin issuers also needing central bank approval and full reserve backing. Existing AML-registered platforms get 12 months to apply and 21 months to secure approval, and violators face up to seven years in prison and NT$100M ($3.14M) fines.

Taiwan’s sweeping crypto law raises the bar with licensing, reserve mandates, and tough penalties (5 minute read)

Taiwan's Legislative Yuan passed the Virtual Asset Service Act, requiring all crypto exchanges and platforms to obtain FSC licensing and meet stricter custody, cybersecurity, and fund-segregation standards, with stablecoin issuers needing central bank and FSC approval plus 100% reserves. Unlicensed operation now carries penalties of up to seven years in prison and NT$100M (about $3.14M), moving Taiwan from basic AML registration to full regulatory supervision once President Lai signs.

The Lowest-Logical-Fare Problem (1 minute read)

Expense management software can automate a travel policy but not the judgment behind it, because the cheapest bookable option often isn't the cheapest actual outcome once you count connections, drive times, and a traveler's wasted hours. The author calls the real target the "lowest logical fare," a per-trip judgment call that no hard-coded rule survives. The moment you write the rule down, reality produces the exception.
