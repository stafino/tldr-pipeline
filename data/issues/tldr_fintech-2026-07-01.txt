Visa, Mastercard and Coinbase have launched a new global stablecoin (1 minute read)

A consortium of over 140 businesses led by Visa, Mastercard, and Coinbase launched Open Standard, a stablecoin network whose dollar-pegged coin, Open USD, ships later this year under the GENIUS Act's 1:1 reserve rules. Cloudflare Launches Monetization Gateway for Stablecoin Payments via x402 (1 minute read)

Cloudflare opened a waitlist for its Monetization Gateway, a tool that lets operators charge for any page, dataset, API, or MCP tool behind its network, with payments settling in stablecoins over the x402 protocol. The product targets sub-cent, per-request billing for AI agents rather than human visitors, and follows AWS wiring Coinbase's x402 into CloudFront in June to let publishers charge agents per request in USDC.

Open Standard Unveils Open USD, a Bank- and Tech-Backed Stablecoin Governed by Its Users (1 minute read)

A consortium of more than 140 companies, including Visa, Mastercard, Stripe, BlackRock, BNY and Coinbase, introduced Open USD (OUSD), a dollar stablecoin that returns nearly all reserve earnings to the businesses that adopt it rather than to a single issuer. Governed collectively through Open Standard and launching natively on Solana in 2026, OUSD inverts the incumbent float-income model, though its reserve custodians, attestation cadence and issuing entity remain undisclosed.

Visa, Stripe and 140 others back new Open USD stablecoin to challenge Tether (1 minute read)

A consortium of more than 140 companies, including Visa, Stripe, BlackRock, Mastercard, and Coinbase, is backing Open USD (OUSD), a new stablecoin from Open Standard LLC launching later this year to challenge Tether and Circle. The token lets businesses mint and redeem with no fees or volume limits and returns most reserve revenue to partners, breaking from the issuer-keeps-the-interest model that made USDT and USDC dominant.

The Lowest-Logical-Fare Problem (1 minute read)

Expense management software can automate a travel policy but not the judgment behind it, because the cheapest bookable option often isn't the cheapest actual outcome once you count connections, drive times, and a traveler's wasted hours. The author calls the real target the "lowest logical fare," a per-trip judgment call that no hard-coded rule survives. The moment you write the rule down, reality produces the exception.
