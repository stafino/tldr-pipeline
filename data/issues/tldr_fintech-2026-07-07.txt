Pay for APIs with USDC via x402 and Apify (5 minute read)

Apify integrated the x402 payment protocol, letting developers pay for its web-scraping APIs with USDC per request instead of maintaining a prepaid account or subscription. The setup revives HTTP's 402 Payment Required status to settle stablecoin micropayments inline with each API call, pointing toward pay-per-use machine-to-machine billing where agents can transact without card rails or human-managed credentials.

Binance taps into Bitcoin holders’ hunger for yield with new covered call yield play (5 minute read)

The launch brings a strategy that once demanded options expertise to retail holders, and arrives as BlackRock rolls out a similar covered-call bitcoin income ETF.

Klarna seeks U.S. bank charter in latest push beyond buy now, pay later (1 minute read)

Klarna applied to U.S. federal and state regulators to establish Klarna Bank USA, an FDIC-backed institution chartered in Utah, marking its latest move beyond buy now, pay later. Owning a charter would let Klarna fund loans with customer deposits instead of pricier wholesale financing and cut its reliance on partner banks like WebBank, joining Mercury and a wave of fintechs pursuing charters over sponsor-bank arrangements.

European banking watchdogs ECB and ESRB warn that frontier AI models pose "systemic risks to the financial system", giving lenders four months to prepare (Financial Times) (1 minute read)

The ECB and ESRB warned that frontier AI models pose systemic risks to the financial system, giving lenders four months to assess and mitigate their exposure. The move signals EU regulators now treat concentrated AI dependence as a stability threat on par with liquidity or credit risk, pressuring banks to inventory model usage and vendor concentration before the deadline.

Coinbase secures UK investment services license to add derivatives and equities trading (1 minute read)

Coinbase received authorization from the UK Financial Conduct Authority to offer investment services, letting institutional traders access crypto, equity, and commodity perpetual futures while retail users trade equities on the platform for the first time. The license sits alongside Coinbase's existing UK e-money license and crypto registration, and marks its largest UK expansion yet as it builds toward an "everything exchange" spanning payments, savings, derivatives, and equities.

Where Robinhood's 7% yield comes from (5 minute read)

Robinhood launched Earn, a 7% APY yield product available in the core app to all 27 million users, powered by a Morpho vault that Steakhouse curates across spUSDG, USDe, and SyrupUSDG for overcollateralized institutional lending. Roughly half the yield is marketing incentive today, but as USDG accumulates float, Robinhood expects that share to come from the stablecoin itself. Paired with Robinhood's stock tokens, the setup starts to resemble a decentralized prime broker, insured by Lloyd's of London against smart-contract risk.

The coin nobody owns (5 minute read)

OpenUSD is a new dollar-backed stablecoin run by an independent company, OpenStandard, backed by Visa, Mastercard, Stripe, Coinbase, BlackRock, and roughly a hundred banks and processors. Unlike Circle and Tether, it shares reserve yield with distributors, keeps governance neutral, and charges no mint-and-burn fees, opening up large-dollar and capital-markets flows that were previously uneconomical. The author argues OpenUSD isn't fighting the incumbents but targeting the money that has stayed on the sidelines.

Vitalik Buterin proposes ‘Extremely Lean’ Ethereum, shrinking the chain to near-zero state with ZK proofs (1 minute read)

Vitalik Buterin proposed "Extremely Lean" Ethereum, a two-step redesign of the Beacon Chain that shifts state management to validators and replaces per-epoch balance updates with a single daily ZK-STARK proof, shrinking validator state to roughly 6 bytes. Buterin frames it as Ethereum's third major iteration, on par with the Merge, and says the full "Lean Ethereum" overhaul of consensus and execution could take around four years while scaling the network to millions of validators.

With AI, a wrong answer is a bug. A wrong action is an incident (3 minute read)

The author argues that as banks push AI agents into production, the security risk shifts from what a model can see to what an agent is allowed to do inside systems that move money and hold regulated data. A copilot's wrong answer is a quality bug, but an autonomous agent's wrong action is an incident, sometimes reportable. On April 30, the Five Eyes cyber agencies issued their first joint guidance on agentic AI, naming overprivileged agents the leading risk and calling strict least privilege critical.
