SpaceX IPO raises total of $85.7 billion as underwriters exercise 'greenshoe' overallotment option (1 minute read)

SpaceX's IPO now totals $85.7 billion after underwriters exercised their greenshoe overallotment option, up from the $75 billion raised Thursday. That makes it the largest public offering on record, and it values Musk's combined space and AI business above almost every company that has ever gone public. The size of the raise signals that capital is again flowing to hard-tech and frontier infrastructure at a scale software founders rarely see, with rockets and compute, not apps, drawing the biggest checks in the market right now.

SpaceX to acquire Cursor for $60B in stock, days after blockbuster IPO (1 minute read)

SpaceX agreed to acquire Cursor for $60B in all-stock, days after Cursor's blockbuster IPO, to shore up its struggling AI division. The pitch to IPO investors leaned on a claimed $26 trillion addressable market in AI, a number that has to absorb a frontier coding tool bolted onto a rocket company. The deal signals that the largest private hardware players now see AI tooling as core infrastructure worth paying public-market premiums to own outright, even when the strategic fit is far from obvious.

DeepSeek Becomes China’s Most Valuable AI Startup After $7.4 Billion Fundraise (1 minute read)

DeepSeek raised more than $7.4 billion in its first outside round, making it China's most valuable AI startup. ChatGPT’s market share slips below 50% for first time (1 minute read)

ChatGPT's share of the AI assistant market fell below 50% for the first time, even as it stays the most-used product with over 1.1 billion monthly users. Gemini now sits at 662 million and Claude at 245 million, so the gap is closing from both the consumer and enterprise sides. The takeaway for founders building on top of these models is that the assistant layer is becoming a real multi-horse race, which changes distribution math, default integrations, and who you bet your roadmap on.

ChapsVision to replace Palantir in major contract with French intelligence agency (5 minute read)

France's domestic intelligence agency is dropping Palantir and handing the contract to ChapsVision, a homegrown French data-analysis firm. The move is the latest sign that European governments increasingly treat dependence on US software as a strategic liability, not just a procurement choice. For founders building in defense, govtech, or any regulated sector, sovereignty is becoming a feature buyers will pay for, and incumbents with the deepest tech can still lose on where their code is written and who controls it.

AI agent authorization startup Arcade nabs $60M investment (1 minute read)

Arcade AI raised $60M in a Series A led by SYN Ventures, with Morgan Stanley and Wipro joining, to build authorization infrastructure for AI agents. As agents move from demos into production, the unsolved problem is letting them act on a user's behalf across third-party services without handing over standing credentials or broad access. The round signals that identity and permissioning are becoming a distinct layer of the agent stack, and the founders building agentic products will need to answer how their software authenticates before customers let it touch real systems.

Space Stocks Dip After SpaceX IPO (1 minute read)

SpaceX's debut went well, but the rest of the public space sector sold off the same day, with major listed players dropping across the board. The move suggests investors are treating SpaceX as the place to own space exposure and rotating out of everyone else rather than bidding the whole category up. For founders in adjacent hardware-heavy markets, it is a reminder that a dominant player going public can pull capital toward itself and away from the rest of the field.

US Offers Conditional $500 Million Loan for Rare-Earths Plant (1 minute read)

The Pentagon's Office of Strategic Capital offered mining startup Phoenix Tailings a conditional $500M in long-term debt to build a domestic rare-earth processing plant. The money flows through a defense channel, not a venture one, signaling that Washington now treats supply-chain independence as a national-security line item it will underwrite directly. For founders in hard tech, the takeaway is that the cheapest capital for capital-intensive builds increasingly comes from agencies trying to reshore critical materials, not from VCs.

State Attorneys General Are Investigating OpenAI (1 minute read)

A coalition of state attorneys general has opened an investigation into OpenAI, probing its handling of user data, the safety of minors, and its move into advertising. The scope matters because it spans the parts of the business OpenAI is leaning on for growth, from the consumer data that trains and personalizes its products to the ad model it is just starting to build. Regulatory pressure at the state level tends to move faster and more unpredictably than federal action, and it lands on every company building on top of OpenAI's platform too.

SpaceX Set to Overtake Microsoft, Amazon in Value as Stock Soars (1 minute read)

SpaceX is on track to pass Amazon as the world's fifth-largest public company, with shares up more than 50% across three sessions since its debut. The run puts a launch-and-satellite business above one of the defining platforms of the internet era, on the strength of Starlink's recurring revenue and Starship's payload economics. The market is now pricing space infrastructure as a core franchise rather than a moonshot, which resets what a credible hard-tech valuation looks like for every founder raising in the category.

Anthropic’s Safety Superpower (1 minute read)

Anthropic's deep conviction in its own safety mission has become a business weapon, justifying moves that favor its commercial interests and even put it at odds with the U.S. government. The author argues that certainty about being the good actor gives a company license to act aggressively, because every decision can be framed as serving safety. The risk is that a safety identity becomes a blank check that few inside the company will question.

European tech has discovered some swagger (5 minute read)

European tech has spent years apologizing for not being Silicon Valley, building quietly and pricing modestly while founders defaulted to the assumption that the real action happened elsewhere. That posture is fading, as a generation of operators who have built and sold at scale now start companies expecting to win globally rather than get acquired early. The shift that matters is not capital or talent, which Europe already had, but the willingness to make ambitious bets out loud and defend them.
