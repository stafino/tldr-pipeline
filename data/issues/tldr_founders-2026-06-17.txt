Pramaana Labs raises $27M seed round from Khosla Ventures to bring formal verification to AI (1 minute read)

Pramaana Labs raised $27M in seed funding led by Khosla Ventures to put a formal-verification layer on top of LLMs, targeting high-stakes verticals like tax, law, and drug discovery. The system runs a conventional model for flexibility but checks its work with LEAN-style deterministic proofs, drawing on precedent like France's CATALA project that codified its tax code into executable rules. The bet for founders is that reliability, not raw capability, is what unblocks enterprise deployment in domains where a wrong answer costs money, health, or freedom.

Uneven Frontiers (1 minute read)

AI is transforming pharma unevenly, and the sequence is what decides where value lands. Discovery has become software-like and the pipeline of drug candidates has roughly doubled over the past decade, but approvals have stayed flat at around 50 a year because clinical development still moves at the speed of the physical world. The durable position sits around that bottleneck, not in the path of discovery models that each frontier release can absorb. Build where the constraint is, because that is the edge a better model can't quietly erase.

Dear SaaStr:  My Top Sales Rep Made $600,000 a Year — And Just Quit!  What Happened? (1 minute read)

Your top sales rep usually quits because of change, not money. A new VP of Sales arrives, the old one who sheltered them leaves, or next year's number simply looks harder to hit, and they bolt even when it means starting over for less. The rep thrived in the current environment and rarely reads change as good for them. You can't fully control it, but you can align any incoming VP with your top AE and pull that rep into the hiring process before the new boss lands.

People prefer products with fewer ingredients (1 minute read)

Most "organic" and "natural" claims now blur together because everyone uses them, but a simpler signal cuts through: telling buyers your product has "just a few" ingredients rather than "many" makes them up to 22% more likely to choose it. The mechanism is perceived purity, not actual ingredient count, so the win is in the framing of what you already have. If your product genuinely runs lean on ingredients, say so explicitly in the copy instead of leaning on the crowded organic and natural language.
