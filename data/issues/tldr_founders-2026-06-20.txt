Headlines & Trends

Noam Shazeer Joins OpenAI (1 minute read)

Noam Shazeer, co-author of the original Transformer paper and most recently a co-lead on Google's Gemini models, announced he is leaving Google to join OpenAI. His exit removes one of the most senior architects from the team building Google's frontier models and hands OpenAI a researcher whose work underpins nearly every modern LLM. The move is the latest in an escalating talent war where the labs are now poaching each other's most foundational people, not just their engineers.

Docs: OpenAI burned through $3.7B in Q1, on revenue of $5.7B, and ended the quarter with $73B+ in cash and marketable securities vs. $40B at the end of December (Erin Woo/The Information) (1 minute read)

OpenAI burned through $3.7B in Q1 against $5.7B in revenue, yet its cash and marketable securities climbed to more than $73B from $40B at the end of December. The gap is fresh financing, not operating strength. The takeaway for founders is that frontier-scale AI now runs on a balance sheet topped up faster than it drains, and the burn keeps pace with revenue because compute capex scales with every new model and user.

Companies rein in AI usage as costs strain budgets (1 minute read)

Companies that rushed AI into everything are now pulling it back as the bills come due, with usage-based pricing turning every query into a line item that finance can't ignore. The pattern is the same across teams: frontier models wired into workflows that don't need them, costs scaling with adoption instead of value. The strain is forcing a reckoning on where AI actually earns its spend, and the budgets are tightening faster than the deployments can prove themselves.

Google Gemini co-lead Noam Shazeer leaves for OpenAI (1 minute read)

Noam Shazeer, Google's VP of engineering and a co-lead on Gemini, is leaving for OpenAI less than two years after Google brought him back as part of its Character.AI deal. The move strips Google of one of its most senior model architects weeks after it shipped Gemini 3.5 Flash, and it lands as OpenAI confidentially files for an IPO. The talent war is now the main front in the model race, and the labs are paying whatever it takes to pull the handful of people who can actually push a frontier model forward.

Midjourney Medical (1 minute read)

Midjourney, the bootstrapped AI image generator, launched a medical division built around a full-body Ultrasonic CT scanner that images the body in 60 seconds using sound waves and water instead of radiation. The company plans to open its first Midjourney Spa in San Francisco in 2027 and scale to 50,000 scanners running a billion scans a month by 2031. The bet is that a generative-imaging company can carry its core competency from pixels into physical hardware and a regulated health market, funded off its own profits rather than venture money.

Early Users of Anthropic Mythos Still Have Access After US Order (1 minute read)

A subset of firms chosen by Anthropic to preview its Mythos model kept access even after a US government order forced the broader shutdown of every other version. Roughly 200 organizations, including banks, Cisco, and Dragos, are still running Mythos Preview through Project Glasswing, a cleared collective using the system to hunt for cyber vulnerabilities. The split shows how frontier access is increasingly governed by security carve-outs and government clearance, not just who is willing to pay for the model.

AI inference startup Baseten reportedly raising $1.5B months after its last mega-round (1 minute read)

Baseten is close to a $1.5B round at a $13B valuation, the WSJ reports, up 160% from the $5B it carried just five months ago. The fine print matters more than the headline: it is a split-priced round, with some investors coming in at $13B and others at $11B, a tactic that inflates the marquee number while making lead investors look good on paper. Baseten sits in the middle of the inference gold rush, routing requests to the cheapest competent model, and capital is chasing that layer faster than the businesses underneath it are maturing.

From $5B to $13B in five months: Baseten reportedly closing in on $1.5B raise (1 minute read)

Baseten is reportedly closing in on a $1.5B round at up to a $13B valuation, less than three times its $5B mark from five months ago and roughly six times its valuation a year ago. The capital is chasing inference, not models, with the company citing estimates that inference will hit two-thirds of all AI compute by the end of 2026, up from a third in 2023. The deal is split-priced, some investors entering at an $11B floor and others paying the full $13B headline, a structure late-stage AI companies are using to raise large sums fast.

What Link data tells us about AI spending (1 minute read)

Stripe analyzed spending across the 250 million customers paying with Link and found AI spend is accelerating sharply. The top 10% of AI spenders nearly doubled their monthly outlay from $183 in December 2025 to $359 in March 2026, a jump that took just three months after the prior climb from $84 to $183 took 22. The clearest signal is where the money goes: that same cohort now spends 5x more on app-builder platforms like Replit, Lovable, and Bolt than it did in January 2025.

OpenAI is bringing on some big guns in the lead-up to its IPO (1 minute read)

OpenAI is stacking its bench ahead of an IPO, hiring Transformer co-author Noam Shazeer from Google DeepMind and former White House AI policy official Dean Ball. Shazeer adds frontier research weight, while Ball will run a new Strategic Futures team shaping policy on catastrophic risk, recursive self-improvement, and lab-government relations. The pattern is the same one playing out across Google, Anthropic, and Meta: the labs are competing as hard for foundational researchers and policy operators as they are for compute, and OpenAI is loading up on both right before going public.

Every fusion startup that has raised over $100M (1 minute read)

Private fusion has crossed from punchline to fundable, with multiple startups now past $100M raised on the back of better chips, AI-driven reactor simulations, and high-temperature superconducting magnets. Commonwealth Fusion Systems alone holds about a third of all private fusion capital, closing an $863M round in August to bring its total near $3B as it builds Sparc in Massachusetts. The science cleared scientific breakeven at a U.S. lab in 2022, but commercial breakeven, where a plant outputs more than the whole facility draws, is still the bet investors are funding.

Paris-based Kyber, which develops a low-latency remote device control SDK and is founded by VLC lead developer Jean-Baptiste Kempf, raised $5M led by Lightspeed (Anna Heim/TechCrunch) (1 minute read)

Kyber, a Paris-based startup building a low-latency SDK for remote device control, raised $5M led by Lightspeed. Founder Jean-Baptiste Kempf is the lead developer behind VLC, the open-source media player installed billions of times, and that track record in squeezing latency out of streaming is the core bet here. The round signals investor appetite for infrastructure that makes controlling hardware over a network feel local, a primitive that cloud gaming, robotics, and remote workstations all depend on.

Microsoft, Amazon Cloud Services Face Tough EU Antitrust Law (1 minute read)

The EU is set to name AWS and Azure as gatekeepers under the Digital Markets Act, with the European Commission expected to share preliminary findings from its cloud probe as early as next week. That designation brings hard obligations on interoperability, data portability, and self-preferencing, aimed squarely at the lock-in that keeps enterprise workloads stuck on one provider. For founders building on top of the hyperscalers, the practical upside is easier multi-cloud and lower switching costs, but the near-term reality is years of legal fights before any of those rules actually bite.

AI data centers just got a government-mandated fast lane to the grid (1 minute read)

FERC unanimously ordered six major grid operators to fast-track data center interconnection requests, with the data centers footing the connection costs and grid tech startups getting an opening through a directive to consider alternative transmission technologies. The orders speed access but don't touch the actual bottleneck, since generating capacity is short and data center demand is expected to nearly triple through 2035. The connection problem is already showing up in prices, with wholesale electricity rates up as much as 267% over five years, so a faster lane onto a strained grid mostly moves the queue, not the constraint.

How Attention.com Turns Sales Calls Into Pipeline: The Best GTM Data You Own, and Why Most B2B Teams Throw It Away (1 minute read)

Your next quarter's pipeline is already sitting in the sales conversations you had this quarter, and most B2B teams never mine it. Attention.com's playbook runs an agent monthly over recent closed-won deals, extracts buyer function, tenure, company size, and firmographics, then feeds those personas into an enrichment tool to build a dozen specific buyer pockets instead of one broad ICP. The shift that matters is cadence: an annual ICP exercise is a 2021 habit, and the teams compounding fastest refresh it off live closed-won data every month or quarter.

6 Key Signs a VP of Sales Can’t Scale Beyond $5m-$10m ARR (1 minute read)

The clearest tell is when a VP threatens to quit at the mention of a CRO above them, because the leaders who actually scale know that hire is coming and say the same thing to their own reports.

Artisan’s Ava 2.0: What a Fully Autonomous AI BDR Actually Looks Like in Production with CEO Jaspar Carmichael-Jack (1 minute read)

Artisan's pitch for Ava, its autonomous AI BDR, is accountability rather than automation: instead of a taped-together stack of sequencer, enrichment, and copywriting tools where no single piece owns the outcome, the UI rolls every campaign up to one number, the credits you paid per positive reply or booked meeting. The enormous size of the oil and gas market drives adoption of synthetic fuel production (1 minute read)

New technology only scales when two things line up: a beachhead market willing to pay a premium while the tech is immature, and a learning rate that drops cost at least as fast as that beachhead gets exhausted. Tesla ran the playbook from Roadster to Model 3, and synthetic fuel follows the same path, except the target is the enormous oil and gas market that makes early production profitable far sooner. Wright's Law, not one giant plant, is what actually pushes the cost down.
