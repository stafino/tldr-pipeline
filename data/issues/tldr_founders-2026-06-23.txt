Google plans a 12-week incubator, picking 10 to 20 AI startups from its "Xoogler" alumni and providing up to $350K in cloud credits and $100K in direct funding (Guinevere Grant/Bloomberg) (1 minute read)

Google is launching a 12-week incubator for AI startups founded by its own alumni, picking 10 to 20 "Xoogler" teams and backing each with up to $350K in cloud credits and $100K in direct funding. The credits dwarf the cash, which tells you what Google is actually buying: early lock-in to its cloud and model stack from the founders most likely to build the next wave. For operators, it is another sign that the frontier labs increasingly compete for startups as a distribution channel, not just as customers.

Memory Chips and China, Microsoft and Chinese Models (1 minute read)

The big three memory makers may come to regret letting Chinese rivals through the door, the same pattern that hollowed out incumbents in solar and displays once domestic capacity scaled. Microsoft, meanwhile, has every incentive to run Chinese open models, which are now close enough on quality and far cheaper to serve at the scale of its own products. The throughline is that China keeps moving up the stack, and the Western firms supplying or competing with it are starting to feed the thing that undercuts them.

How Sam Altman's 80+ personal investments, many from his time at YC, benefit from ties to OpenAI; 10+ companies have discussed business deals with OpenAI (Wall Street Journal) (1 minute read)

Sam Altman holds personal stakes in more than 80 startups, many seeded during his YC years, and several have spiked in value after OpenAI explored or signed deals with them. His position in fusion company Helion and others rose as OpenAI pursued tie-ups with Altman-linked startups, and at least 10 companies he has backed have discussed business deals with the lab. When the buyer's CEO is also an investor in the seller, the line between OpenAI's strategy and Altman's portfolio gets hard to read.
