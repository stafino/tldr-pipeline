The PIK Fuse: How Private Equity Software Deals Like Medallia Actually Blow Up, and Exactly Who’s Next (1 minute read)

Private equity loaded a dozen software leaders with debt at the 2021 peak, and the PIK toggle is what hides the damage until it's terminal. Payment-in-kind lets a borrower defer cash interest and roll it onto the principal, so the balance compounds while the business weakens and covenant tests still pass right up until the deferral window closes. Thoma Bravo just handed Medallia to its lenders, wiping out $5.1B in equity on a company earning $200M a year against $300M in debt service. It is the second giant wipeout in 18 months, and the fuse runs the same way every time.

Sources: Qualcomm is in advanced talks to acquire Modular in a transaction valuing the AI chip startup at ~$4B, up from a $1.6B valuation in September 2025 (Bloomberg) (1 minute read)

Qualcomm is in advanced talks to acquire AI chip startup Modular at roughly $4B, more than double its $1.6B valuation from September 2025. The deal signals how aggressively non-Nvidia players are paying up for the software and hardware layer that makes AI inference cheaper to run at scale. For founders, it is another sign that the compute stack is consolidating fast, and the independent tooling you build on today may belong to a chipmaker by next year.

AI chipmaker Groq confirms $650M raise, re-staffs after Nvidia’s $20B not-acqui-hire deal (1 minute read)

Six months after Nvidia licensed Groq's chip IP and hired away its CEO and top executives in a $20B not-acqui-hire, Groq confirmed a $650M round led by Disruptive and Infinitum. Rather than wind down, the company pivoted to its neocloud business, which now runs 13 data centers and serves over five million developers, and restocked its bench with a new COO, CTO, and CPO. The not-acqui-hire is becoming a recurring exit shape, where a rival buys the IP and the talent while investors cash out, and the open question is whether the husk left behind can rebuild into a real business.

Qualcomm Nears Deal for AI Chip Startup Modular (1 minute read)

Qualcomm is in advanced talks to buy AI chip startup Modular at a roughly $4B valuation, pushing the mobile-chip giant deeper into the data center fight against Nvidia. Modular's pitch was always software, a unified layer meant to run AI workloads across any hardware, which is exactly what Qualcomm lacks as it tries to sell inference silicon. The deal signals that the next round of competition is less about raw chips and more about owning the software stack that decides where AI runs.

Air Space Intelligence won an $875M, 12-year FAA contract to develop AI tools that map flight trajectories and identify areas of congestion to reduce delays (Allyson Versprille/Bloomberg) (1 minute read)

Air Space Intelligence won an $875M, 12-year FAA contract to build AI that maps flight trajectories and flags congestion before it turns into delays. The deal is one of the largest federal bets yet on applied AI inside legacy infrastructure, and it signals that agencies are now willing to write multi-year, nine-figure checks for software that optimizes systems they already run rather than replacing them. For founders selling into government, the path is widening for startups that target a specific operational bottleneck instead of pitching a sweeping platform.

Sources: marketing tech startup AppsFlyer raised a $1B Series E at a $2.7B post-money valuation; Moloco, Google, Meta, and Unity acquire minority stakes (Kerry Flynn/Axios) (1 minute read)

AppsFlyer raised a $1B Series E at a $2.7B post-money valuation, with Moloco, Google, Meta, and Unity each taking minority stakes. The investor list is the real signal: the same ad platforms AppsFlyer measures are now on its cap table, betting on neutral attribution as signal loss from privacy changes makes independent measurement harder to fake. For founders in martech, it shows where the money is going as the post-cookie, post-IDFA scramble pushes measurement back toward third parties the big platforms can't fully see.

SpaceX signs a computing deal worth up to $6.3B with Reflection AI for access to Nvidia GB300s at Colossus 2; Reflection will pay $150M per month through 2029 (Deirdre Bosa/CNBC) (1 minute read)

Reflection AI committed up to $6.3B to SpaceX for compute, paying $150M per month through 2029 to run on Nvidia GB300s at Colossus 2. The deal locks one AI lab into a four-year, fixed-cost compute contract before its own revenue can plausibly cover it, a bet that model demand keeps compounding faster than the bill. The signal for founders is that frontier compute is now sold like infrastructure leases, with multi-year minimums and counterparties that also happen to be building rival hardware empires.

Oracle Cut 21,000 Jobs in 12 Months, Says AI Replaced Some Roles (1 minute read)

Oracle cut 21,000 jobs over the past 12 months, a deeper reduction than previously reported, and named AI as the reason some of those roles no longer exist. The detail that matters is the explicit attribution: a legacy enterprise vendor is now stating on the record that automation, not just restructuring, is shrinking headcount. Expect more incumbents to frame layoffs the same way, both because it's increasingly true and because "AI did it" reads better to investors than admitting the core business is slowing.

Google DeepMind bets $75M on AI’s future in Hollywood with A24 deal (1 minute read)

Google DeepMind invested $75M in indie studio A24 to co-develop AI filmmaking tools, with Demis Hassabis framing it as a first-of-its-kind partnership that gives DeepMind direct feedback from working artists. A24, the studio behind "Everything Everywhere All At Once" and "Backrooms," joins a pattern already set by Netflix buying Affleck's InterPositive and Amazon's MGM standing up an AI production unit. The frontier labs are no longer just selling models to Hollywood, they are buying their way into the creative process to source the proprietary taste and feedback data that generic models lack.

AI-Services Startup Baseten Nabs $13 Billion Valuation (1 minute read)

Baseten raised $1.5B in a Series F at a $13B valuation, co-led by Altimeter, to sell software and compute capacity to companies running lower-cost AI models. The bet is that as open and cheaper models close the gap with frontier ones, the money moves to whoever makes them easy to deploy and serve in production. Inference infrastructure is becoming its own venture category, separate from the labs, because the teams adopting AI increasingly want the tooling layer rather than another model to pick from.

Inference chip startup Groq raises $650M to grow its cloud platform (1 minute read)

Groq raised $650M to expand its inference cloud, seven months after licensing its LPU chip technology to Nvidia in a $20B deal that also took its founding CEO. The bet is that the value sits in operating the inference, not owning the silicon: Groq's platform now runs trillions of tokens a week for 5 million developers across 13 data centers. The proceeds go toward scaling capacity to 200 megawatts by 2027, even as Nvidia ships the same LPU 3 hardware and rival clouds can theoretically stand up competing services.

The minimum viable unit of saleable software (1 minute read)

LLMs flipped the buy-vs-build calculus, so the cheap internal package that replaces your $400/mo SaaS is now a real threat to anyone selling software. But cheap isn't zero, and the author's point is that LLM-built systems still demand dozens of feedback loops to get right plus an open-ended maintenance bill for every new feature and bug. The opening for saleable software is the work LLMs make easier but not free, where the ongoing cost of owning the thing yourself still outweighs the price of buying it.
