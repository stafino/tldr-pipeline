The PIK Fuse: How Private Equity Software Deals Like Medallia Actually Blow Up, and Exactly Who’s Next (1 minute read)

Private equity loaded a dozen software leaders with debt at the 2021 peak, and the PIK toggle is what hides the damage until it's terminal. Payment-in-kind lets a borrower defer cash interest and roll it onto the principal, so the balance compounds while the business weakens and covenant tests still pass right up until the deferral window closes. Thoma Bravo just handed Medallia to its lenders, wiping out $5.1B in equity on a company earning $200M a year against $300M in debt service. It is the second giant wipeout in 18 months, and the fuse runs the same way every time.

AI chipmaker Groq confirms $650M raise, re-staffs after Nvidia’s $20B not-acqui-hire deal (1 minute read)

Six months after Nvidia licensed Groq's chip IP and hired away its CEO and top executives in a $20B not-acqui-hire, Groq confirmed a $650M round led by Disruptive and Infinitum. Rather than wind down, the company pivoted to its neocloud business, which now runs 13 data centers and serves over five million developers, and restocked its bench with a new COO, CTO, and CPO. The not-acqui-hire is becoming a recurring exit shape, where a rival buys the IP and the talent while investors cash out, and the open question is whether the husk left behind can rebuild into a real business.

Oracle cut 21,000 jobs this year and its SEC filing explicitly blames AI (1 minute read)

Oracle's headcount fell to 141,000 from 162,000 over the past year, a net cut of roughly 21,000, and its annual SEC filing states plainly that AI adoption "have resulted, and may continue to result, in reductions to our workforce." It is a rare case of a major tech company putting the AI-replaces-jobs argument into a securities disclosure rather than an earnings call, where lawyers are comfortable telling regulators what most CEOs only imply. The deepest cuts hit Oracle Health and legacy SaaS teams, while Cloud Infrastructure and AI roles were spared or expanded.

Inference chip startup Groq raises $650M to grow its cloud platform (1 minute read)

Groq raised $650M to expand its inference cloud, seven months after licensing its LPU chip technology to Nvidia in a $20B deal that also took its founding CEO. The bet is that the value sits in operating the inference, not owning the silicon: Groq's platform now runs trillions of tokens a week for 5 million developers across 13 data centers. The proceeds go toward scaling capacity to 200 megawatts by 2027, even as Nvidia ships the same LPU 3 hardware and rival clouds can theoretically stand up competing services.

SpaceX Strikes $6 Billion Deal With AI Startup for Data-Center Space (1 minute read)

SpaceX signed a $6B deal to rent data-center capacity to Reflection AI, which will use it to train open-source models. The move puts a rocket company in the compute-landlord business and gives a young lab access to scale without building its own infrastructure. It is another sign that the AI buildout is being financed through long-term capacity contracts, where whoever controls power and data-center space increasingly sets the terms for the labs renting it.

The minimum viable unit of saleable software (1 minute read)

LLMs flipped the buy-vs-build calculus, so the cheap internal package that replaces your $400/mo SaaS is now a real threat to anyone selling software. But cheap isn't zero, and the author's point is that LLM-built systems still demand dozens of feedback loops to get right plus an open-ended maintenance bill for every new feature and bug. The opening for saleable software is the work LLMs make easier but not free, where the ongoing cost of owning the thing yourself still outweighs the price of buying it.

7 Mistakes Founders Make When Chasing Their First Deals (1 minute read)

Most early founders stall on the same two mistakes: waiting for a perfect product before selling, and pricing themselves into irrelevance. Selling is a contact sport, so the faster move is to ship a hypothesis about a real problem and let customer conversations correct your aim, because early signals are what get you to product-market fit. On price, charging one or two thousand a month is pocket change to a serious buyer. If the number is that small, run it as a design partnership and charge real money once you have proof.

GEO is following the same path as early SEO (1 minute read)

GEO, the practice of getting your brand mentioned in AI-generated answers, is running the same playbook early SEO did, and the same shakeout is coming. The durable tactics are the boring ones: high-quality content that answers real prompts, FAQs, TL;DR summaries, question-based headlines, and clean schema. The black hat shortcuts that work right now, like AI spam, fake reviews to game E-E-A-T, and cloaking content for crawlers, are the ones SEO history says get penalized out of existence. Build for the version of GEO that survives the cleanup, not the gold rush that funds it today.
