Headlines & Trends

Adobe Just Deferred a Big Annual Price Increase. It’s the First Big Crack in B2B Pricing Power Since 2022. (1 minute read)

Adobe just deferred roughly $500M ARR of Creative Cloud price increases it had already planned for the back half of FY2026, breaking a decade-long habit of raising prices like clockwork. For four years the B2B playbook was identical: hike every 12 to 18 months, bolt on an AI SKU, push net revenue retention above 110%, and customers paid because they had nowhere to go. When one of software's most pricing-powerful companies voluntarily walks away from a planned hike, the signal isn't generosity. It's the first real crack in B2B pricing power since 2022.

Qualcomm says it will acquire Modular, which builds a chip software platform and has a proprietary coding language, in a nearly $4B deal set to close in H2 2026 (Lauren Goode/Wired) (1 minute read)

Qualcomm agreed to acquire Modular, the AI chip-software startup behind the Mojo language, for nearly $4B, with the deal expected to close in H2 2026. The bet is on software, not silicon: Modular's platform abstracts away the hardware so developers can run AI workloads without rewriting for each chip, which is the layer Nvidia's CUDA has locked up for years. Qualcomm is paying a steep price to buy a credible path into the AI compute stack rather than build one, a sign of how much the data-center fight now turns on the developer toolchain.

OpenAI unveils its first custom chip, built by Broadcom (1 minute read)

OpenAI released Jalapeño, its first custom inference chip, co-designed with Broadcom and partly developed with OpenAI's own models. The play is margin: the chip targets the cost of running real-time models like Codex, where early tests show better performance-per-watt than current alternatives, while pre-training likely stays on Nvidia. It puts OpenAI on the same path as Google and Amazon, owning every layer from chip to product so inference economics stop depending on a single supplier. For startups built on OpenAI's API, cheaper inference upstream is the lever that eventually moves their own unit costs.

OpenAI reveals its first AI processor: Jalapeño (1 minute read)

OpenAI revealed Jalapeño, its first custom inference chip, built with Broadcom and aimed at cutting the company's dependence on scarce Nvidia GPUs. Broadcom's CEO says it matches Nvidia's Blackwell and Google's TPUs on performance, with early testing showing substantially better performance per watt, and OpenAI expects to deploy it by the end of 2026. The frontier labs are now vertically integrating down to silicon, which over time loosens Nvidia's pricing grip and reshapes the compute capex math that every AI startup ultimately pays for.

RunPod, which rents access to non-Nvidia servers, raised $100M led by Summit Partners at a $1B valuation, a source says up from $100M after its seed in 2024 (Stephanie Palazzolo/The Information) (1 minute read)

RunPod raised $100M led by Summit Partners at a $1B valuation, up from a $100M valuation after its 2024 seed, a tenfold jump in under two years. The company rents GPU compute on non-Nvidia hardware, betting that founders training and serving models care more about price and availability than which chip is underneath. The signal for operators is that the compute bottleneck is pushing capital toward anyone who can offer cheaper alternatives to Nvidia, and that demand is now strong enough to mint a unicorn out of arbitraging it.

OpenAI, Broadcom Unveil Chip to Run Models Faster, Cheaper (1 minute read)

OpenAI got its first samples of Jalapeno, a custom AI chip built with Broadcom, and is now testing how it runs the company's own workloads. Early results show roughly 50% cost savings versus the GPUs everyone else rents from Nvidia, according to Broadcom CEO Hock Tan. The frontier labs are moving down the stack into their own silicon, and if the savings hold at scale, OpenAI's cost-per-token advantage becomes a moat that anyone still paying GPU rates cannot match.

Qualcomm to Buy Modular for $3.9 Billion to Help AI Push (1 minute read)

Qualcomm is acquiring Modular for about $3.9 billion in stock, adding AI software to support its move into the data center market. The deal hands Modular's owners 19.2 million shares and is set to close in the second half of 2026. Modular's pitch was always portable inference that runs across different chips, so the buyer is a mobile-chip company trying to break Nvidia's grip on AI infrastructure by pairing its silicon with a software layer that already abstracts away the hardware underneath.

‘Digit’ maker Agility Robotics to go public in $2.5B deal — here’s what the filings say about its finances (1 minute read)

Agility Robotics is taking its Digit humanoid public through a $2.5B SPAC merger with Churchill Capital Corp XI, becoming the first US-listed company built solely around humanoid robots. The filings show why the timing matters more than the numbers: Agility doesn't disclose revenue, remains unprofitable, burned roughly $100M in cash last year, and saw operating expenses climb to about $111M from $71M. Its headline $300M in "committed" Digit v5 orders comes with fine print that it isn't current revenue and hinges on a single undisclosed customer. The SPAC reload is funding humanoid bets long before the unit economics are proven.

PayPal Put Agentforce on 8,000 Leads a Month No Human Was Going to Call. Conversions Jumped 50%. (1 minute read)

PayPal pointed an Agentforce SDR agent at the 8,000 merchants a month its 200-rep org was never going to call, the ones who stalled partway through onboarding, and meeting conversion is now running about 50% higher than reps managed alone. The agent runs a 10-nudge cadence on every lead and books the meeting, then hands the rep a qualified prospect with full context loaded. The lesson for operators is to stop waiting on clean CRM data and aim agents at the leads you already drop, where anything beats zero coverage.
