The Minimum Viable Unit of Saleable Software (1 minute read)

LLMs flipped the buy-vs-build calculus, so the cheap internal package that replaces your $400/mo SaaS is now a real threat to anyone selling software. But cheap isn't zero, and the author's point is that LLM-built systems still demand dozens of feedback loops to get right plus an open-ended maintenance bill for every new feature and bug. The opening for saleable software is the work LLMs make easier but not free, where the ongoing cost of owning the thing yourself still outweighs the price of buying it.

7 Mistakes Founders Make When Chasing Their First Deals (1 minute read)

Most early founders stall on the same two mistakes: waiting for a perfect product before selling, and pricing themselves into irrelevance. Selling is a contact sport, so the faster move is to ship a hypothesis about a real problem and let customer conversations correct your aim, because early signals are what get you to product-market fit. On price, charging one or two thousand a month is pocket change to a serious buyer. If the number is that small, run it as a design partnership and charge real money once you have proof.

GEO is following the same path as early SEO (1 minute read)

GEO, the practice of getting your brand mentioned in AI-generated answers, is running the same playbook early SEO did, and the same shakeout is coming. The durable tactics are the boring ones: high-quality content that answers real prompts, FAQs, TL;DR summaries, question-based headlines, and clean schema. The black hat shortcuts that work right now, like AI spam, fake reviews to game E-E-A-T, and cloaking content for crawlers, are the ones SEO history says get penalized out of existence. Build for the version of GEO that survives the cleanup, not the gold rush that funds it today.

Algorithmic Monocultures in Hiring (1 minute read)

Ninety percent of US employers now run candidates through AI screening tools, and most rely on the same few third-party vendors. A new Stanford study following 3.4 million applicants found that when one algorithm screens for many employers, the same people get rejected everywhere they apply, and 40,000 more Black and Asian applications would have advanced if the AI recommended them at the top group's rate. The monoculture turns one vendor's bias into a systemic lockout.
