Anthropic accuses Alibaba of campaign to 'brazenly' and 'illicitly' extract AI capabilities (1 minute read)

Anthropic told the Senate Banking Committee that Alibaba ran the largest known distillation attack against its models to date, using roughly 25,000 fraudulent accounts to pull 28.8 million exchanges between April 22 and June 5. Distillation lets a weaker model train on a stronger one's outputs, so the cost of catching up keeps falling for whoever can scrape enough responses. For founders building on frontier APIs, the takeaway is that your model's outputs are now a contested asset, and the fight over who can copy them is moving from blog posts to Congress and export controls.

The engineer who built Swift and LLVM sold his AI startup to Qualcomm for $4B (1 minute read)

Qualcomm is acquiring Modular, the AI infrastructure startup co-founded by Chris Lattner, in an all-stock deal worth about $3.92B, doubling its valuation from $1.6B just nine months ago. Modular's pitch is hardware-agnostic AI software that lets developers write an app once and run it across Nvidia, AMD, Apple, and Qualcomm chips without rewriting their stack, and it already counts Oracle, Amazon, Nvidia, and AMD as customers. The target is the CUDA software lock-in that keeps Nvidia almost impossible to displace, and Qualcomm just bought the most credible attempt to break it.

AI Revenue Starts to Exceed a Key Infrastructure Cost But Not By Much (1 minute read)

AI revenue at the biggest cloud players has finally crossed $25B, edging past the depreciation on the infrastructure built to generate it, but the margin is thin. For Meta, Alphabet, and Microsoft the numbers now technically work, while Google and Amazon still need sustained premium pricing and strong user growth to recoup what they've spent. The takeaway is that the capex bet is no longer underwater, but it isn't paying off either, and the gap between spending and return stays narrow enough that any slip in demand flips the math back to red.

Sail, whose software optimizes how AI models run on existing chips, emerges from stealth with $80M in seed and Series A led by Kleiner at a $450M valuation (Lily Mae Lazarus/Fortune) (1 minute read)

Sail emerged from stealth with $80M in combined seed and Series A led by Kleiner Perkins at a $450M valuation. Its software squeezes more performance out of AI models on the chips companies already own, rather than chasing scarce new hardware. That points at a quieter shift in the AI buildout: with GPU supply tight and compute capex ballooning, the cheapest path to more capacity is increasingly software that wrings more out of the silicon already racked, not another order with Nvidia.

The AI era requires a different kind of experimentation. (1 minute read)

Your 2022 experimentation playbook optimized for minor tweaks, slow rollouts, and two-week wins, mostly pulling forward revenue that would have arrived anyway rather than driving step-function change. The author argues AI has collapsed the build cost that justified that caution, so the smart play is to run fewer cosmetic tests and aim experiments at foundational complexity and monetization, the bets that were too slow to attempt before. Booking.com for Business Shipped a Free Expense Tool in 6 Weeks. Don’t Copy the Tool, Copy the Sequence. (1 minute read)

Booking.com for Business shipped a free expense tool in six weeks by inverting the usual order: instead of asking what AI can do, they hunted for where customers hit friction. In B2B that matters because friction is where churn lives, the moments users stall out and leave, so the admin wrapping a trip, reconstructing a $42 receipt three weeks later, was a bigger pain than booking the trip itself. The playbook to copy isn't the expense product, it's the sequence of finding the friction and building straight at it.
