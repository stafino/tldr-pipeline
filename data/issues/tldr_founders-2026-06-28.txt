An analysis of US payroll data across 730+ occupations: employment among workers ages 22 to 25 in highly AI-exposed jobs is now shrinking by 3.8% per year (Nick Lichtenberg/Fortune) (1 minute read)

Entry-level hiring is where AI is showing up first in the labor data. An analysis of US payroll records across more than 730 occupations found that employment among workers ages 22 to 25 in highly AI-exposed jobs is now shrinking by 3.8% per year, even as older and less-exposed cohorts hold steady. The squeeze lands on the cheapest, most automatable rung of the workforce, which is also the one founders have always leaned on to staff junior roles cheaply.

California will tax downloaded software for the first time as part of a $351.7 billion budget deal (1 minute read)

California's new $351.7B budget extends sales tax to downloaded software for the first time, a change expected to raise about $2B a year, $900M for the state and $1.1B for local governments, starting in fiscal 2028. The same deal puts a measure on the November ballot to let the state bank more of the windfall from AI IPOs like OpenAI's and Anthropic's, since its progressive tax base swings hard with the market. For any software business operating in California, a line item that was untaxed for years is about to carry sales tax.

The BIS warns an AI bust could hit credit markets as hard as the 2008 financial crisis (1 minute read)

The BIS, the central bank for central banks, warned in its annual report that an AI investment bust could disrupt credit markets as severely as the 2008 financial crisis. The specific vulnerability it named is circular financing, where chipmakers take equity stakes in AI labs that then commit to buying those same chips, with deal terms so poorly disclosed that the same asset can be pledged multiple times. If returns disappoint and financing pulls back, the capex boom flips into a protracted bust, and the leverage hiding inside these arrangements becomes everyone's problem.

OpenAI Codex lead on the new shape of product work | Andrew Ambrosino (1 minute read)

When everyone on your team can build anything, the bottleneck stops being who can ship and becomes who has the taste to know what's worth shipping. Codex's desktop lead at OpenAI argues AI has inverted product development, collapsing the old engineer-designer-PM roles into a single build function while making judgment the scarce skill. His "zone defense" model for PMs is the practical answer: when anyone can produce the work, the job is owning a problem area and steering it, not guarding a discipline. Nearly 100% of OpenAI employees, not just engineers, now use Codex weekly.
