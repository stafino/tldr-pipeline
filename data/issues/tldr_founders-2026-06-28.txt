A two-year-old robotics startup with about thirty million dollars in revenue was just valued at more than fourteen billion, which is the clearest sign yet that the AI money has decided robots are next and that reliability can come later (1 minute read)

Skild AI, a two-year-old robotics startup with roughly $30M in revenue, raised close to $1.4B in a SoftBank-led round that values it above $14B, several hundred times sales for a product that doesn't yet ship. The bet is physical AI: pretrain one general model to control almost any robot, the way LLMs did for text. The catch is there's no internet of robot movement to learn from, so Skild trains on human videos and simulations that only approximate the friction and failure a machine hits in a real room. The capital has decided robots are next and reliability can come later.

Masayoshi Son questioned Musk's orbital AI data centers, noting electricity is just 7% of costs and the AI race will be won on Earth within a few years (Tim Higgins/Wall Street Journal) (1 minute read)

Masayoshi Son pushed back on Elon Musk's plan to build AI data centers in orbit, arguing that electricity is only about 7% of the cost of running them. His bet is that the AI race gets decided on Earth within a few years, well before space-based compute could matter. The disagreement is really about where the binding constraint sits: if power is a small slice of the bill, then chips, capital, and deployment speed on the ground still set the pace, and exotic infrastructure is a distraction from the fight already underway.

One year at Anthropic, then $200M at $1B: The researchers who just closed one of AI’s largest-ever seed rounds (1 minute read)

Two ex-Anthropic researchers raised $200M at a $1B valuation for Mirendil, one of the largest seed rounds in AI history, despite having no product and barely a year together at Anthropic before leaving in December 2025. The bet is AI that automates AI research itself: designing experiments, tuning models, and iterating with progressively less human input. Andreessen Horowitz and Kleiner Perkins co-led, with NVIDIA participating, on the premise that frontier R&D should reach universities and hospitals, not just the handful of labs that can afford the teams to do it.

Anthropic cleared to release Claude Mythos 5 to over 100 US institutions [U] (1 minute read)

The US government has partially lifted the export control directive that blocked Anthropic two weeks ago, clearing Claude Mythos 5 for release to more than 100 trusted US institutions, including major companies and government agencies. Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick's letter stays silent on Fable 5, which remains unavailable for general use. The frontier is now gated by Washington, with model access granted partner by partner. OpenAI made the same move hours earlier, releasing GPT-5.6 only to a small group of government-approved partners, so founders building on these labs should expect rollout timelines to hinge on policy, not engineering.

OpenAI limits GPT-5.6 rollout after government request, says restrictions shouldn’t be the norm (1 minute read)

OpenAI is limiting GPT-5.6, including its flagship Sol model, to a small group of government-vetted partners after the Trump administration restricted release of all three models in the lineup. The order follows the same playbook used on Anthropic, which pulled Fable 5 entirely after being told to cut foreign-national access, and it stems from an executive order asking labs to submit frontier models for review up to 30 days before launch. OpenAI complied but said this access process shouldn't become the default, warning that vague safety standards could stall launches, hand China an opening, and put billions in infrastructure spend at risk.

Soaring iPad, Xbox Prices Reveal Pain of Memory Chip Mess (1 minute read)

Apple and Microsoft raised prices on Xboxes, Macs, and iPads within a five-hour window Thursday, and the trigger is a memory chip shortage flowing straight into consumer hardware. Memory makers have redirected capacity toward AI data centers, so DRAM and NAND costs are climbing fast enough that device makers can no longer absorb them. If your product depends on commodity memory, the AI compute boom is now your cost problem too, and the squeeze shows up in margins long before it shows up in headlines about chip supply.

Ford rehires ‘gray beard’ engineers after AI falls short (1 minute read)

Ford rehired 350 veteran "gray beard" engineers after its automated quality systems failed to deliver the quality it expected, with executives admitting they wrongly assumed that feeding design requirements into AI would produce a high-quality product. The specialists now hunt for failure points before parts hit the plant floor and train younger staff to reprogram the AI tools, rather than replacing them. The move is on track to cut $1 billion in costs this year, and Ford just took the top mainstream spot in JD Power's Initial Quality Survey.

AI Bust Risks Ripple Effects From Growth to Credit, BIS Says (1 minute read)

The BIS named an AI bust alongside inflation and fiscal stress as the biggest current threats to global prosperity. The concern isn't the technology but the financial plumbing: a sharp reversal in AI spending could spill from growth into credit markets, where the capex boom is increasingly funded by debt. For founders, the read is that AI-linked capital is now systemically coupled, so a correction would tighten funding well beyond the labs and into the broader startup economy.

We Booked 614 Meetings With One Inbound Agent. Your “Contact Us” Form Is Costing You Deals. (1 minute read)

Most B2B sites still route inbound through a contact-us form, where a human round-robins the lead and a rep follows up two or three days later, by which point the prospect has gone cold or talked to three competitors. SaaStr replaced its form with an AI agent that booked 614 meetings at roughly $85K average ticket size across about 2.25 million sessions and 402,000 interactions, run by a three-person team. How HappyFox Closed $1M in Expansion on a $20 AI Agent Spend with CEO Shalin Jain (1 minute read)

Most B2B companies pour money into the top of the funnel while their cheapest growth sits untouched inside their own customer base. HappyFox, a $20M bootstrapped company with four AEs, built an autopilot agent named Rex that reads every support ticket the moment it closes and flags expansion signals that reps see but never route and AEs never read. It closed $1M in expansion revenue on less than $20 in total tokens. The lesson is that proactive expansion was never a data problem, it was a reading problem, and an agent solves it for almost nothing.

Accretion, Dilution, and the Cold Hard Truth of M&A Math (1 minute read)

Most founders celebrate the exit at the handshake, then learn the next morning that how you get paid matters more than the headline number. Stock-funded deals can quietly dilute your remaining ownership, and debt-funded ones load the combined company before the cash clears. The fix is unglamorous: model accretion and dilution per scenario before you sit down, so you can show exactly how each structure hits your ownership and profit. Walking in with that math is the negotiating lever, because the person who can pressure-test every term is the one who doesn't get outmaneuvered by the fine print.
