Headlines & Trends

Jalapeño: OpenAI's new Chip (5 minute read)

OpenAI and Broadcom released Jalapeño, OpenAI's first in-house inference chip, built around its own LLM roadmap and already running GPT-5.3-Codex-Spark on engineering samples in the lab. Early testing shows performance per watt substantially better than current state-of-the-art, and the chip went from design to production in nine months, accelerated by OpenAI's own models. It is the clearest sign yet that OpenAI wants the full stack, from models to products to silicon, with deployment planned at gigawatt scale across multiple generations alongside data center partners.

Humanoid robot maker Apptronik raised ~$1B at a ~$5B valuation, including from Mercedes-Benz, which has a handful of Apptronik's Apollo robots in its factories (Joshua Brustein/Bloomberg) (1 minute read)

Apptronik raised about $1B at a roughly $5B valuation, with Mercedes-Benz among the backers and already running a handful of its Apollo robots in factories. The strategic-investor pattern matters more than the round size: automakers are funding the humanoid makers whose machines they're testing on their own lines, turning customers into capital. Apollo's factory deployments give the valuation something most humanoid startups still lack, which is paying industrial pilots rather than demos.

South Korea, Samsung, and SK Hynix say they plan to invest ~$590B to build a new chip complex, including four chipmaking plants and a chip packaging cluster (Song Jung-a/Financial Times) (1 minute read)

South Korea, Samsung, and SK Hynix plan to invest around $590B to build a new chip complex, including four fabrication plants and a packaging cluster. The scale signals that the next phase of the AI buildout is being fought on memory and advanced packaging, not just logic, with the two companies that dominate HBM doubling down on supply for AI accelerators. For founders building on AI infrastructure, the bet is that compute scarcity is structural, and the countries and suppliers locking in capacity now will set pricing and availability for the next decade.

Shanghai-based Momenta, whose driver-assist tech is used by Toyota, Mercedes, and Audi in China, files for an IPO in Hong Kong, seeking to raise up to $751.1M (Reuters) (1 minute read)

Momenta, the Shanghai-based autonomous-driving firm whose driver-assist stack already ships in Toyota, Mercedes, and Audi cars sold in China, filed for a Hong Kong IPO seeking up to $751.1M. The raise signals that the next wave of self-driving money is flowing to suppliers embedded inside legacy automakers, not just standalone robotaxi bets. For founders selling into hardware OEMs, Momenta is the template: become the default software layer for incumbents racing to ship features they can't build fast enough themselves.

Soaring memory costs are posing existential threats to small electronics makers, amid thin margins, low supply chain leverage, and little room for price hikes (Kif Leswing/CNBC) (1 minute read)

Soaring memory prices are now an existential threat to small electronics makers, who run on thin margins, have little supply chain leverage, and can't pass costs along without losing customers. The AI buildout is pulling DRAM and NAND toward the highest bidders, leaving smaller hardware companies competing for supply against deep-pocketed data center buyers. The squeeze rewards scale and locked-in supplier relationships, so the makers most exposed are the ones least able to absorb a component shock they didn't cause.

Amazon’s Rapid Delivery Push Triggers $15 Billion Rout for Eternal, Swiggy (1 minute read)

Amazon and Walmart's Flipkart are pushing into 10-minute delivery in India, and the threat alone wiped roughly $15B off Eternal and Swiggy, the two startups that built the quick-commerce category. The incumbents that opened the market are now defending it against rivals with deeper capital, broader logistics, and existing customer bases. The lesson for founders is familiar: proving a category works is an invitation for better-funded players to enter, and being first counts for little once the giants decide the margins are worth chasing.

South Korea's Massive AI Investment Push (1 minute read)

South Korea unveiled a 1,350 trillion won ($880 billion) plan in corporate AI investment to lock in its lead in semiconductors and build out domestic AI infrastructure. President Lee Jae Myung framed it as a "national survival strategy" and stood alongside the CEOs of Samsung and SK Hynix to lay out the multi-year roadmap. The bet is that owning the chip and compute layer is what decides who controls the AI stack, and Korea is putting state and corporate capital behind that thesis at a scale few countries can match.

South Korea unveils plans for new AI data centers backed by ~$357.5B from SK Group, GS Group, and Naver, targeting 8.4GW initially and 18.4GW by 2035 (Yi Whan-woo/The Korea Times) (1 minute read)

South Korea is backing a national buildout of AI data centers with roughly $357.5B from SK Group, GS Group, and Naver, targeting 8.4GW of capacity initially and 18.4GW by 2035. The plan puts a single country's industrial conglomerates behind compute capacity at a scale that rivals the hyperscalers, with the state coordinating rather than leaving it to the market. For founders, it signals that frontier compute is becoming national infrastructure, and where you can train and serve models cheaply may soon depend on which government got there first.
