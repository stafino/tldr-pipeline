Crunchbase Data: Q2 Brought The Most Billion-Dollar Startup Exits Since 2021 (1 minute read)

Big startup exits are back, but the story is size, not count. Q2 2026 brought the most billion-dollar venture-backed exits since the 2021 peak, led by SpaceX's record IPO, which hit a $2.1 trillion first-day market cap and raised $75 billion, followed days later by its $60 billion acquisition of Cursor, the priciest purchase of a private startup ever. Deal counts are still well below the 2021 boom, but returns blow past it, and with Anthropic and OpenAI filing confidentially for IPOs that could test the trillion-dollar mark, the size trend has room to run.

Databricks’ Co-Founder Arsalan Tavakoli: Every Software Monopoly Falls in the Next 24 Months (1 minute read)

Databricks co-founder Arsalan Tavakoli-Shiraji says any business with a monopoly today won't have one 12 to 24 months from now, as build costs, the low end, and migrations break pricing power at once. He has unusual visibility here, with Databricks running at a $6.9B revenue run-rate, growing 80%+, and AI products alone past a $1.7B run-rate. His sharper read is that enterprises are token maxing without knowing their true AI ROI, so the opening for founders is to tie AI spend to a clear business outcome rather than just standing up agents.

Supreme Court allows firing of FTC commissioners, ends agency independence (1 minute read)

The Supreme Court ruled 6-3 in Trump v. Slaughter that the president can fire FTC commissioners at will, overturning the 1935 Humphrey's Executor precedent that had shielded independent agencies. Antitrust enforcement, consumer-protection rules, and merger reviews now answer directly to the White House rather than to a bipartisan commission insulated from the president. For founders, that means the agency policing your competitors, scrutinizing your acquisitions, and writing the rules on data and pricing can swing with each administration, so regulatory risk is now a function of who holds the executive branch.

In Q2, there were thirteen $1B+ US venture-backed startup exits, either through acquisition or IPO, the most exits since the 2021 market peak (Joanna Glasner/Crunchbase News) (1 minute read)

US venture-backed startups produced thirteen $1B+ exits in Q2 through acquisition or IPO, the most in a single quarter since the 2021 market peak. The activity signals that the long exit drought, which left more than 1,500 unicorns with nowhere to go, is finally starting to thaw at the top end. For founders and their backers, large liquidity events returning means the capital locked in late-stage paper has a clearer path back to LPs, which tends to loosen funding further down the stack.

Sources: Amazon is weighing using OpenAI's and its own Nova models to cut costs after Anthropic raised prices for using its models in Amazon products (Catherine Perloff/The Information) (1 minute read)

Amazon is weighing OpenAI's and its own Nova models to cut costs after Anthropic raised the price of running its models inside Amazon products. The move signals that even a deep partner and major investor will route around a frontier lab the moment its margins get squeezed. For any startup whose product sits on a single model provider, the lesson is that pricing power flows to whoever owns the cheaper substitute, and the buyer relationship is only as loyal as the next invoice.

Accel backs Pocket’s $11M raise, as the AI hardware startup turns a viral launch into $27M ARR (1 minute read)

Pocket, an AI device that records and summarizes conversations, raised $11M led by Accel after turning a 2024 viral concept video into $27M in annualized revenue. The startup has shipped more than 35,000 units since its October 2025 launch, with monthly growth topping 50% in some months and enterprise customers like DoorDash on board. That traction stands out in a hardware category littered with high-profile flops, and the difference is a narrow, opt-in use case with HIPAA and SOC 2 compliance rather than an always-on ambient gadget chasing a vague promise.

Internal documents: Meta is placing strict limits on how engineers in its applied AI division can use Claude Code and Codex, fearing inadvertent distillation (Jyoti Mann/The Information) (1 minute read)

Meta is restricting how engineers in its applied AI division use Claude Code and Codex, worried that leaning on rival models could inadvertently distill their capabilities into Meta's own systems. The limits land as the company pours billions into catching up at the frontier, yet its own engineers reach for Anthropic and OpenAI tools to ship faster. The signal for founders is that even the best-funded labs quietly depend on competitors' models, and the harder problem is building a workflow that doesn't leak your edge to the vendor underneath it.

Rocket Lab continues buying spree by acquiring satellite company Iridium (1 minute read)

Rocket Lab agreed to acquire satellite operator Iridium for $54 per share, valuing it at $8B and turning the launch company into a full-stack space services player. It is at least the fifth deal in roughly a year, after Motiv, Mynaric, Geost, and a precision component maker. The prize is Iridium's in-orbit constellation and its spectrum holdings, and the move fits a broader consolidation wave that already saw Viasat take Inmarsat and Amazon buy Globalstar for $11.6B.

Arena, the AI leaderboard everyone uses, is now a $100M business (1 minute read)

Arena, the crowdsourced AI leaderboard built on more than 10 million user evaluations, hit $100M in annualized revenue just eight months after launching its commercial service. The money comes from AI Evaluations, a paid analytics product that sells labs and enterprises the post-training performance signal its free community generates, up from $30M when it raised a $150M Series A in January. It competes for the same post-training dollar as human-labeling shops like Mercor, Surge, and Scale, a market where appetite is surging as labs chase every last point of model performance.

Beyond automation: How much does AI really cost? (3 minute read)

The hidden line item in AI budgets isn't your power users, it's the high-frequency automated tasks running quietly in the background. One enterprise spent $500M on Claude in a single month because nobody set usage limits before rollout, Uber burned its entire 2026 AI budget before midyear, and JPMorgan published a report titled "AI Token Costs Are Eating into Internet Profits." The teams that avoided the surprise modeled token cost per workflow before finalizing architecture, treating cost as tokens times frequency times users rather than a function of how sophisticated the task looks.

SCOTUS finds that Trump's 2025 firing of FTC Commissioner Rebecca Kelly Slaughter without cause was lawful, placing independence of several agencies in doubt (NPR) (1 minute read)

The Supreme Court ruled that Trump's 2025 dismissal of FTC Commissioner Rebecca Kelly Slaughter without cause was lawful, eroding the for-cause protections that have shielded independent agencies since 1935. The decision puts the FTC, FCC, SEC, and NLRB under tighter White House control, meaning antitrust, securities, and labor enforcement can now swing with each administration. For founders, the regulators who police mergers, fundraising disclosures, and hiring practices are becoming political instruments, so the rules you build around today may not survive the next election cycle.

SpaceX's $25 billion bond sale drives huge demand - and a potential headache for investors (1 minute read)

SpaceX raised $25B in senior unsecured notes last week, pulling nearly $90B in orders just two weeks after the largest IPO in history. The catch is that owning both SPCX equity and SpaceX bonds isn't diversification, it's the same execution risk priced into two instruments, with both the equity story and the debt service riding on Starlink scaling and Starship working. Equity investors took the hint, selling the stock down more than 13% on the week against a $5B net loss and capex that more than doubled year over year.

Anthropic and Gov. Newsom forge deal allowing California government to use Claude at half price (1 minute read)

Anthropic struck a deal with California Governor Gavin Newsom to give every state agency and local government access to Claude at half price, along with training and support. Super Micro Office Raided as Taiwan Expands Chip Smuggling Probe (1 minute read)

Taiwanese authorities raided Super Micro's offices on Monday, widening a probe into the alleged smuggling of Nvidia chips into China through the company's servers. The raid signals that export-control enforcement is moving past the chipmakers and onto the hardware vendors and integrators that move the silicon. For any founder building on top of restricted GPUs, supply-chain provenance is becoming a compliance liability, not just a sourcing detail, and the labs and clouds you depend on now have to prove where every chip went.

California signed a deal with Anthropic to roll out Claude across state agencies and local governments at a 50% discount. For founders selling AI into regulated or public markets, the labs are now establishing the procurement relationships and price anchors directly, which compresses the room for resellers and middleware built on top of the same models.
