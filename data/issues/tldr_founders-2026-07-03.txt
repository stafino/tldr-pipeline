ElevenLabs targets $22bn valuation with fresh share sale, reports say (5 minute read)

ElevenLabs is in early talks over a secondary share sale that would value the AI voice startup at around $22bn, double the $11bn it fetched when it raised $500m in February. The tender, which would let employees cash out some shares, could land as soon as September. The repricing tracks a deliberate shift upmarket: revenue was split roughly 50-50 between consumer and enterprise at the end of 2025, and CEO Mati Staniszewski expects enterprise to reach 70% by the end of 2027, with an eventual IPO the stated destination.

5 Interesting Learnings from Toast at $6.5 Billion Run-Rate: 22%+ Growth, Profitable, No Deceleration. But AI Is Just Getting Started (1 minute read)

Toast is running at a $6.5B revenue run-rate, still growing 22% while crossing into durable GAAP profitability, with net income more than doubling to $126M. Underneath the payments business sits a high-margin software layer that just crossed 80% gross margin, a fintech lender, and an AI agent platform now used weekly by 40,000 locations. The lesson for founders chasing vertical SaaS is that the winners own the whole stack, payments, software, lending, and AI, in one integrated package, and Toast is adding roughly 7,000 restaurants a quarter without slowing down.

Meta could use its compute for its own models, ad scaling, SpaceX-like neocloud deals, and hosting 3rd-party models; it may be close to an Anthropic deal (Jeremie Eliahou Ontiveros/SemiAnalysis) (1 minute read)

Meta has four options for its massive compute buildout: train its own models, scale ads, cut SpaceX-style neocloud deals, and host third-party models, and it may be close to a hosting deal with Anthropic. That last path is the notable one, because it would put Meta in the business of renting capacity to a direct AI rival rather than only feeding its own Llama efforts. The move signals that even the labs racing to build frontier models increasingly treat compute itself as the asset worth monetizing.

Trunk Tools' stack cut document review from 60 days to 10 by ditching general-purpose models (2 minute read)

Trunk Tools cut construction document review from 60 days to 10 by dropping general-purpose LLMs for a purpose-built stack. Foundation models are trained to be okay at everything, so they fumble the jargon, abbreviations, and implicit workflows that domain practitioners rely on, and RAG alone just feeds better facts to a model that still can't reason in the vertical. The playbook is to pre-process your proprietary data through an ontology and knowledge graph, then fine-tune on a few thousand real practitioner examples, which beat millions of scraped noisy ones.

Half-Baked Product (1 minute read)

The founder who can't bake decides to build a better oven, hires an engineer who lives to argue oven theory on Italian forums, and together they ship an MVP that produces a perfect bake only a third of the time. The failure isn't technical. It's that nobody in the loop actually bakes, so "good enough for a prototype" quietly becomes the standard nobody questions. Domain obsession and market spreadsheets don't substitute for living in the customer's daily reality.
