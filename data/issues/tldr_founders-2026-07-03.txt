Investors sue Oracle, say it hid how shaky the OpenAI deal was (1 minute read)

Oracle's own investors sued the company, alleging it hid how shaky its $300B OpenAI deal was when it sold $25B in notes in February. The suit says Oracle touted a backlog that had ballooned from about $97B to more than $523B in a year without disclosing that nearly all of it traced to OpenAI, which had missed revenue targets and privately questioned whether it could pay. This is the AI boom's circular-financing worry filed as a legal document, and Oracle isn't alone, with Microsoft facing its own shareholder suit over AI spending.

Most AI Work Can Wait (5 minute read)

Most teams building AI agents pick the model first and the architecture second, when the model choice should be the last decision. What matters is the router, a small piece of code that reads a task's classified intent and features to decide which tier handles it, so 70-80% of traffic runs on local models that cost nothing or async batch jobs that cut spend 90%. The real question is narrower than it looks: what fraction of work actually needs a real-time answer? Once the system can queue, surprisingly little.

How Sony sold 400 million Walkmans (5 minute read)

In 1979, Sony shipped the Walkman without a record button, ignoring its own market research, its retailers, and its engineers, who all insisted a tape player that couldn't record wouldn't sell. Akio Morita cut the feature anyway and bet his presidency on selling 100,000 units, and the product went on to move 400 million. The lesson isn't to ignore data, it's that a clear point of view about what a product is for can outrank what customers say they want.
