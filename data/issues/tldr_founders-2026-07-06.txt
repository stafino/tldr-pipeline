Tired vs. Wired: Our Deep Dive on Why Software Spend Is Up Record Amounts … Yet Half of SaaS Is Still Dying (1 minute read)

SemiAnalysis: Nvidia delays its next-gen AI rack system Kyber NVL144 by 12+ months to 2028 due to PCB manufacturing issues, and cancels its NVL72x2 architecture (Anniek Bao/CNBC) (1 minute read)

Nvidia pushed its next-gen Kyber NVL144 rack system out by more than a year to 2028 over PCB manufacturing problems, and killed its planned NVL72x2 architecture outright, according to SemiAnalysis. The slip stretches the current Blackwell and Rubin generations at the top of the datacenter stack, which resets the timeline every hyperscaler and neocloud built their compute buildouts around. For founders pricing multi-year GPU capacity or betting on when the next efficiency jump lands, the frontier just moved further out, and the incumbent hardware you can actually buy today has a longer runway than expected.

Nvidia's next-gen AI rack system delayed to 2028 on manufacturing snags, SemiAnalysis says (1 minute read)

Nvidia's Kyber rack system, the cabinet meant to house its 2027 Rubin Ultra chips, has slipped more than a year to 2028 after the PCB midplane at its core proved too hard to manufacture, SemiAnalysis says. The fallback of bolting two current-gen racks together was scrapped after cloud providers rejected it as costly to operate, leaving Nvidia with no proven way to scale up Rubin Ultra at the high end. That opening is where AMD and Google's in-house chips, already winning business at top AI labs, get a rare technical shot at the market's most lucrative tier.

AI-native startups hire fewer juniors and more elites, Harvard study finds (1 minute read)

AI-native startups hire fewer entry-level workers than their peers, according to a Harvard Business School and INSEAD working paper on Y Combinator firms from 2020 to 2024. These startups are 25% smaller, employ 13% more engineers, and carry roughly 15% lower shares of both entry-level workers and managers, with the seniors they do hire skewing elite-educated, Silicon Valley-based, and male. That cuts against the hope that juniors would use AI to punch above their grade, and the authors warn that uneven adoption could widen performance gaps into compounding inequality.

UK startups raise $17B in H1 2026 as AI takes 74% of VC and deeptech share nearly doubles (1 minute read)

UK startups raised $17B in the first half of 2026, with AI companies taking 74% of all venture capital and deeptech's share nearly doubling. The concentration means a founder outside those categories is now competing for a shrinking slice of a growing pool. Capital is clustering around a few technical bets, and the gap between what gets funded and what merely gets meetings is widening.

Jim Keller's startup is building a factory to mass-produce small semiconductor fabs —Atomic Semi rebrands as 'Fab2' underlining intended role as a 'fab fab' (1 minute read)

Jim Keller's Atomic Semi has rebranded as Fab2 and moved to Texas, betting on a "fab fab": a factory that mass-produces small, software-defined semiconductor fabs and builds every tool inside them in-house. The pitch is prototype chips in hours instead of weeks, but the model runs on electron-beam lithography that writes patterns directly, so a single step can take longer than an EUV scanner needs for a whole 300mm wafer. That throughput ceiling keeps Fab2 aimed at prototyping and low-volume runs, not the high-volume foundry work TSMC owns.

Tech billionaires pour $120M into killing California’s wealth tax (1 minute read)

Silicon Valley's wealthiest have put more than $120M into defeating California's Proposition 40, a one-time 5% tax on residents worth over $1B, roughly four times the $31M raised by the union backers behind it. Sergey Brin leads the opposition at $82M and has moved a raft of LLCs out of state, with Doerr, Moritz, Collison, Schmidt, Thiel, and others following. The measure would hit about 200 billionaires and, proponents project, raise $100B for healthcare, food assistance, and education when Californians vote in November.

Skello souffle sa 10e bougie avec un financement de 200 millions d’euros pour conquérir l’Europe (1 minute read)

Skello, the French HR and shift-management platform for frontline teams, raised €200M in equity and debt, led by Bridgepoint Development Capital with returning backers Partech and XAnge. That is a fivefold jump on its €40M Series B from September 2021, and it funds a push to become the first pan-European leader in a market with no clear winner yet. The company already serves 25,000 businesses and 600,000 daily users across France, Spain, Benelux, and Italy, and plans to expand into Southern then Northern Europe through acquisitions.

Building relationships with customers through support didn't turn out as hoped (1 minute read)

Thoughtful human support turned out to be a weak differentiator, not the loyalty engine the founder expected. Castro's owner read and answered every email personally, even hiring a power user to help, but most careful replies landed worse than no reply, and pricing complaints reliably ended more negative than they started. The lesson is that support quality only wows customers when you ship an instant, exact fix. For most requests, an honest thoughtful answer is not what the sender actually wanted.
