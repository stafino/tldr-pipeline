Open-weight AI models are facing an existential policy test in the US, with Anthropic leading a campaign against Chinese models over distillation concerns (Nathan Lambert/Interconnects AI) (1 minute read)

Open-weight AI is facing its first real policy fight in the US, and the battle lines run straight through China. Anthropic is leading a campaign to restrict Chinese open models like DeepSeek and Qwen, arguing that letting US developers distill from them creates security and dependency risks. The outcome matters for any founder building on open weights, because a win for the restriction camp narrows the pool of cheap, permissively licensed models just as those models were becoming good enough to displace frontier APIs.

Revolut to launch US bank in 2027, says US boss (1 minute read)

Revolut plans to launch its own US bank in 2027 after applying for a national banking licence in March, a move that would let Europe's most valuable private company, valued at $75B, drop its partner-bank arrangement and go it alone. The licence unlocks FDIC-insured deposits, credit cards, and crypto access, plus credit interchange fees to fund travel perks in a credit-driven market. Revolut has earmarked $500M for US expansion across capital, people, and marketing, betting that its 75M global customers and a friendlier Trump-era regulatory climate carry it into the world's biggest banking market.

Moneybox crowned Europe’s newest unicorn as it gears up to test London’s Pisces market (5 minute read)

Moneybox hit a $1.1B valuation as it prepares a £45M secondary sale for long-tenured employees, becoming Europe's newest unicorn. What matters more than the badge is the venue: the shares will be auctioned on the London Stock Exchange's new Private Securities Market, built under the UK's Pisces framework to let private companies trade stock without going public. Wayve ran a similar sale last week, and the LSE has courted Revolut and OakNorth, part of a government push to keep British startups liquid at home rather than listing abroad.

DeepSeek cut prices 75%. The 100x problem remains (3 minute read)

DeepSeek cut prices on its V4-Pro model by 75%, but cheaper inference isn't fixing anyone's margins. Agent systems consume tokens faster than prices are falling, turning one user-visible request into a chain of planning, retrieval, tool use, and verification that pushes the input-to-billed ratio from roughly 1:5 in a chatbot to 1:700 or higher in production agents. Falling model prices don't fix a product architecture that turns one prompt into dozens of billable operations, which is why OpenAI is reportedly offering YC startups $2M in API credits, an admission of what running an AI-native company now costs.

Nvidia, CoreWeave, and Nebius: Inside the Circular Financing of the GPU Boom (1 minute read)

Neoclouds like CoreWeave and Nebius have turned quick access to Nvidia GPUs into soaring sales, backlog, and share prices, but the buildout is funded by GPU-backed debt, hyperscaler contracts, and Nvidia's own equity. Both have locked up 3.5 GW of contracted power while most of it isn't live yet, and converting that backlog into revenue is expensive against thin cash flow and rising debt. The circular part is Nvidia investing in and backstopping the same customers buying its chips, which props up demand and makes the surge harder to read.
