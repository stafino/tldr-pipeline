European defencetech leader Helsing secures $1.8B Series E at $18B valuation (1 minute read)

Helsing raised $1.8B in a Series E at an $18B valuation, with demand far exceeding the allocation and the company staying predominantly European-owned. Founded in 2021, it fuses drone, radar, satellite, and sensor data into a single real-time battlefield picture and has since expanded into strike drones and underwater surveillance systems built for GPS-denied, contested environments. Europe's defence-AI leader is now one of the continent's most valuable private companies, a sign that capital is flowing toward sovereign, software-defined military capability as governments rearm and look to keep that infrastructure at home.

Helsing’s $18B valuation is not really about Helsing — private markets are pricing European rearmament as a multi-decade procurement cycle, and the capital is positioning accordingly (1 minute read)

Helsing raised $1.8B at an $18B valuation in an oversubscribed round, but the deal is really a bet on European rearmament as a multi-decade procurement cycle. The tell is the cap table: a Canadian pension fund, JPMorgan, Goldman's alternatives arm, and Silicon Valley growth funds are now underwriting autonomous strike weapons, a category ESG mandates kept institutional capital out of until recently. This is the second nine-figure European defence-tech round in months, after Quantum Systems' $1.2B raise, and the capital is positioning for a structural shift, not a single company.

Nvidia Partner GMI Cloud Seeks $635 Million GPU-Backed Bank Loan (1 minute read)

GMI Cloud, a US data center operator in Nvidia's partner network, is seeking a NT$20.45 billion ($635 million) multi-tranche loan backed by its customer GPU contracts. It is one of the first GPU-collateralized financings in Asia, where AI compute demand is outrunning what operators can fund off their balance sheets. The deal signals that GPUs are becoming a financeable asset class, with future rental revenue underwriting the debt that buys the next round of chips. Founders competing for capacity now face lenders and hyperscalers using the same contracts to lock up supply before it ships.

Hermes agent maker Nous Research in talks for new funding at $1.5B valuation (1 minute read)

Nous Research is in talks to raise at least $75M at a $1.5B valuation, led by Robot Ventures with participation from USV, after previously raising $70M from investors including Paradigm and Balaji Srinivasan. The startup's open-source Hermes agent has drawn a large following, with roughly 214,000 GitHub stars and nearly 40,000 forks, and ships with built-in skills like web search and coding that improve automatically from usage. The round shows investors are still paying up for open-source agent makers that own a developer base, even as the frontier labs push their own agents down the stack.

Artificial Bloom (1 minute read)

The scramble to turn cheap US natural gas into AI electricity has created a multi-year backlog for gas turbines, and Bloom Energy stepped into that void with solid oxide fuel cells pitched as a China-free alternative. That claim just cracked. Short seller Hunterbrook traced four China-linked routes supplying the scandium Bloom depends on, contradicting the CEO's repeated "no China supply chain" statements since early 2025, and shares fell as much as 12%. The lesson for anyone riding the AI-infrastructure trade is that supply-chain claims which sound too clean usually are.

Revolut to launch US bank in 2027, says US boss (1 minute read)

Revolut plans to launch its own US bank in 2027 after applying for a national banking licence in March, a move that would let Europe's most valuable private company, valued at $75B, drop its partner-bank arrangement and go it alone. The licence unlocks FDIC-insured deposits, credit cards, and crypto access, plus credit interchange fees to fund travel perks in a credit-driven market. Revolut has earmarked $500M for US expansion across capital, people, and marketing, betting that its 75M global customers and a friendlier Trump-era regulatory climate carry it into the world's biggest banking market.

Should You Visit More Of Your Prospects In Person?  Almost Certainly (1 minute read)

In-person visits still win competitive deals, even in a world where seven-figure contracts close routinely over Zoom. Prospects arrive pre-sold from your free trial and their 100-plus existing SaaS apps, but a competitor who shows up in the office can flip a renewal you thought was locked. The rule of thumb: show up for any $20K+ competitive deal, any $50K+ deal within reach, and every Top 100 logo you want, even when today's contract is only worth $5K.

Don’t Put AI on Your Hot Leads. Put It on the Ones Your Reps Will Never Call. There’s Millions in Revenue There. (1 minute read)

Most teams point their first AI sales agent at their hottest leads, which is exactly backwards. A leads close on their own, so an agent there adds nothing and risks stepping on a deal a human would have won anyway. The money is in the B leads: real signal, real fit, but never hot enough for a rep with a quota to call. That pile sits in the database forever because the incentives are rational, and an agent with infinite patience and no quota anxiety will work it three follow-ups deep when a human does zero.

Generated and suppressed demand. (5 minute read)

Recovering a struggling team can leave it just as underwater as before, because success generates demand that was previously suppressed. Suppressed demand is work people stopped asking for once they learned the team couldn't help; as the team ships and gets noticed, that latent demand converts into real requests. The disorienting result is that a team can do everything right, clear its backlog, and still end up as far behind as at its worst.
