Who’s Afraid of Chinese Models? (1 minute read)

Open weights models out of China like Kimi K3 aren't free, they just move the spending from R&D to COGS, and COGS is a real line item for AI in a way it never was for software. The old zero-marginal-cost logic that made internet businesses winner-take-all doesn't hold when every query costs money to serve. The author argues this shifts the industry's structure back toward classic universal business principles, where cost discipline and gross margin matter more than the aggregation dynamics that defined the last two decades.

Chinese open models like Kimi K3 and Qwen3.8 Max shift the industry's focus back to marginal costs and cost of goods sold, as inference costs scale with revenue (Ben Thompson/Stratechery) (1 minute read)

Cheap Chinese open models like Kimi K3 and Qwen3.8 Max are pulling the industry's attention away from training runs and back toward marginal cost. The argument is that AI businesses look less like classic software, where serving one more customer is nearly free, and more like a business with real cost of goods sold, because inference spend rises with usage and therefore with revenue. For founders building on top of models, gross margin becomes the number that decides whether the business works, and model routing becomes a margin decision rather than an engineering one.

What I learned selling 2,500 MIDI recorders: Hardware is not so hard (1 minute read)

The founder behind Jamcorder sold 2,500 units of a piano-recording device and found that the hardware, the part everyone warns you about, was the smooth part. The hard part was software: roughly 200K lines across firmware, app, and manufacturing tooling, built over three years before LLMs. He kept the device deliberately simple, with one screw, one PCB, no power button, and no USB-C, and his conclusion is that hardware is as hard as you make it.
