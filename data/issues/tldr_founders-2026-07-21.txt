Sila raised $300M led by Atreides Management and Sutter Hill Ventures to expand its Moses Lake, Washington plant from 2 gigawatt-hours to tens of gigawatt-hours of silicon-carbon anode material, enough for more than 100,000 EVs. AI materials science startup CuspAI raises $450M in funding (1 minute read)

CuspAI raised $450M at a $2.6B valuation, led by Kleiner Perkins and NEA with backing from Bezos Expeditions, to compress materials discovery projects that normally take years into about six months. Its MIRA platform runs on Meta's open-source UMA models for atomic-level simulation plus CuspAI's own kUPS toolkit for molecular structure, with exclusive AI training rights to several academic datasets doing the real moat work. The company also launched the AI Materials Foundry, a research consortium of more than 45 members including Nvidia and Samsung, which turns customers into co-development partners before the science is proven.

Who’s Afraid of Chinese Models? (1 minute read)

Open weights models out of China like Kimi K3 aren't free, they just move the spending from R&D to COGS, and COGS is a real line item for AI in a way it never was for software. The old zero-marginal-cost logic that made internet businesses winner-take-all doesn't hold when every query costs money to serve. The author argues this shifts the industry's structure back toward classic universal business principles, where cost discipline and gross margin matter more than the aggregation dynamics that defined the last two decades.

Next-gen battery startup Sila raises $300M to expand manufacturing plant in Washington state (1 minute read)

Sila raised $300M led by Atreides Management and Sutter Hill Ventures to expand its Moses Lake, Washington plant, the first automotive-scale silicon-anode facility in the US. The current site produces about 2 gigawatt-hours of anode material, enough for 20,000 to 50,000 EVs, and the expansion targets tens of gigawatt-hours. US EV sales have cooled, but the demand pull has shifted to drones, robotics, wearables, and AI data centers, all of which need higher energy density than graphite anodes deliver. Hard-tech capital is following the AI buildout down into the materials layer.

How to Give Top-Tier Demos Like a Pro (1 minute read)

Founders spend weeks on the deck and an hour on the demo, but the average investor gives a seed deck 3 minutes and 44 seconds and only 58% reach the final slide. A deck describes what you claim to have built; a live demo proves it exists and that you can run it while someone watches. The post argues a demo is a compressed argument, not a feature tour, and works best when you pick the one belief you want to install, open on the pain, run it live without dead air, and close with a specific ask.

What I learned selling 2,500 MIDI recorders: Hardware is not so hard (1 minute read)

The founder behind Jamcorder sold 2,500 units of a piano-recording device and found that the hardware, the part everyone warns you about, was the smooth part. The hard part was software: roughly 200K lines across firmware, app, and manufacturing tooling, built over three years before LLMs. He kept the device deliberately simple, with one screw, one PCB, no power button, and no USB-C, and his conclusion is that hardware is as hard as you make it.
