Headlines & Trends

Sources: Stripe is in talks to acquire OpenRouter, which helps developers use AI models and could fetch ~$10B; PitchBook: OpenRouter was valued at $1.3B in May (Wall Street Journal) (1 minute read)

Stripe is in talks to acquire OpenRouter, the marketplace that routes developer traffic across AI models, in a deal that could reach roughly $10B. PitchBook pegged OpenRouter at $1.3B in May, so the mooted price is nearly eight times its valuation two months ago. Stripe already sits in the payments layer under most of the AI economy, and buying the router that decides which model handles each request would push it up the stack into how developers actually consume inference.

Travis Kalanick raised $1.7B for his robotics comeback, and Uber chipped in (1 minute read)

Travis Kalanick raised $1.7B for Atoms, his stealth industrial-AI and robotics company, in a round led by a16z that includes Uber, the company that forced him out in 2017. Atoms bets against the field's humanoid rush, building purpose-built machines for mining, construction, heavy transport, and food, and it stacked the equity with credit facilities from JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, Bank of America, Wells Fargo, and Barclays. The bank debt signals the real shift: this is capital raised to build heavy machines, not apps, and the winners in physical AI may specialize where everyone else is going general.

5 Interesting Learnings from Stripe at $6.8 Billion in Revenue: 33% Growth, 47% Free Cash Flow Margins, and a $53B Bid for PayPal (1 minute read)

Stripe's 2025 revenue hit $6.8B, up roughly a third year-over-year, its fastest growth since 2021, on a base more than 4x larger. The re-acceleration traces to AI: Stripe processes payments for OpenAI, Anthropic, Cursor, Lovable, and the rest of the AI-native long tail, so its take-rate model inherits their growth without paying their CAC. Free cash flow grew 52% to $3.2B, a 47% margin, making this a Rule of 80 company at $6.8B in revenue while the best public B2B comps run Rule of 30 to 40.

Investment-grade bonds from the largest US tech companies sold off Thursday as investors reassessed how much debt is funding the AI buildout, with Middle East tensions adding to the pressure. The concern is concentration: a handful of hyperscalers are borrowing heavily to fund compute capex on the bet that AI demand shows up on schedule, and any doubt about that timeline now moves credit markets, not just equities. Nvidia rival Etched doubles its value to $10.3B in seven months (1 minute read)

Etched raised $300M in a Series C that values the inference-chip startup at $10.3B, double its $5B mark from December and its total funding past $1B. The bet is specialization: chips built only for transformer inference, run at under half the usual voltage to beat the heat throttling that caps Nvidia's general-purpose GPUs. That the round pulled in SK Hynix, the top AI-memory maker, signals where the moat is forming, and Google hardwiring Gemini into its Frozen v2 chip shows the fixed-architecture idea is going mainstream. The unglamorous part starts now: moving from working prototypes to mass production.

Vertical AI Market Map 2026: Israeli Startups and Funding Rounds (1 minute read)

Israel's H1 2026 funding data is where the long-predicted vertical AI thesis first shows up in the numbers, with billions raised but the more telling signal in where application-layer capital landed. AMD Commits Up to $5 Billion to Anthropic in 2-Gigawatt Chip Deal (1 minute read)

AMD committed up to $5 billion in equity to Anthropic, tied to milestones, in exchange for Anthropic deploying up to 2 gigawatts of AMD's Instinct MI450 GPUs starting in early 2027. The structure inverts AMD's OpenAI deal, where the chipmaker handed the customer a warrant for nearly 10% of itself. Here the cash flows to the buyer and AMD ends up both supplier and shareholder, a sign that the frontier labs now command enough leverage to make their chip vendors pay for the privilege of being designed in.

Strategies & Tactics

When and When Not to Raise (1 minute read)

Venture capital is jet fuel, not gas for everyone, and the best investors will tell you when your business is a motorcycle. A profitable agency throwing off $1M a year in cash, run with people you like, alongside time with your family, is often a great business that VC would only wreck. Raise when your company is built to become a jet, one where outside capital compounds into a much larger outcome. If the honest answer is that you already have what you want, taking the money just adds pressure to fly a plane you never wanted to build.

The Forward Deployed Engineer Playbookhe Forward Deployed Engineer Playbook (1 minute read)

The forward-deployed engineer is the role every AI company is now racing to hire, because the best ones turn a raw capability into a business outcome and carry the customer until they can run it alone. They are scarce and expensive, and they resist a standard interview rubric, which is exactly why most teams staff them badly. Insight Partners pulled FDE leaders from ServiceNow, Databricks, Workato, and Wonderful into one room, and the playbook turns their agreement into a copyable operating manual: the charter, interview loop, comp bands, pod model, 90-day runbook, and bench budget math.

The most valuable thing Apple built in the 2000s wasn't the iPod (5 minute read)

iTunes, not the iPod, was Apple's most consequential build of the 2000s. Its 99¢ per-track store launched in April 2003 with about 200,000 songs, hit its six-month sales forecast in six days, and carried more than 70% of legal digital downloads within three years. It worked because Apple stacked strategic levers rather than just selling songs online, and in the process it unbundled the album and set up the streaming and playlist economy we live in now.
