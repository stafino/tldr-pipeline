SkillCloak Lets Malicious AI Agent Skills Evade Static Scanners with Self-Extracting Packing (1 minute read)

Researchers at Hong Kong University of Science and Technology released SkillCloak, a technique that rewrites malicious AI-agent skills to slip past static scanners while preserving their behavior, with its self-extracting packing variant evading all eight tested scanners over 90% of the time by hiding payloads in skipped directories like .git/. The same team built a runtime checker that catches most of the disguised skills, since pre-install file scanning alone fails to stop packed or obfuscated payloads.

Opera GX Flaw Let Malicious Sites Auto-Install Mods to Steal Data From Visited Pages (1 minute read) [flag]

Researchers disclosed a no-click flaw in Opera GX that let a malicious website silently auto-install a GX Mod, then abuse its CSS as a universal injection to exfiltrate page data via attribute-selector XS-Leaks, reconstructing a signed-in user's Gmail address from one visit. Opera patched it in GX 130.0.5847.89 (no CVE, rated P1) and found no in-the-wild abuse; there was no workaround short of updating, so verify your build at opera://about.

New ClamAV security patch closes seven scanner bugs dating back two decades (1 minute read)

ClamAV 1.5.3 and 1.4.5 patch seven flaws in the Cisco Talos scanner, most in PE unpacking code including CVE-2026-20213, a heap overflow write triggered by malformed Aspack-packed files, and CVE-2026-20214, an FSG unpacker underflow present since 2004. Web-based cryptography is always snake oil (1 minute read)

The author argues that all web-based "end-to-end" encryption is incoherent, because the code implementing the cryptosystem is distributed by the same server operator it claims to secure against, who could simply push malicious JavaScript at any time. The proposed rule: a cryptosystem is incoherent if its implementation is distributed by the entity it purports to secure against. The critique extends beyond the browser to any service that both distributes the client and bans third-party clients, including Signal and WhatsApp.

A sociotechnical threat model for AI-driven smart home devices (1 minute read)

This paper builds a sociotechnical threat model for AI-driven smart home devices, drawn from interviews with 18 UK-based domestic workers monitored in employers' homes. Using Communication Privacy Management, the authors extend threat models past abstract adversaries to cover AI analytics, residual data logs, and cross-household data flows, and they identify staffing agencies as institutional adversaries. The takeaway for defenders: real-world privacy risk maps across interconnected households and opaque data retention, not single-device threat surfaces.

EU Council forces Chat Control via fast-track (1 minute read)

The EU Council adopted a position via written procedure to reactivate the expired Chat Control 1.0 regulation, using a fast-track legal maneuver to authorize voluntary AI and hash-matching scans of messenger, webmail, and VoIP content. Critics argue the move sidesteps Parliament and the E-Privacy Directive's confidentiality protections, reviving suspicionless client-side scanning of encrypted communications ahead of the summer break.

Alibaba bans Anthropic's Claude Code after an alleged hidden China-detection backdoor is uncovered — employees told to switch to Qoder as the rift between the firms widens (1 minute read)

Alibaba banned employees from using Anthropic's Claude Code for work starting July 10, after researchers alleged the coding agent shipped obfuscated logic that fingerprints China-based users by checking system timezone and proxy URLs against a hardcoded list of Chinese AI labs, then exfiltrates the result steganographically. The ban, which extends to all Anthropic products and redirects staff to Alibaba's in-house Qoder, follows Anthropic's June accusation that Qwen-linked operators ran the largest known distillation attack against Claude.
