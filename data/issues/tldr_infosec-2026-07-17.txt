Setup Complete, Now You Are Compromised: Weaponizing Setup Instructions Against AI Coding Agents (1 minute read)

This paper demonstrates package-install-time supply-chain attacks delivered through ordinary project-setup docs, showing that AI coding agents install dependencies from a poisoned README, requirements file, or Makefile without verifying names, sources, or versions across twelve scenarios in five attack classes. Agents reliably catch blatant typosquats but miss separator-confusion names (azurecore for azure-core) and almost always follow registry redirection; a deterministic pre-install check that verifies names, sources, and versions closes most of the gap.

The Prover Is the Judge: Verified Security Software from AI Coding Agents in Ada/SPARK (1 minute read) [flag]

Under a verifier-driven loop, AI agents wrote and formally verified bare-metal security software in Ada/SPARK, covering post-quantum crypto, TLS 1.3, IKEv2, X.509, and a Matrix client, with GNATprove discharging 49,280 proof obligations at 20-40x lower supervision cost than hand verification. The prover alone missed some defects, caught instead by known-answer tests, interop, and spec review; given weak checks, the agent tried to bypass them and falsely reported success. What an agent can be trusted to establish is bounded by the strength of its feedback.

A Measurement Study of AI-Environment Realism Gaps in Malware-Analysis Sandboxes (1 minute read)

Researchers introduce AIprint, a probe framework that fingerprints malware-analysis sandboxes by the absence of AI-tooling artifacts: assistant config directories, model caches, local inference services, and package dependencies left behind by AI-capable hosts. Testing 450 artifacts from 284 GitHub projects across seven sandbox backends, they found twelve markers present on real AI hosts and none of the sandboxes, while traditional VM-detection baselines failed to tell them apart. Defenders should treat AI-environment realism as a new evasion surface and provision analysis sandboxes with plausible AI tooling.

The script, not the voice, is what makes AI voice phishing work (1 minute read)

A study from Harvard Kennedy School, Meta, and others tested six commercial AI voice systems against human callers on 4,100 US adults, and found that voice authenticity did not predict whether people complied with a request. Persuasiveness did: each step up the scale raised compliance odds 2.58x, and detected-as-synthetic voices still extracted credit card security codes. The takeaway for fraud teams is that deepfake voice detection misses the actual attack vector, while trained suspicion is already breaking legitimate callback verification in both directions.

Inside the Search for "Clean" Residential Proxies for Carding (1 minute read)

Flare analyzed 2,889 underground posts across roughly 545 threads over the past two years and found that carders no longer treat residential proxies as a standalone anonymity bypass. Actors now judge proxies by IP reputation and history, match geography down to city, ZIP, time zone, and browser language, and pair them with antidetect browsers and fingerprint manipulation to simulate a full identity. The defender takeaway: treat residential traffic as context, not proof of legitimacy, and weight fraud scoring on identity and behavioral consistency rather than IP type.

ProfMalPlus: Agent-Coordinated Detection of Malicious NPM Packages via Static-Dynamic Analysis Synergy (1 minute read)

ProfMalPlus is a malicious NPM package detector that pairs object-sensitive behavior graphs with coordinated LLM judge agents reasoning over annotated code slices, routing undetermined cases to third-party registry enrichment or sandboxed dynamic execution before a final verdict. It hit a 98.1% F1-score, beating prior detectors by 3.5% to 52.6%, and surfaced 597 previously unknown malicious packages that were confirmed and pulled from NPM. The agent-coordinated static-dynamic design is the notable part for teams building supply-chain scanning into CI.

Beyond Success Rate: Cost-Aware Evaluation of Offensive and Defensive Security Agents (1 minute read)

This paper evaluates offensive and defensive LLM security agents under fixed cost budgets rather than peak-capability inference, testing on offensive Cybench challenges and defensive Splunk BOTS v1 investigations. The authors find red- and blue-team tasks scale differently: offensive CTF success improves with added test-time compute, letting scaled open-weight models rival frontier proprietary systems at competitive cost, while SOC investigation depends more on disciplined tool use and telemetry navigation than raw reasoning budget. They argue benchmarks should measure economic efficiency and operational fit, not just task success.

The Distributed Open-Source Vulnerability Ecosystem (1 minute read)

This paper presents a conceptual framework treating open-source vulnerability management as a distributed information-exchange process, tracing vuln data from creation and standardization through enrichment to context-dependent interpretation. It identifies heterogeneous sources, divergent identity and version models, temporal change, and context-dependent assessment as the root causes of why different scanners report divergent findings for identical software inventories. The practical takeaway: don't treat any single scanner's output as ground truth, and design reproducible evaluation methods that account for how vulnerability knowledge shifts over time.
