Headline formats and Google Discover: What 3.4 million articles reveal (1 minute read)

A study of 3.4 million Google Discover articles found that headline format is mostly a proxy for which publisher, audience, and Discover surface an article reaches, not an independent driver of visibility. Pooled across publishers, quote-led headlines show a +37% lift in English and +48% in French, but the effect collapses once you control for publisher and segment, a classic case of Simpson's paradox. The takeaway for content teams: rewriting statements as quotes or adding a question mark rarely delivers measurable lift on its own.

How AI helped build hreflang XML sitemaps at scale (1 minute read)

An SEO lead used Google Gemini to build a custom Python script in Google Colab that mapped thousands of URLs into accurate hreflang XML sitemaps across a dozen multilingual sites, eight regional domains, and seven languages. The takeaway is in the prompting sequence: ask the model for an approach before a script, since "write a Python script to create an hreflang sitemap" returns generic code that breaks on real data. For technical SEO teams, this is a faster path than specialized software or days of spreadsheet work for recurring data-processing tasks.

Why next-question intent matters for AI search visibility (1 minute read)

Traditional search returned a ranked list users interpreted themselves; AI search synthesizes one answer from multiple sources, which changes what a page must contain. The author argues for optimizing around "next-question intent": whether a page answers the follow-up comparisons, constraints, and objections that come after the initial query, not just the query itself. Pages that cover the full decision path give AI systems more material to summarize, compare, and cite, making them likelier to be recommended.

People prefer products with fewer ingredients (1 minute read)

New research found that framing a product as having "just a few" ingredients rather than "many" made shoppers up to 22% more likely to choose it, even when the actual formulation was identical. For CPG and B2C marketers, the takeaway is a copy test, not a reformulation: lead with ingredient simplicity in product descriptions and ad creative rather than defaulting to the now-generic "organic" and "natural" claims that competitors have already saturated.
