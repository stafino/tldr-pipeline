Pinterest launches an experimental AI shopping app called ‘Ask Pinterest’ (1 minute read)

Pinterest released Ask Pinterest, a limited-access experimental standalone app that turns its visual discovery into a conversational, chatbot-style interface for multi-step shopping queries like planning a dinner party or furnishing a room over time. The app runs on Pinterest's Taste Graph and users' saved Pins to personalize recommendations, letting the company test agentic shopping without disrupting the main app and feed what it learns back into the flagship.

LinkedIn will tell others how you really use Adobe’s apps (1 minute read)

LinkedIn launched a "connected apps" feature that auto-generates verified, non-editable descriptions of how members actually use linked tools like HubSpot, Fiverr, and Buffer, with Adobe Express, GitHub Copilot, and Zapier coming soon. The statements update in real time without user approval, giving recruiters tool-specific proof of proficiency and giving PMs a new signal to weigh when hiring for roles that require hands-on fluency with particular software.

How to design AI agent loops: schedules, goals, and subagents in Claude Code and Codex (1 minute read)

The author breaks down four loop types for AI coding agents — heartbeat, cron, hook, and goal — and argues a loop is just an automated prompt, then names the five things any production loop needs: work trees, skills, plugins, subagents, and state tracking. He builds two live examples: a daily aging-PR reviewer in Claude Code that self-schedules and spawns subagents, and a weekly skills-identification loop in Codex whose goal-based subagents validate their own output. The practical warning for PMs: goal loops are the hardest to write and the fastest way to burn tokens for nothing.

A comparison of human and LLM-simulated participants in a writing style task (1 minute read)

A study tested whether GPT-4o prompted as a synthetic participant could replace 30 humans in evaluating how writing-style preference algorithms adapt over repeated interactions. The LLM showed bias and a lack of depth in both its text generation and judgment, preventing it from accurately reproducing human behavior, while the humans revealed their own biases. For PMs tempted to run user research on simulated participants, the takeaway is that LLM stand-ins miss the human factors that make a study worth running.
