Google Finance exits beta with a dedicated Android app, portfolio tracking, and AI-powered scheduled briefings (1 minute read)

Google Finance exited beta with a dedicated Android app, portfolio tracking, an AI research tool, and scheduled briefings, restoring the portfolio feature it cut in 2017 with Gemini layered on top. The portfolio dashboard ingests CSVs, PDFs, or screenshots and lets the AI answer holdings-specific questions like which sectors are underweight, while natural-language scheduled briefings run in the background, with iOS arriving later this year.

An Interview with Figma CEO Dylan Field About Design and AI (1 minute read)

Stratechery published an interview with Figma CEO Dylan Field, whose company IPO'd in 2025 at a $56.3B valuation but has since fallen below $10B on a market narrative that Figma is an AI loser. Field argues the opposite, framing AI as a tailwind and pointing to Config, where he positioned Figma's canvas as the natural meeting point between design and AI workflows.

Notion shutting down its AI-powered email client, including Mac and iOS apps (1 minute read)

Notion is shutting down Notion Mail across web, desktop, and iOS on September 22, just over a year after launching the AI-powered email client in April 2025. The company says more than half of Mail users already manage email without opening their inbox, so it is redirecting effort toward agents that run the inbox rather than a standalone client.

Meta Relaunches Facebook Creator Studio as AI-Powered App (1 minute read)

Meta relaunched Facebook Creator Studio as a standalone AI-powered app that learns a creator's voice and audience on first open, surfaces daily priorities, and drafts high-priority comment replies for review. Meta is also splitting its Professional Dashboard into separate Creator and Business products and adding Facebook creators to Creator Marketplace, signaling a push to consolidate creator tooling against rivals competing for the same audience.

The AI era requires a different kind of experimentation. (1 minute read)

The author argues that the experimentation playbook most product teams built in 2022 is now obsolete, because AI has collapsed the build cycles that justified small, incremental A/B tests aimed at fast revenue wins. She makes the case for shifting toward bigger bets, testing foundational complexity and monetization rather than superficial surfaces, and accepting that some changes are now better shipped without a test at all.
