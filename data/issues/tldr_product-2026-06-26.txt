Notion Mail shuts down amid agent takeover (1 minute read)

Notion is shutting down Notion Mail on September 22, discontinuing the standalone inbox in favor of its AI agents after finding that more than half of Mail users now manage email without ever opening their inbox. The move retires a product Notion built on its 2024 Skiff acquisition to challenge Superhuman and Fyxer, betting instead that agents running the inbox is the more defensible position.

The company says more than half of Notion Mail users already manage email without opening their inbox, handing the workflow to Notion agents, so it is redirecting the effort toward agent-run inbox management instead.

The gap is a signal for PMs shipping generative-media features: text-based assistance has crossed into mainstream habit, while image and video generation remains a niche behavior most users have not yet adopted.

TBM 427: The Bottleneck Strike Again! (1 minute read)

The author argues that "engineering is no longer the bottleneck" is a flawed frame, because product development isn't a linear production line with one clogged pipe to clear. Work spans sensing, deciding, learning, aligning, and adapting, so constraints are distributed and constantly co-evolving rather than sitting in one movable spot. The practical takeaway for PMs: treat a visible bottleneck as a symptom to diagnose, not the problem to optimize away, and resist roadmap bets built on the idea that faster code-writing fixes the system.

Rethinking Figma in an AI world (3 minute read)

At Config 2026, Figma is expanding its canvas into code, motion, shaders, and agent-driven workflows, a direct response to AI pulling design work into code editors and agentic environments. The author argues the real stakes are Figma's seat-based business model: if engineers can generate and inspect UI inside a coding environment, the passive review-and-comment seats Figma sells to PMs, engineers, and execs lose their justification. For PMs, it reframes Figma less as the default design canvas and more as one bet among several on where product work will actually happen.

LLM-Based Discovery of Latent Requirements from Stakeholder Conversations: Preliminary Results from Industry (1 minute read)

This paper introduces LENS, an LLM approach that reads stakeholder interview transcripts to surface not just explicitly stated requirements but the latent ones buried in how people describe their workflows and pain points. Across twelve industrial cybersecurity interviews, it hit an 84.4% F1 on explicit requirements, and domain experts judged 75% of the inferred latent requirements as offering real automation or time-saving value. For PMs running discovery, the signal is that the gap between what stakeholders say and what they actually need is becoming machine-tractable, with transcript excerpts linked back to each user story for traceability.

How HappyFox Closed $1M in Expansion on a $20 AI Agent Spend with CEO Shalin Jain (1 minute read)

HappyFox CEO Shalin Jain detailed how a $20 AI agent named Rex generated $1M in closed expansion revenue by reading every support ticket at the moment it closes and flagging buying intent that reps see but never route and AEs never read. The takeaway for PMs running land-and-expand: the cheapest growth signal already lives in unstructured customer conversations nobody on the revenue side mines, and an agent that surfaces it at ticket-close turns reactive expansion into a proactive motion without adding headcount.
