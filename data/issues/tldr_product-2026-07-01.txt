Using Large Language Models as Low-Cost Statistical Estimators for Human-Response Data (1 minute read)

This paper proves that under squared loss, a well-calibrated LLM can match the Bayes-optimal risk for any inference that depends on data only through the conditional mean, making it a low-cost stand-in for human-subject experiments. The authors formalize the LLM as a misspecified estimator, decompose its error into representation bias and optimization error, and provide finite-sample bounds plus a calibration protocol with explicit decision rules. For PMs, it sets out the conditions under which synthetic user data can substitute for real research, and where it cannot.

39 principles for designing human-AI interaction (2 minute read)

The author distills research from human-AI interaction, mixed-initiative systems, and trust-in-automation into 39 applied principles for designing AI interfaces that support appropriate reliance, user control, and transparency. The core argument is that product quality isn't set by model capability alone: the same model can feel useful or dangerous depending on when the system suggests versus acts, how it surfaces uncertainty, and what evidence backs a generated answer. Newer principles pull directly from OpenAI's Model Spec and Anthropic's Claude Constitution on honesty and preserving human oversight.

Great Products, Bad Companies (1 minute read)

Marty Cagan argues that great products are necessary but not sufficient for great companies, because a proven product-making company becomes an attractive target for people who infiltrate boards, replace missionary founders, and strip-mine the culture for a fast financial win. He points to Eric Ries's new book Incorruptible as a reason to stop treating that fate as unavoidable, and to start designing governance that protects long-term value the way product teams protect customer trust.
