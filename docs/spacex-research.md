## Top picks (3 stories worth a LinkedIn post)

### 1. The 30-flight Falcon 9 booster (B1067)

- **Hook**: SpaceX designed Falcon 9 Block 5 to fly 10 times. Booster B1067 just flew its 32nd mission. Reusable hardware that flies past its accounting life is what changes an industry.
- **Source**: https://spaceflightnow.com/2025/08/27/live-coverage-record-breaking-30th-flight-for-spacex-falcon-9-booster-as-it-launches-starlink-satellites/
- **Concrete artifact**: B1067 has now flown two astronaut missions, two ISS cargo runs, and 26+ Starlink batches. Refurbishment cost: roughly $300K. Cost to build a new booster: ~$30M. Of 165 Falcon 9 flights in 2025, 157 used a previously flown booster.
- **Draft post (140 words)**:

> Falcon 9 Block 5 was designed to fly 10 times before retirement.
>
> Booster B1067 just flew its 32nd mission.
>
> Two astronaut launches. Two ISS cargo runs. 28 Starlink batches. One stage of metal, flown three times more often than the people who built it planned for.
>
> The economics that follow are what actually matter: refurbishing a recovered booster costs roughly $300,000. Building a new one costs $30 million. Of the 165 Falcon 9 flights SpaceX launched in 2025, 157 used a flown booster.
>
> Reusability has stopped being a feature. It is the default. Every competitor pricing against expendable hardware is pricing against a museum.
>
> The lesson for operators: the real win isn't hitting your spec. It's discovering your spec was the ceiling, not the asset, and then flying past it. Coverage from Spaceflight Now: https://spaceflightnow.com/2025/08/27/live-coverage-record-breaking-30th-flight-for-spacex-falcon-9-booster-as-it-launches-starlink-satellites/

### 2. 165 launches in one year - the cadence curve

- **Hook**: SpaceX launched orbital rockets 165 times in 2025. The rest of the world combined launched fewer. The interesting number is not 165 - it is the slope.
- **Source**: https://www.space.com/space-exploration/private-spaceflight/spacex-shatters-its-rocket-launch-record-yet-again-167-orbital-flights-in-2025
- **Concrete artifact**: 25 (2020) → 31 → 61 → 96 → 134 → 165 (2025). 75% Starlink. 98.2% landing success rate. 85% of all US orbital launches were SpaceX. Pad turnaround record: 2 days, 8 hours, 31 minutes.
- **Draft post (135 words)**:

> SpaceX launched 165 orbital rockets in 2025. More than the rest of the world combined.
>
> The headline number is impressive. The slope is the story:
>
> 25 launches in 2020. 31. 61. 96. 134. 165.
>
> Six straight years of compounding cadence. 98.2% landing success across the year. 85% of every American orbital launch was a SpaceX vehicle. The pad turnaround record at LC-39A dropped to 2 days, 8 hours, 31 minutes - a Falcon 9 launching from the same pad the previous one used, before the average commute home.
>
> Most companies treat cadence as an output. SpaceX treats it as the input - every other metric (cost per kg, reliability, customer leverage) is downstream of how often you fly.
>
> Full breakdown: https://www.space.com/space-exploration/private-spaceflight/spacex-shatters-its-rocket-launch-record-yet-again-167-orbital-flights-in-2025

### 3. The $15M acquisition that fixed a Starship failure mode

- **Hook**: When a part keeps failing on your rocket, you can keep filing supplier complaints. Or you can buy the supplier for $15M. SpaceX picked option two.
- **Source**: https://www.teslarati.com/spacex-strengthens-manufacturing-hexagon-purus-deal/
- **Concrete artifact**: $12.5M cash + up to $2.5M earn-out for Hexagon Masterworks, Hexagon Purus's aerospace division. They make Type 4 composite overwrapped pressure vessels (COPVs) - high-pressure gas storage cylinders implicated in multiple Starship prototype losses. Deal announced February 9, 2026.
- **Draft post (140 words)**:

> SpaceX kept losing Starship prototypes to the same failure mode: composite pressure vessels cracking under vibration.
>
> They could have written longer specs to their supplier. Filed quality complaints. Demanded design changes.
>
> Instead, in February 2026, they bought the supplier.
>
> $12.5 million in cash plus up to $2.5 million in earn-outs for Hexagon Purus's aerospace division - the people who make the Type 4 COPVs that store high-pressure helium and nitrogen on Starship. Total cost: less than the price of one Falcon 9 launch.
>
> What they actually bought wasn't a factory. It was the ability to change the design of a critical part on Monday and test it on Wednesday, without a vendor in the loop saying "next quarter."
>
> Vertical integration is not ideology. It is cycle time disguised as a balance sheet. Coverage: https://www.teslarati.com/spacex-strengthens-manufacturing-hexagon-purus-deal/

## Secondary candidates

- **Raptor 3 part-count reduction via 3D printing** - https://www.3dnatives.com/en/spacex-optimizes-raptor-3-dfam-3d-printing-120820244/ - SpaceX consolidated Raptor 2's external plumbing into the engine body using laser powder bed fusion. The result: ~30% fewer parts, no heat shield needed (the structure is the shield), and engines that no longer need decorative ductwork hanging off them. Companion piece to the existing "Algorithm" post - same idea, different artifact.

- **The McGregor test stand operates 18 hours a day** - https://www.nasaspaceflight.com/2025/09/spacex-starship-block-3-engine-testing-mcgregor/ - McGregor's seven test stands run 18 hours/day, six days/week, hot-firing roughly seven engines on a typical day. Most rocket programs hot-fire seven engines in a year. The interesting question is what your week looks like when your engine factory is also a torture chamber that fires every part it builds.

- **70 Starlink satellites built per week in Redmond** - https://www.geekwire.com/2026/spacex-is-churning-out-70-starlink-satellites-a-week-in-redmond-and-other-tidbits-from-its-ipo-filing/ - Disclosed in the SpaceX S-1. Approx. 3,640 satellites per year out of one factory. V3 line targeting 5,000-10,000/year on top of that. Production cadence as a structural moat: by the time a competitor builds one constellation, SpaceX has shipped another.

- **The S-1: Starlink is 61% of SpaceX revenue and the only GAAP-profitable line** - https://www.satellitetoday.com/finance/2026/05/20/spacexs-ipo-filing-gives-first-look-into-companys-financials/ - $11.4B in Starlink revenue in 2025, $4.4B operating profit. The launch business is, in accounting terms, the marketing budget for the connectivity business. A reframe most operators don't see coming: launch wasn't the product, it was the distribution channel for the product.

- **Hans Koenigsmann on SpaceX's "no process" early days** - https://www.worldofaerospace.com/interview-with-hans-koenigsmann-from-spacexs-first-engineer-to-shaping-the-future-of-commercial-spaceflight - SpaceX's fourth technical hire on how the early company had no processes - engineers decided how to do things and codified rules only after failures forced them. A counterpoint to the cargo-cult of process design: rules are an output of iteration, not a prerequisite.

- **Pad turnaround record: 2 days, 8 hours, 31 minutes** - https://spaceflightnow.com/2025/06/27/live-coverage-spacex-to-launch-27-starlink-satellites-on-falcon-9-rocket-from-cape-canaveral-4/ - Two launches off the same pad in under 57 hours. Inspect, repair, refuel, restack, launch - in the time most teams ship a sprint.

- **Single-digit booster turnaround** - https://spaceexplored.com/2025/03/24/spacex-hits-single-digit-booster-turnaround-times/ - Booster B1088 flew again 9 days after its previous mission, breaking the prior 14-day record. The interesting subtext: every gain comes from deleting refurbishment steps, not from doing them faster.
